Hungary's Viktor Orban urges supporters to stand up against PM Magyar's 'tyranny'
BUDAPEST, July 22 - Hungary's former nationalist Prime Minister Viktor Orban, who was ousted after 16 years in power in an election in April, has urged his supporters to restore democracy in the country which he said had been crushed by the new government.
Orban made his first strong political statements since his landslide election defeat late on Tuesday on Facebook, issuing a "Statement of Resistance" shortly after his Fidesz party said prosecutors had raided offices housing its internet servers.
Prosecutors told Reuters in an emailed response that the action was linked to an investigation into "misappropriation and other criminal offences" at the National Culture Fund (NKA) under Orban's government.
Orban said that Hungary had "ceased to be a democratic state" after the new centre-right government of Prime Minister Peter Magyar passed a constitutional amendment ending the mandate of the president and the head of the constitutional court.
Tisza denies taking an authoritarian stance towards political opponents. It has pledged to curb corruption, which it says was rampant under Orban's administration, part of Tisza's drive to bring Hungary back into the EU mainstream.
Orban himself faced heavy criticism during his rule over democratic standards in Hungary, which was in frequent conflict with the EU over issues such as the rule of law and minority rights.
Orban has denied the corruption allegations.
"Again the age of tyranny started in Hungary," Orban said.
"The new political system created this way is illegitimate...Fidesz will use all peaceful tools against abuse of power inside parliament and outside parliament."
He will hold a press briefing at 0830 GMT.
Magyar's Tisza party has a supermajority in parliament, which enables it to roll back changes introduced by Orban that Magyar says harmed democracy.
The constitutional amendment ended President Tamas Sulyok's term, citing society's "serious loss of confidence" in a leader elected in 2024 by lawmakers from Orban's Fidesz party. Parliament will soon elect a new president.
Magyar's Tisza commands huge public support, with a poll by pollster Median earlier this month showing 60% support, against 18% for Fidesz. Since the election, Fidesz has been in disarray, with its parliamentary group leader resigning last week. REUTERS

Jo Whiley shares heartbreaking tribute after death of ‘lovely’ father
The 61-year-old broadcaster previously revealed on her 'Dig It' podcast that her father had been diagnosed with Parkinson’s disease
- Bookmark
- CommentsGo to comments
BBC Radio 2 presenter Jo Whiley has received an outpouring of support following the death of her "lovely" father, whom she described as "the kindest man".
The 61-year-old broadcaster had previously revealed on her 'Dig It' podcast that her father, Martin, had been diagnosed with Parkinson’s disease, a condition she found "heartbreaking".
According to the NHS, Parkinson's is a progressive neurological condition where parts of the brain become damaged over many years, affecting movement, as well as bodily and mental functions.
Whiley announced her father’s passing on Instagram on Tuesday night, sharing a series of photos and videos of them together throughout the years.
She wrote: “My lovely Dad died today. He was the kindest man, who lived solely to make others happy.
“He was supremely selfless and loved nothing more than caring for others and making people laugh.”
The broadcaster said she has received “so many messages today” praising her father and describing him as “a character” – which she said “sums him up completely”.
Whiley added: “I have Dad to thank for my love of music – he used to walk around the building site where he worked as an electrician with a huge ghetto blaster – the OG DJ and his passion for music never went away.
“His love for Elbow, Coldplay and Dido was unrivalled.”
The TV and radio star, who was born in Northampton, recounted how her father’s childhood was “as sad as it gets”, saying he was “sent alone to a sanatorium in London for two years” after being diagnosed with tuberculosis.
Whiley added: “(This) is why we’re all Spurs fans!”
The broadcaster also explained that her father had “no parents” from a young age, and was raised by a family friend, adding: “Meeting my mum meant that dad got a family of his own and finally got to experience love.
“Knowing how it felt not to be cared for, he devoted his life to caring for others.”
Whiley, whose younger sister Frances has cri du chat syndrome, a genetic disorder that can cause significant learning disabilities, wrote: “Being the father of someone with learning disabilities (LD) in the 70s meant that life has had its challenges.
“But Dad has been such a force for good in the LD world simply by embracing and loving everyone with a difference.
“He may have had the saddest childhood but he triumphed by giving me, Frances, and his grandchildren the happiest childhoods anyone could have dreamt of.”
“He was the greatest Dad and he was everybody’s favourite Grandad, but especially to India, Jude, Cass and Coco who adored him,” she added, referring to her four children.
The DJ also wrote a message from her sister at the end of the post, in which she described their father as a “legend” who the family will “miss so very much”.
Friends fans and fellow stars expressed their condolences, with award-winning screenwriter and TV producer Russell T Davies, best known for reviving Doctor Who, writing: “The OG DJ. So sorry, love to you and the family. Take care.”
“Oh Jo – so so sorry for your loss – sending love,” broadcaster Kate Garraway wrote.
Celebrity chef Jamie Oliver said: “Bless you Jo I’m so sorry for the loss of your father, sending loads of love,” while image consultant and DJ Gok Wan, singer-songwriter KT Tunstall, actor Richard E Grant, TV hosts Gaby Roslin and Vanessa Feltz, and retired radio presenter Bob Harris also sent messages of support.
BBC Radio 2, where Whiley has worked for 15 years after leaving Radio 1, said: “Sending you and your family lots of love Jo.”

Do home alarm systems have smart home control?
What to know about controlling your home security system remotely
- Bookmark
Our smartphones play a role in almost every aspect of our lives, from communication and research, to banking, transportation and life admin – so of course, they also now help control our homes, too.
Many smart home devices and systems rely on a smartphone app for control. From simple smart plugs, fans and heaters, to more advanced devices like lighting, air conditioning, robot vacuum cleaners, car chargers and security systems.
The latter sees apps used to monitor security cameras, control smart door locks, receive notifications when alarms are triggered, and arm or disarm home alarm and security systems.
In some cases, the smartphone app acts as a central hub for controlling a home alarm – and some door locks even work with just a tap of your smartphone – while for other systems the app acts more like a useful companion.
For many home alarm customers, this will be the sweet spot – and that’s why Verisure has taken this approach with its own smartphone app. Instead of having to open an app for every system interaction, and receive dozens of notifications throughout the day, it’s there to adjust settings, create routines, contact customer support and remotely control the system.
This is where a home alarm app makes sense for many users. They can arm and disarm the system with a tap of their key fob most of the time, but open up the app if they need to operate the door lock remotely – handy if your partner locks themselves out while you’re away from home, or you need to let someone in, like a cleaner or dog walker.
How to set your home alarm system remotely with smart home control
Protected by biometric security, like FaceID, with compatible phones, the My Verisure app shows what mode your system is in, and the status of every door lock at a glance. It also shows a status for the entire system, and flags up any issues with each device, such as warnings for low battery and poor connectivity.
Plus, it’s possible to view your cameras from the app, either by streaming live or by requesting a photograph from each camera. Verisure uses Arlo cameras for outdoor surveillance. So while these cameras also appear in the My Verisure app, tapping on them takes you to the Arlo app, which handles settings and live streaming.
An activity history page shows precisely when your Verisure system was interacted with, and by whom. It shows when the system was armed and who did it (or rather, which key fob was used, and who in your household that fob is assigned to).
Lastly, the app can be used for adjusting settings, updating your contacts details and code words, checking how to replace dead batteries, and contacting customer support, which is handled through WhatsApp or a phone call.
So, while some home alarm systems rely more heavily on their smartphone app – alerting you every time a door or window is opened if you so wish, and tapping into broader smart home platforms – the My Verisure app focuses on a simpler, calmer experience.
The My Verisure app isn’t designed to replace the keypad, fobs or professional monitoring service. Instead, it complements them and gives customers an easy way to check the system status, change settings and control their alarm, cameras and door lock remotely, while the day-to-day experience remains centred on dedicated hardware.
