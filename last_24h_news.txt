China missed the World Cup. Its brands didn’t
From broadcast operations to sponsorships, Chinese companies hold key roles at football’s biggest event despite geopolitical friction
Inside a sprawling broadcast hub in Dallas, thousands of devices supplied by Lenovo are helping Fifa manage and distribute content from stadiums across the United States, Canada and Mexico, forming part of the technological backbone of the largest World Cup ever staged.
The central role played by the Chinese-founded technology company highlights a reality often obscured by geopolitical tensions between Washington and Beijing: while US policymakers have increasingly scrutinised Chinese technology firms and sought to reduce security risks in strategically sensitive sectors, Chinese companies remain deeply embedded in global commercial ecosystems, including one of the highest-profile international events ever hosted in North America.
China’s men’s national team once again failed to qualify for the World Cup, having reached the tournament only once, in 2002.
The stakes are enormous. Fifa is expected to generate between US$2.5 billion and US$3 billion in sponsorship revenue from the 2026 World Cup, making it one of the most commercially valuable sporting events ever staged and a coveted platform for companies seeking global visibility.
“It’s the coming out party for Chinese global brands,” said Craig Allen, former US ambassador and senior fellow at the Asia Society.

Nenni Ekki: Working Hard Or Hardly Working
Unknown
Unknown Publisher
2026-06-12
21:11:21 UTC
06:11:21 KST

To some, the 1st of May is synonymous with International Worker’s Day, honouring the endless and slow-moving crawl toward labour rights for all workers. To others, the 1st of May is May Day, in which people get drunk and look at flowers in the modern-day remnant of some horny pagan festival. To most, however, it’s just another bank holiday. This one, however, is especially ironic because the people who actually get to fuck off for a day are mostly office workers who have probably never done any actual labour in their lives. Meanwhile, the folks in food service, hospitality, and pretty much any kind of shift work (cough, cough, us foreigners) are the only ones left labouring on Labour Day.
This doesn’t really make sense, but somehow, it makes a little more sense in Iceland. “Work” is kind of a different concept here, if not a foreign one entirely. To say that the highest standard here is half-assed would be giving a quarter of an ass too much credit. To “nenni ekki,” or not be bothered to do something, is a time-honoured tradition enshrined even in Icelandic literature. There is an archetypal character that dates back to the sagas and probably even before, back when Icelanders were still just middle-aged Norwegian manchildren that hadn’t moved out of their parents’ house yet. This figure is essentially The Lazy One, known as a “coal biter” due to their penchant for loafing around the fireplace back in those times. This figure became an especially beloved hero in Icelandic folklore and the tales proliferate with these good-for-nothing shitheads, so I’m gonna give you a couple of key examples.
These coal biters usually go on to prove their disappointed parents wrong by finding success. One would expect this happens by learning the value of working hard and proving themselves worthy, therefore giving these folktales some kind of moral backbone upon which to sit up. But don’t forget, this is Iceland. Instead, these coal biters become heroes not through diligence but rather opportunism. They lie, cheat, and steal, maliciously manipulating their circumstances to achieve success without actually working hard. It’s a bit like the adage “work smarter, not harder,” only it’s missing both “smarter” and “harder.” Iceland’s adage would be more like, “work… not…” Or in the local language: “nenni ekki.”
The tale of the peasant’s son and the King’s Herdsman
So of course there is a peasant couple, and of course their only son is a lazy piece of shit. His only job is to watch the family cow, but one day he lets it wander off. His parents are so angry that they throw him out. He wanders until he meets some guy and asks the guy to take him in. The man agrees, but says that if he stays the night, he’ll have to work tomorrow. “And you should know that I am the King’s Herdsman,” the smug bastard adds, as if that makes him important. The next day, the herdsman tasks the boy to herd 100 pigs. He takes the job, but he gets sick of the pigs pretty quickly, so he leads them to his parents’ house.
He admits that he stole these pigs, so his dad may as well kill them all and make use of them. Because that makes total sense. The father eventually agrees and slaughters them all. The boy then takes their tails, ties them together, throws them in a swamp, and weighs them down with a stone. He returns to the herdsman and says, “Man, you will not believe what just happened.” Of course the man doesn’t believe that the pigs just ran into the swamp and got stuck, so he goes to check. He is unable to pull them out and buys the lie.
So this guy gives the boy another task and guess what happens? Yeah, the same damn thing. The boy steals 100 rams and convinces the man they flew away in the wind by hiding their bells in a tree. He then steals 40 oxen, driving one into a chasm, lighting it on fire, and telling the man they all fell straight down into hell. The boy is next tasked with making farm equipment and since he can’t lie his way out of this one, he sneaks back to his parents’ house and behaves for a while.
Then one day, he decides he wants to marry the herdsman’s daughter, so he shows back up at his door. He pulls a sword on the man, saying that he’ll kill him on his doorstep if he doesn’t allow him to marry his daughter. For some reason, the man relents and they marry. The boy later tells the whole story to the herdsman, who tells it to the king. The king then makes the boy his minister and everyone lives happily ever after. Good job, coal biter! You done your parents real proud.
The tale of the peasant’s daughters
Iceland likes to think of itself as the most feminist country in the world. As women here have indeed fought long and hard for the right to be as lazy as their male counterparts, I would be remiss not to include a story highlighting the craftiness of some coal-biting girlbosses. So don’t worry, I’ve got you. This story starts like they all do. Some peasant couple have some shitty, lazy daughters who don’t help out enough around the house. The girls get really tired of their tedious parents always harping on them, so they decide to just straight up murder them. Then they are free to eat all the food and do none of the chores. Eventually, however, they run out of food. So they decide to steal the king’s favourite ox and eat it.
The king sends his minister to search for it. He comes to the girls’ house, and they invite him in. When he turns to leave, he finds a snowstorm outside. The sisters insist that he sleep with the youngest sister. He obviously agrees, but only because he fears desperately for his life in the storm. That’s definitely the only reason, so don’t ask him again! The next morning, the women are all gone. He ventures out and finds a boat in the river. As he gets into it, he suddenly realises that he is actually scrabbling about in the trash bin. All of the women surround him, threatening to kill him if he doesn’t marry the youngest sister. He agrees and returns to spill the tea at the castle.
Next, the king sends his son, and he falls into the exact same trap. Then the king decides to go himself because obviously it wouldn’t happen to his highness. But it does and the next morning, he finds a lake outside the house. He takes up a branch to help himself wade out into the water until the illusion melts away. He finds himself standing in a tub of whey holding a butter-churn and all the sisters cackling maniacally at him. They threaten to drown him if he doesn’t marry the oldest sister. He agrees and returns to the castle where the three men finally realise they’ve all been beguiled by witchcraft. Men, amirite?
It’s totally fine though. Nothing to worry about. The minister, the prince, and the king just marry the three sisters and everybody lives happily ever after… except the parents. I would actually argue that three broke bitches swindling rich men out of their money is totally worth celebrating if not for that whole murdering-their-own-parents-for-no-reason thing. I’m one to resist hustle-culture and see relaxation as a radical act in a capitalist world, but I’m afraid that even I have to draw a line there. You can’t tell your mom, “I told you so” if she’s dead! ￼
