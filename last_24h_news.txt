How will Khalil al-Hayya's past shape Hamas' future? - analysis
Unknown
Unknown Publisher
2026-07-20
19:13:06 UTC
04:13:06 KST

In September 2025, Khalil al-Hayya was in Doha, Qatar, when an Israeli airstrike was carried out.
“Senior Hamas official Khalil al-Hayya [was] targeted with others in an Israeli operation in Doha on Tuesday,” UAE daily The National noted at the time. It also said he had been leading the group’s negotiations.
“Hamas’s six-man leadership [was] targeted in Doha by Israel,” the report said. Now, reports say that he has been named the head of Hamas’s political bureau, essentially replacing former Hamas leader Yahya Sinwar.
Hayya is expected to play a crucial role going forward. Notably, he is not in Gaza. This means that Hamas operatives in Gaza, who have remained in the shadows thus far, will continue to do so, benefiting from an opaque leadership style.
Hamas has claimed that it is ready to transfer the governance of Gaza to another party. However, with its leaders living abroad, Hamas can claim it has given over control and still maintain power behind the scenes. Hayya, thus, will be key to its plans.
From Hamas founder to political bureau chief
Hayya is a member of the original Hamas generation. Born in 1960 in Gaza, he grew up with other leaders of Hamas, who also lived in Gaza. The group formed in the 1980s, inspired by the Muslim Brotherhood in Egypt. He joined the group when it was founded during the First Intifada in 1987.
Hayya was detained by Israel in the 1990s and served three years in prison. He left Gaza before the October 7 massacre and ended up in Doha alongside other Hamas heavyweights.
Before October 7, Hayya was already well-known to both Hamas and Israel. For instance, in November 2023, Hayya was running a communications office for Hamas and was Sinwar’s deputy.
“Al-Hayya’s past includes a three-year stint in an Israeli prison, and he has survived multiple attempts on his life. These assassination attempts led to the death of 19 of his family members. Currently, al-Hayya resides in Qatar,” Ynet said at the time.
In the past, Hayya appeared at semi-public events in Qatar. For instance, in August 2024, he appeared at a memorial for assassinated Hamas chief Ismail Haniyeh in Doha.
Arab newspaper Asharq al-Awsat noted, “Hayya has been widely seen as Hamas’s most influential figure abroad since Haniyeh’s death. He is part of a five-man leadership council that has led Hamas since Yahya Sinwar’s death. Hailing from the Gaza Strip, he has lost several close relatives, including his eldest son, to Israeli strikes.”
Hayya alleged that Israel has been trying to assassinate him since 2007, and Palestinian media has repeated this claim. Back in 2007, he claimed that Israel struck his home, “killing seven of his children and family members,” the Palestine Information Center claimed.
“In 2014, Israel tried once again to assassinate him, failing, but spilling more of his family’s blood,” the report said. It is, crucially, impossible to verify this.
One thing is startlingly clear, however: Hayya has made his personal losses an integral part of his identity. Hayya’s son was killed in the 2014 war. In 2025, TRT alleged that, “in August, Israeli media reported that two of al-Hayya’s nephews, Ayman Abd al Salam al-Hayya and Mu’az Abd al Salam al-Hayya, were killed in Gaza while cutting firewood in Gaza City’s Shejaiya neighborhood.”
Hayya's role in Hamas negotiations and leadership
Hayya’s narrative has been that he will continue the course set by Haniyeh and Sinwar. This would entail endless war, as well as Hamas’s continuous attempts to rebuild itself.
A 2025 article at The Dispatch noted: “Al-Hayya has long held roles in Hamas’s leadership. He served as the group’s lead negotiator in the talks that followed the wars Hamas fought in 2012 and 2014 against Israel. He was the leader of Hamas’ parliamentary bloc in the Palestinian Legislative Council from 2006. In 2017, Yahya Sinwar, then the leader of Hamas in the Gaza Strip, appointed al-Hayya as his deputy, and Hamas’ political bureau reelected al-Hayya for that role in 2021.”
The Dispatch goes on to note that Hayya was involved in contacts with the Assad regime in 2022, which attempted to reconnect Hamas and Assad. The group left Syria in 2012.
“Interestingly, al-Hayya’s role as hostage deal negotiator for Hamas is not a new one for him,” The Dispatch’s report added. “He participated in the hostage negotiations after Hamas kidnapped Israeli soldier Gilad Shalit in 2006, and Israel subsequently traded 1,000 Palestinian prisoners for his release in 2011. Al-Hayya was hardly the only Hamas leader to say it, but he proclaimed that the Shalit deal proved that kidnapping works, and he promised more abductions.”
Hayya is an educated man, having received a doctorate in Sudan. He was involved in academic research for several years in the late 1990s, and ran in Palestinian elections in 2006.
Hayya continues to support the October 7 massacre. In a 2025 interview shown on CGTN Europe, it was noted that “senior Hamas leader Khalil al-Hayya lauded the October 7 attack on Israel as a significant achievement, claiming it would be remembered with pride by future generations of Palestinians.” He also angered Egypt and Jordan by calling for their citizens to march toward Gaza.
What Hayya's appointment could mean for Hamas
For many years, Hayya was widely seen as a candidate for top leadership. He was already a member of the triumvirate of leadership that emerged after Sinwar’s death. He also worked alongside Hamas Shura Council chairman Mohammed Darwish, Zaher Jabarin, and Khaled Meshaal.
Considering his background, it is unlikely that Hayya will bring change to Hamas. On the contrary, it seems that he will continue on the same path as its previous leadership.

Maine Democrats have found Graham Platner’s replacement. Can he win control of the Senate seat?
Graham Platner’s self-destruction was a major blow to progressives, but his replacement proves that their critics don’t have much juice, writes John Bowden
- Bookmark
- CommentsGo to comments
After the implosion of Graham Platner, many Maine Democrats feared they were lost in the wilderness.
On Monday, it was clear: Troy Jackson is the man they believe will lead them out.
Jackson ended the weekend with the committed support of roughly two-thirds of the delegates in the state who are set to gather this coming weekend and formally nominate a replacement for Platner, whose Senate campaign ended after his support dissolved in the wake of a sexual assault allegation from an ex-girlfriend, which he denied. Three of his rivals for the nomination have already dropped out of the race.
Barring a major shake-up, he’s poised to walk into that convention as the overwhelming favorite.
Now, he has to win the general election against incumbent and Republican Susan Collins - and if he does it could help flip control of the entire Senate to Democrats.
Jackson’s apparent victory was cheered on by members of the left-wing faction that has challenged numerous Democratic incumbents this cycle and sought to push the party towards a more aggressive populist, working-class centric platform: The Democratic Socialists of America. The DSA, which counts new Democratic primary victors Melat Kiros and Darializa Avila Chevalier among its members, endorsed Jackson as he ran for governor this year in a ranked-choice Democratic primary.
Jackson was defeated in that race, but with the rumors around Platner already swirling by the June primary, one of his supporters presciently suggested on Twitter: “I now want Troy Jackson to not win the governor race and finish 2nd so he’ll most likely be the backup Senate nominee if Platner drops out.”
A DSA-endorsed progressive and former state director for Bernie Sanders’s presidential campaign in 2016, Jackson’s victory represents a major coup for a progressive left wing that took a rhetorical battering in the press after Platner’s withdrawal.
Accusations of poor vetting and an insufficient focus on the voices of women in the party were leveled at not just Platner but his senior advisers and wider campaign staff, many of whom rallied behind Jackson after the collapse of the Platner bid.
Many of those same progressives were adamant, in the face of that criticism, that Maine voters had spoken clearly and chosen a progressive over the party establishment’s hand-picked candidate, incumbent Maine Gov. Janet Mills, to represent the party in November against Collins.
Many of Platner’s supporters argued that winning the seat in November would be an impossible and fruitless goal should national Democratic Party figures be allowed to select a candidate to run in his stead.
Barring something major happening, Jackson has seemingly lined up overwhelming support within the state part for his bid. His campaign is now well-positioned to scoop up the existing infrastructure already in place powering Platner’s impressive victory over Mills last month. The question is: Can he recapture the same voter enthusiasm that accompanied Platner’s win?
On paper, Jackson checks all the boxes. As his endorsements (which also included Bernie Sanders in his gubernatorial bid, and the Maine AFL-CIO) would suggest, Jackson is a dyed-in-the-wool progressive, supporting Medicare for All, the abolition of Immigration and Customs Enforcement and an end to American support of Israel’s assault on Gaza, which he’s referred to as a genocide.
Like Platner, he also hails from a working-class background, having previously worked as a logger before joining Maine’s state senate, and later serving as its majority leader.
Whether Jackson can translate his similarities into the same momentum against Collins is an open question, though, as he came in third place in the Maine gubernatorial primary this year despite his list of powerful endorsements. Supporters argued that he built an impressive ground game and fared well against politicians with better name recognition, but some are still skeptical about his path forward.
Some fans of Platner and other Democratic observers questioned whether Jackson had the same charisma that led Platner to pack town hall events to capacity for months ahead of his primary victory in June.
Collins, meanwhile, has nearly faded into the background amid her onetime opponent’s self-destruction.
Much as the Trump campaign did in 2024, Collins’s campaign has largely stayed out of the fray as Democrats rushed to replace Platner — the incumbent senator, 73, is taking a more passive approach to campaigning as she holds on to advantages in name recognition, her reputation as a moderate in the Senate and a massive war chest supported by Senate GOP leadership which is waiting to flood the airwaves with more than $100 million in reserved advertising space through November.
The reality for Jackson is stark: His quick collection of the delegates necessary to win the Senate nomination was only the first battle he’ll face, with a much tougher one coming down the pipes.
He’s stepping into a race where the comparisons to Kamala Harris’s doomed 2024 bid are widely apparent, banking on the notion that a unified Democratic Party and dissatisfaction for the Trump administration and the Republican Congress will be enough to take a jump-started campaign over the finish line against potentially the hardest target for Democrats to beat of this entire cycle.
