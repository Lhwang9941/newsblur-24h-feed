A Moscow court has sentenced rapper Alisher Morgenshtern in absentia to a seven-million-ruble fine on criminal charges of failing to comply with his obligations as a designated foreign agent, the independent Russian news outlet Mediazona reported.
The charges stemmed from posts Morgenshtern published on Telegram, Instagram, and YouTube between March and June 2024 without the required foreign agent disclaimer. Prosecutors had sought a fine of 8,206,000 rubles.
Defense attorney Ildar Islamov argued in closing statements that the evidence was insufficient to establish Morgenshtern’s guilt and that the criminal case had been opened over posts that had already been deleted.
Before Russia’s full-scale invasion of Ukraine, Morgenshtern repeatedly expressed support for Vladimir Putin’s policies, but changed his position after the invasion began and left Russia. In May 2022, Russian authorities designated him a foreign agent. In 2024, criminal proceedings were opened against him for failing to fulfill his foreign agent obligations, and he was placed on a wanted list in Russia. His Moscow property has been seized.

Why Australia's big gas producers haven't jacked up domestic prices unlike during Ukraine war
It's a miracle!
After a decade and a half of mostly failed threats from three prime ministers and constant surveillance by the competition regulator, the east coast gas export giants are suddenly putting Australians first.
While soaring gas prices are crushing European and Asian countries, Australians are being sheltered from the storm.
It is a situation that, on past experience at least, defies all logic.
Qatar, one of the world's biggest suppliers of Liquified Natural Gas (LNG), has shut gas production after Iranian drone attacks inflicted serious damage on its massive production and storage facility in the Persian Gulf.
On top of that, more than 20 per cent of the world's supply of LNG has been locked in the Gulf for more than six weeks, unable to pass through the Strait of Hormuz, creating global energy shortfalls.
Four years ago, a similar situation — Russia's invasion of Ukraine — saw Australian east coast domestic gas flows directed offshore, to capitalise on windfall profits.
It was a move that fuelled inflation, pushed interest rates higher than was otherwise necessary and sent corporations packing. But this time, we've been spared.
While most Australians have understandably focused on the incredible spike in the cost of petrol and diesel, gas has the potential to inflict greater economic damage.
Soaring gas prices were among the biggest contributors to the post-pandemic inflation wave that swept across Australia, helping send electricity costs into orbit.
That's because gas is the swing factor in power generation. It kicks in when coal generation and renewables cannot meet demand. So, the price of gas, determines the cost of electricity.
What's different this time?
While the three east coast gas giants have almost total market control, they've been spooked by a change of community attitude, the prospect of much higher taxes and yet to be formulated new laws that require them to prioritise domestic customers.
That has them walking on eggshells.
According to the competition watchdog, the three consortia led by Origin, Shell and Santos dominate almost the entire eastern market, which they used to great effect during the early days of the Ukraine crisis.
"As the LNG exporters and their associates influence almost 90 per cent of the proven and probable east coast gas reserves, much higher international prices have increased Australia's domestic wholesale gas prices," the ACCC noted.
For two years, up until 2023, the ACCC noted, the exporters withdrew more gas from the domestic market than they supplied, which they then shipped offshore.
While West Australian gas exporters for more than two decades have been forced to ensure domestic users had enough supply, there was no such requirement on the east coast.
The main culprit was the Santos-led consortium, which wrote export contracts for more gas than it could extract, leaving it perennially short.
Such was the outcry from manufacturers and other industrial users, the Albanese government was forced to step in after its previous intervention along with those of the Turnbull and Morrison governments failed.
Late last year, it was decided to introduce a gas reservation policy to ensure Australians have access to their own gas supplies.
While the principle has been accepted, the design has yet to be finalised and that's left the exporters in a heightened state of fear.
It would now appear the export giants are going to great lengths to avoid a repeat of the price gouging they enjoyed three years ago.
This graph from the Australian Energy Regulator shows average gas prices until the start of this year and the sharp decline from the 2022 spike.
The reservation policy isn't the only thing striking fear into the hearts of gas export executives.
There is also the prospect of a windfall profits tax in the upcoming May budget, which will be explored this week by a Senate Select Committee.
Miraculously, despite all the international energy market turmoil, gas prices have fallen even further, from around $12 a gigajoule at the beginning of the year to around $10.40 now.
That's half the level of the Ukraine crisis peak.
While welcome, it's difficult not to view the recent price action as a reinforcement of the extent of the export giants' market dominance and their ability to influence price.
European gas prices, meanwhile, are now 30 per cent higher than pre-Iran war and, at one stage, had more than doubled.
Companies closed, jobs lost
There's a long list of casualties from the great east coast gas gouge.
Plastics and glass producers were among the host of power-intensive industries that either have gone to the wall or begged for taxpayer help.
But perhaps the most tragic was the urea plant on Gibson Island near Brisbane. The main ingredient for producing nitrogen-rich urea — an essential in agriculture — is natural gas.
With a ready-made domestic market, given our status as one of the world's biggest rural goods producers and exporters in addition to being the third biggest exporter of LNG, urea production on paper would appear a no-brainer.
Incitec Pivot, however, was forced to shut the plant in 2021 as LNG prices soared, leaving Australian farmers at the mercy of imports.
"Despite extensive efforts, [we have] been unable to secure an economically viable long-term gas supply," the company told the ASX.
About 60 per cent of our urea supplies are now sourced from the Middle East and, along with a huge cache of the world's oil and gas supplies, have been trapped for more than a month.
The prime minister over the weekend secured 250,000 tonnes of urea from Indonesia, about 20 per cent of our needs for this season as planting for winter crops is underway. The company distributing that supply is, coincidentally, Incitec Pivot.
Sovereignty vs sovereign risk
The war in Iran and the trend towards rising geopolitical instability is rewriting the trade textbook.
Globalisation is being wound back and, everywhere, nations are looking for greater self-reliance or, at the very least, reliable partners.
But that will involve greater government involvement in the global economy and greater public expenditure, winding back the dominant theme since the 1980s of minimal government involvement.
LoadingThe federal and West Australian governments have tipped in almost $600 million for a new domestic urea processing plant in the Pilbara in Western Australia.
The plant is expected to open in 2027 and will be the nation's largest processing site, capable of producing an estimated 2.3 million tonnes of the fertiliser each year.
Such moves will require greater tax-raising measures, particularly since the huge public spending programs undertaken during the pandemic and the global financial crisis.
Resource companies, in particular, are likely to come under increasing pressure to pay their way.
There has long been criticism of Australia's poorly designed Petroleum Resource Rent Tax which extracts minimal amounts of tax. In 2023/24 it raised just $1.1 billion, around the same as a decade earlier when the gas industry was just kicking into gear.
Gas exporters are gearing up for a huge anti-new tax campaign, arguing any measures will raise sovereign risk and simply force operators offshore.
But where exactly would they go? Doha, anyone?
To borrow a line from Crocodile Dundee: "You call this sovereign risk?"

The newest influencer health hack? Nicotine
The influencers claim that products such as patches, gums and pouches utilize the ‘natural’ product and that it has been unfairly condemned by the medical establishment
- Bookmark
- CommentsGo to comments
Multiple influencers who are supporters of Robert F Kennedy Jr’s Make America Healthy Again movement are pushing a new and somewhat surprising health hack to their followers – nicotine.
The influencers claim that products such as patches, gums and pouches utilize the “natural” product and that it has been unfairly condemned by the medical establishment,
Nicotine pouches entered the U.S. market in 2016, and scientists are still learning about the short and long-term effects of the products, according to the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention.
“There are no safe tobacco products, including nicotine pouches. This is particularly true for youth, young adults, and women who are pregnant,” the CDC website states.
“Youth, young adults, and women who are pregnant should not use nicotine pouches. People who do not currently use tobacco products, including nicotine pouches, should not start.”
The center notes that nicotine can harm brain development, which continues up until the age of 25, as well as increasing the risk for young people of future addiction to other drugs. Symptoms of addiction can start “quickly” even if the person has not used nicotine products previously.
“Nicotine can also increase blood pressure and heart rate, which could, over time, raise the risk of heart disease; the compound may also harden the walls of arteries in the heart, which can lead to heart attacks. Nicotine can also exacerbate existing heart conditions, according to the CDC.
This has not deterred the MAHA influencers, who argue that nicotine has been vilified in a similar way to peptides, raw milk and beef tallow – which has been promoted by Kennedy. The U.S. Health Secretary himself has been pictured carrying around a tin of nicotine pouches, and has said previously that such pouches are “probably” the safest way to consume nicotine.
The Independent has contacted the Department of Health and Human Services for comment.
Dave Asprey, a “biohacking influencer” who boasts over 1.3 million followers on social media, has claimed nicotine can increase cognitive function, reverse Alzheimer’s disease and help you live longer, according to The New York Times.
Celebrity fitness guru and former trainer on TV show The Biggest Loser, Jillian Michaels, has pushed the benefits of nicotine on her podcast – including claiming she uses low doses of the substance to protect against Parkinson’s and combat her own ADHD.
“Nicotine, unto itself, is not toxic. It’s beneficial,” Michaels said, though he acknowledged that people should consult doctors before using it.
Even former Fox News show host Tucker Carlson, who is a vocal advocate of the MAHA movement, has promoted nicotine as a positive product, releasing his own brand of nicotine pouches named ALP. Carlson has previously referred to ZYN-branded pouches as “lifesaving.”
Medical experts are united in their condemnation of such promotions.
“If there really was a health benefit for nicotine, then the medical community would be recommending it to their patients,” Doctor Adam Leventhal, director of the Institute for Addiction Science at the University of Southern California, told The New York Times.
“And what’s happening is the opposite.”
