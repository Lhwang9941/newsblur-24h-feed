UK: Two trains collide north of London, injuries reported
June 19, 2026British Transport Police announced on Friday they were responding to reports of a train collision north of London.
"We're responding to reports of a collision involving two trains in the Bedford area," the transport police force wrote on X, referring to a market town around 56 miles (90 kilometres) north of London.
Other emergency services also confirmed they were responding to the incident.
The East of England Ambulance Service said it sent multiple resources, including an air ambulance, to respond to a collision on the railway south of Bedford. The service urged people to avoid the area.
What do we know about the collision?
Unverified footage posted to social media appeared to show two East Midlands Railway (EMR) trains colliding, with one running into the other.
According to the images posted, the trains appeared to have remained upright on the track.
EMR posted on X that "emergency services are dealing with an incident between London St Pancras and Leicester," but did not provide further details.
Some British media outlets reported that passengers were injured in the incident. However, the emergency services have not yet provided any details about the extent of the injuries.

Two websites restrict reviews of JD Vance’s book after savage critiques
JD Vance’s new book is a memoir about his conversion to Catholicism
- Bookmark
- CommentsGo to comments
Two websites have restricted or banned reviews of Vice President JD Vance's new book following a stream of savage critiques.
Communion: Finding My Way Back to Faith, published June 16 by HarperCollins, is Vance's memoir detailing his conversion to Catholicism. It's his second book following Hillbilly Elegy, which chronicled his upbringing in a poor white Appalachian family.
On Friday, The Daily Beast reported that Amazon restricted reviews of the vice president’s new book to verified purchasers only, leaving the tome with just seven reviews: six five-stars and one two-star. On Goodreads, which is owned by Amazon, reviews were disabled entirely. A pop-up message informs viewers that "rating this book is temporarily unavailable." No prior ratings for the book are visible.
Both groups are connected to Amazon founder Jeff Bezos who has been cozying up to President Donald Trump in recent months.
The X account @HQNewsNow, affiliated with former Vice President Kamala Harris, posted an apparent screenshot from Goodreads on Thursday, writing: "Reviews have been turned off for JD Vance's book after it received numerous 1-star reviews."
The account also included a sampling of critiques, which seems to have been posted before the review function was disabled.
"Why would anyone believe anything that comes from this man?" read a one-star review. "He does not uphold Christian values, has lied to all Americans, does not respect his wife & his soul has been sold to the highest bidder."
"Save your time and money. Read anything else," one reviewer wrote, while another chimed in: “Dude wouldn’t know religion if it or the pope hit him in the face.”
Goodreads, one of the world’s largest book discussion sites, only removes rating in response to “specific” situations, the website’s guidelines state. These include instances in which users rate books “based on factors irrelevant to the contents of the book,” or when users “artificially inflate or deflate a book’s average rating.”
Reviews from media outlets weren't particularly glowing either.
The Wall Street Journal describes it as “part religious memoir, part campaign book,” which suffers from moments of “egregious sloppiness.”
“Communion does [Vance] no favors, though, mostly in its sheer banality,” The Cut’s review states. “It’s not even incendiary…though he can’t help but hide his contempt for liberals (one woman is smeared for the mortal sin of wearing a beret).”
The Ohio-born vice president is currently in the middle of a nationwide promotional tour for the book, which has taken him to unusual places, including The View— which stars a gaggle of largely liberal women.
During that June 16 appearance, he described his new memoir as “actually way less political than you might think.”
Much of the conversation, though, centered on him defending Trump’s agenda. The panel grilled him on a number of issues, including Jeffrey Epstein, the Iran war and immigration.
At one point, Vance exclaimed: “Let’s talk about the book, I’m here to sell books.”
The Independent has reached out to Amazon and Goodreads for comment.

What is 'monoculturalism' and what does it look like in practice?
Sat 20 Jun 2026 at 5:17am
Pauline Hanson has called for Australia to reject multiculturalism and "live under the one cultural umbrella".
"We are a multiracial society, but we must be monocultural," the One Nation party leader said at her National Press Club address on Wednesday.
Since then, observers have asked what this means and how it would work in practice for Australia.
"Hanson made it plain — there is no room in her world for anything other than assimilation. But assimilation to what, exactly?" journalist Fotis Kapetopoulos wrote in the national Greek community newspaper Neos Kosmos newspaper this week.
"She says all Australians must live 'under one cultural umbrella' — but whose umbrella?"
Alan Gamlen, director of the Australian National University's Migration Hub, said "monoculturalism" was the idea that everyone followed a uniform set of norms and rules for behaviour.
That included the foods they ate, the clothes they wore, the gods they worshipped, the language they spoke, and more.
So what does it involve and are there any nations overseas that show what it might look like in Australia?
Where does 'monoculturalism' exist?
Professor Gamlen said truly monocultural nations did not exist.
"The first thing to say is monoculturalism is a fiction … All societies have differences in them," he said.
But he said some nations had tried to remove these differences, and portray themselves as homogenous, more than others.
"The question is … what type of difference exists, and then also what types and levels of difference are tolerable and desirable?"
In her speech, Senator Hanson objected to two aspects of modern Australia in particular: The number of people who were born overseas, and the number of people who spoke a language other than English at home.
She also appeared to oppose religious diversity, saying migrants should adopt a "Judeo-Christian" perspective.
Observers say that without more details, it's unclear exactly how Senator Hanson is proposing to make Australia "monocultural".
"What part of our culture would we forfeit for assimilation into Hanson's 'monocultural' world?" Kapetopoulos asked.
Senator Hanson's office has been contacted for comment.
One of the countries most often thought of as being a monoculture is Japan, which from the outside appears largely ethnically homogenous.
However, Kaori Okano, Professor of Asian Studies and Japanese at La Trobe University, said the country was more diverse than official statistics indicated.
She said the Japanese government collected data on the number of foreign residents, including permanent residents and work visa holders, of which there are about 4 million.
But not on the ancestry of Japanese citizens, the kind of data that the Australian census collect.
"So for instance, the Vietnamese refugees that arrived in the 1970s. Many of them either went on to North America or they became Japanese citizens, but there is no official category of 'Vietnamese-Japanese' because the government doesn't collect the data on ethnic heritage," she said.
Professor Okano said the actual percentage of the Japanese population of "non-Japanese descent" was about 6.5 per cent to 8 per cent.
"Obviously, it is much lower than migrant countries such as Australia or Canada," she said. "However, it doesn't mean Japan is a monocultural country."
She said the figure also included the Indigenous Ainu from the north of the country and Okinawan people from the southern tip, who both have their own language dialects and cultures.
Professor Okano said Japan was becoming increasingly multicultural due to migrants coming to fill labour shortages caused by its aging population and low birthrate.
"So definitely in terms of labour productivity, diversity is important but there are much more benefits to the society beyond that."
Neighbouring South Korea is also regarded as a more monocultural nation than many. People with migrant backgrounds reportedly make up 5 per cent of the population.
However, it also has diversity, including in religious faith.
Half of the population report having no religion, 17 per cent practise Buddhism, 20 per cent identify as Protestant and 11 per cent as Catholic.
Is there monoculturalism in Europe?
Some European countries have come under criticism recently for their cultural diversity and migration policies, including from the Trump administration.
Senator Hanson added her own on Wednesday when asked whether she believed Australia was "in danger of being swamped by Muslim migration", saying they had made the United Kingdom, France and Germany "just absolutely s-holes".
"I don't want Australia to become like that," she said.
Within Europe, there is fierce political debate about cultural diversity and far-right parties have capitalised on anti-migration sentiment.
Hans Kundnani, who has authored books about culture, empire and race on the continent, said discussion of monoculturalism intensified after 2015, during a wave of migration by refugees fleeing conflicts in the Middle East.
"There are different versions [of monoculturalism] in different European countries based on their own particular histories," he said.
Then-German Chancellor Angela Merkel declared multiculturalism had failed in 2010, calling for immigrants to do more to integrate, including learning German.
As British prime minister, David Cameron similarly criticised "state multiculturalism" in a 2011 speech on radicalisation and the causes of terrorism.
But Mr Kundnani, a former director of the Europe programme at the Royal Institute of International Affairs, said moves towards monoculturalism in Europe had mostly been "performative" and an "electoral strategy" to appease far-right parties and voters rather than about any real policy change.
Among European countries, Poland is seen as more monocultural than others, partly a result of World War II, the Holocaust and decades of communism.
"During the [post-war] Socialist period … achieving population homogeneity was an official aim of the state as part of a nationalist ideology: a process facilitated by tight border controls and restrictions on free movement," a 2021 research paper by University of Sheffield academics Aneta Piekut and Gill Valentine said.
Politicians have voiced opposition to migration in recent years as the country undergoes demographic change.
Opinion polls also showed the number of people saying Poland should not accept refugees from regions with armed conflicts rose from 20 per cent in 2015 to 60 per cent by 2018, the paper said.
The former United Nations Special Rapporteur on cultural rights, Karima Bennoune, accused Poland's government of disseminating a "myth" of a "homogenous" Polish culture after she visited the country in 2018.
"It's a very limited way of viewing what is a very complex society in Poland that includes minorities, that includes migrants, but that also includes people from the majority culture with a wide range of interpretations of that culture and with very different political views, including views about culture and religion," she said.
What is the cost of 'monoculturalism'?
Andrew Jakubowicz, Professor of Sociology at the University of Technology, Sydney, said "larger, more complex nations" were never monocultural.
"Though we have a long and unfortunate history across the world in which particular groups try and cleanse their society of other ethnic groups," he said.
"If you think of the sorts of things that Nazi Germany did, where they wanted to get rid of Jews, Gypsies, Slavs, anyone who didn't fit their particular very limited view."
He said groups trying to create monocultures tended to try removing people who did not fit into "some sort of imaginary perfection, usually in the most drastic way possible".
Experts say the closest the world has to a true monoculture in the world is North Korea, where about 99.8 per cent of the population are ethnically Korean and norms around language, religion, music, clothing are strictly policed.
"And what that tends to suggest is that in larger societies, monocultures can only exist through the use of force and violence to suppress differences," Professor Jakubowicz said.
"And whether you want to live in that sort of framework, I think would be very, very difficult."
Professor Jakubowicz said another example of a project to impose a monoculture was when ISIS attempted to set up a state adhering to its extremist interpretation of Islam in the Middle East.
"They were taking people who didn't fit their view and either exterminating them or turning them into slaves," he said.
What would monoculturalism change in Australia?
Professor Jakubowicz said in some ways, what Senator Hanson advocated in her National Press Club speech — a multiracial society sharing a common culture — already existed in Australia.
"If we're saying, look, people can come from any background you like as long as they support the core values of the society, well, in many ways, that's what multiculturalism is," he said.
"So this is maybe a distinction without a difference.
"The core elements that make a multicultural society work are it's democratic, it's respectful, it recognises difference. It recognises the rights of everybody else."
Meanwhile, a recent Australian Bureau of Statistics survey showed support for cultural diversity remained high in Australia.
More than 70 per cent of people agreed that accepting immigrants from many different countries made Australia stronger, a decline compared to 2023 (78 per cent), but still higher than pre-pandemic levels.
Professor Gamlen said societies needed a balance of diversity and cohesion, pointing to the impact of fragmented media environments in Western countries.
"We all inhabit different social media-constructed realities. We're all in our different social networks, so we see a different version of reality," he said.
"There is a sense in which the centre cannot hold in modern society with the level of fragmentation that we're experiencing.
"However, it's not necessarily the case that migration is responsible for that fragmentation."
