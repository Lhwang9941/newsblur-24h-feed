Treasure Island: Inside the Hawaii antique store where ‘very rich dead people’s possessions are up for grabs
‘Endangered Pieces’ on Maui was rebranded as ‘Very Rich Dead People’s Things For Sale,’ Rachel Dobkin discovers
- Bookmark
- CommentsGo to comments
In the beach town of Kihei, on the Hawaiian island of Maui, sits a warehouse full of treasures from millionaires’ colorful pasts.
“I would say there's a million items in this shop,” 83-year-old owner David Ross told The Independent.
Ross’ store used to be called “Endangered Pieces” until he rebranded it as “Very Rich Dead People’s Things For Sale.”
The owner explained what inspired the eye-catching name.
“A guy was having a garage sale, and he called it ‘Dead People's Garage Sale,’ so I picked up on that, and I called myself ‘Very Rich Dead People Things For Sale,’ and ever since I did that, my business tripled, because everybody wanted to see what the rich dead people had,” Ross said.
The warehouse holds more than 2,000 pieces of art — because, as Ross put it, “most rich dead people have a lot of art” — as well as more than 200 amethyst crystals, some of which are 10 feet tall, and more than 200 lamps.
Ross is especially proud of an almost four-foot, penis-shaped amethyst that he refuses to sell.
“Everybody likes looking at it and checking it out and taking their picture with it, so it's pretty incredible,” the owner said.
When asked about the coolest antiques that Ross has collected in his 60 years in the business, a few items come to mind.
“I have this airplane wing that's made into a dining room table, it's from a 1939 DC-3 Gooney Bird, that's pretty amazing too,” Ross said.
Ross said he also recently acquired five 18th-century life-size statues that resemble Chinese warriors.
“That's pretty fascinating, but I could go on and on,” he said.
Ross’ shop has not just attracted locals and tourists but celebrities.
Musicians Steven Tyler, Mick Fleetwood, Paul Simon and Alice Cooper have all visited the store, and CBS Mornings host Gail King.
“Most of those people are regulars. They come in, they're in there for like three, four hours, sometimes. Fleetwood spent half a day in there,” Ross said.
“They'll buy a lot of stuff, and I'll deliver it to their houses, and sometimes they have things to get rid of, and I end up acquiring some of their goodies.”
Those who want to visit the museum-like warehouse will have to pay a $2 admission fee.
“It weeds out some of the people that I don't want in the shop,” Ross said, explaining that it’s a deterrent for some visitors who may try to steal items.
“Some people really get upset at it. They say, ‘I'm not paying $2 to come in.’ I go, ‘You know what? I'll pay you $2 not to come in. I don't care,’” the owner added.
Ross grew up in San Francisco where his family owned pawn shops. “That kind of got me interested in buying and selling items,” he explained.
When Ross moved to Hawaii in 1988, he started a moving company. When people used his services, they would ask him if he wanted to buy items they no longer needed, and he would then sell them. Shortly after, he opened his first antique shop.
Ross said he acquires the antiques he sells “many, many different ways,” including from people who buy a house already furnished - but not to their style; people who move to Maui and then realize they don’t have enough space for all their things and through people dying, of course.
“Since I started saying ‘Rich Dead People Things For Sale,’ I would get calls from all over the country where people say, ‘Look, my aunt just died in Maui, and we don't want to come all the way out there from Baltimore. Would you be interested in buying her estate?’ And she has Maseratis, and she has all kinds of amazing antiques.’”
Ross said that he probably buys a lot more items than he sells, but that he has fun doing it.
“I have a big overhead, but I do okay. I gotta say, I do it for the labor of love. It's fun,” the owner said. “I'm a skater, sometimes I put on my skates, and I'll skate around the warehouse while I'm working.”

Hong Kong’s dog-friendly licence scheme triggers fierce online debate
Debate emerges as branch of Cafe 100% in Kam Ying Court Shopping Centre pulls out of dog-friendly scheme, with staff citing safety concerns
Hong Kong’s dog-friendly restaurant scheme has sparked a fierce online debate over public hygiene and acceptable pet behaviour, while a branch of a cafe has opted out of the initiative just three days after it took effect.
While more than 940 restaurants opened their doors to owners and their dogs last Thursday, clips showing dogs licking plates and sitting on dining tables and chairs have gone viral online.
But other internet users have also made the case that many canine diners have been well-behaved.
The debate raged as a branch of Cafe 100%, located in Ma On Shan’s Kam Ying Court Shopping Centre, posted a notice outside the premises on Friday saying it had opted out of the policy.
A staff member told the South China Morning Post that the cafe had pulled out over safety concerns.
“Our space is very tight, and staff are constantly carrying boiling water around, so we are concerned that dogs could easily get hurt,” she said, stressing that the decision was not the result of any complaints.
