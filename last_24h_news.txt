Knox Family brings country, rock and stories to Sydney Opera House for 'Generations and Dynasties'
When you see old school photos of Uncle Roger Knox from back in the day, it is easy to see why he was labelled "The Black Elvis".
The slick black hairdo accompanied by some big sideburns, the giant collars on bright clothing and wide-open unbuttoned shirts, Uncle Roger has always been an entertainer.
Loved by fans, who call him the "Koori King of Country", this seasoned singer-songwriter will perform at the Sydney Opera House for one night only, accompanied by his family.

Music has been a huge part of Uncle Roger's life and the way he talks about it, you get a sense that it has got him through some dark and rough times.
"It's a feeling. Music to me is a form of healing. When we go out to perform, we go out and give a 100 per cent effort," he says.
"That's the way you can open doors, you can open minds, and you can open up, and people see you. So, give 100 per cent."
Uncle Roger is 77 years old and living with a few ailments that come with age. But it hasn't stopped his passion for singing or performing.
Once the music legend picks up the trusty guitar and starts strumming away, it's like he's transported back to a different time.
"I look forward to it [performing] every time, even though it's hard at this point now. I got sort of crook knees and things. But I look forward to going out and visiting communities, working with elders, sitting around and talking to them," Uncle Roger says.
Classic songs for a new generation
Uncle Roger's musical catalogue includes classics like Warrior in Chains, Streets of Tamworth, Koori Rose and Stranger in My Country.
When he reflects on a lifetime in music, he tells a story that makes him smile.
"I think it was a month ago, I was in Cherbourg Mission, and I saw a three or four-year-old child singing Koori Rose," he says.
"And I think, that song ain't going to die because she's going to grow up and teach her kids when we're gone.
"That means something to me. To know that they continue to sing these songs … that does a lot to my senses."
Now in its third season at the Sydney Opera House, Generations and Dynasties is a series that celebrates Indigenous musical families, bringing them together to perform their songs and share their stories to an intimate audience.
The Knox family will be the latest clan to sing and show off their amazing talents.
Family patriarch Uncle Roger will play side-by-side with his son, Buddy, and his two grandsons, Googoorewon and Teangi.
Buddy has played with his father, Roger, countless times over the years. He remembers fondly that he didn't need to be asked twice to play guitar for his father at the age of 15.
For Buddy, this was his education. Travelling the country with his father, Roger and his famed Euraba Band. Hanging out and playing songs with the likes of other Black music giants like Uncle Jimmy Little, Uncle Col Hardy and Uncle Vic Simms.
"I wasn't welcomed at school. I didn't want to be there anyway. I hit the road and it just went from there, he says.
"I've got a lot of heroes, but that starts there."
This performance promises to be different. Buddy will be on stage with his legendary father, plus his own sons, not just playing music and entertaining, but also sharing culture and giving insights into the Knox family.
"I'm looking forward to it; you know what I mean? We've played together all the time, but not like this. It's a different deal to be able to tell the stories," Buddy says.
Googoorewon, better known as Goori, is Buddy's son. Aside from sharing the musical genes, he is also an accomplished actor, appearing in musical theatre productions, including Hamilton and Rent and many television commercials.
Goori explained, he is inspired by his famous grandmother, Auriel Andrew, a talented musician and actress who was the first Aboriginal woman to appear on Australian television, featuring in numerous TV shows.
In 1973, Ms Andrew performed at the opening of the Sydney Opera House — the same venue where her grandsons will perform.
Performing with his family will be another career high point, but as much as Goori is looking forward to playing and singing with his family, nervousness is building within.
"Now I'm becoming more of a musician, I feel a bit of pressure because they're all incredible musicians," he says.
"If I mess up, they're going to know who did that.
"I'm looking across at Dad [Buddy], making sure that I'm on the right track. I'm looking across [brother] Teangi, making sure I'm on the right track. And if I can get that look of approval, then I know I'm on the right track."
'Generations and Dynasties' suits the Knox family
Other Black musicians and their families who have been part of Generations and Dynasties include Bunna Lawrie from Coloured Stone and his family, Troy Casser-Daley and his daughter Jem, as well as the Wenitong brother and sister combination of Naomi from Shakaya and Joel, who performed with hip-hop trio, Local Knowledge.
Michael Hutchings, Head of First Nations Programming at the Sydney Opera House, says there's been a lot of love, culture, music and education shared across the three seasons.
"Music was a way for them to tell their stories, whether it be stories of resilience or racism or stories of hope and overcoming challenges and, it didn't take a lot to pick up a guitar around the campfire or an old piano and just start making music."
It's fitting to end this series of Generations and Dynasties with the Knox family because they are loved by people, young and old, Mr Hutchings says.
He added that many previous performers all mentioned Uncle Roger Knox and the influence he had on their music and the respect they had for him.
"The Jarrett family [Wire MC, Tasman Keith and Kapital J] mentioned him, Troy Cassar-Daley mentioned him. We've had the [muscians] Donovans recently. They also mentioned Uncle Roger," Mr Hutchings says.
"So, it was just natural, we had to have the Knox family in.
"What's great about them is they cover so many different genres of music as well, from country to blues to musical theatre."
Timeless singer across generations
From performing to thousands at concerts or a couple of hundred people at a pub or club, Uncle Roger says there are mixed emotions about the Generations and Dynasties performance, but he knows his family will get him through the night.
"I'm looking forward to it," he says.
"I am nervous. I'll tell you the truth. But I think once we get in there, settle down, I'll be all right.
"They make me confident, they lift me up, then I can just bang, go and do what I want to do."
