Diplomacy, the art of navigating international relations, used to be practiced primarily by trained professionals. However, in recent years, there have been increasing reports of academic diplomacy, sports diplomacy, and cultural diplomacy – all of which help to strengthen bilateral and multilateral ties.
Now it appears that there has also been rabbinic diplomacy between Israel and the US for more than a century.
Part of the tale of that diplomacy was revealed on Sunday at the President’s Residence, at the unveiling of an exhibition under the title of Chief Rabbis at the White House, which was conceived by the Jerusalem Center for Applied Policy, a research center dedicated to the sovereignty, security, prosperity, and international standing of Jerusalem.
According to JCAP founder and President Chaim Silberstein, it was realized in 2024 that it was the 100th anniversary year of the meeting at the White House between President Calvin Coolidge and Rabbi Abraham Isaac Kook, who was Chief Rabbi of Palestine.
The meeting had taken place on April 15, 1924.
Silverstein and his 20-member team thought this was something to celebrate and met with President Isaac Herzog to sound him out.
Herzog was in favor and recalled that his grandfather, Rabbi Isaac Halevi Herzog, the first Chief Rabbi of the State of Israel, had twice been to the White House to meet with presidents of the United States.
The first time was in 1941, when Israel was still under the administration of the British Mandate, and the second was in May 1949. At the first meeting, Herzog begged President Franklin Delano Roosevelt to do something to save the Jews of Europe from the Nazi death machine. At the second meeting, Herzog thanked President Harry S. Truman for what he had done to enable the Jewish People to return to their ancestral homeland.
Anniversary year of American independence
In 2024, Israel was campaigning for the release of hostages who were still in captivity in Gaza. It was agreed that this was an inappropriate year for a celebration. But with this year being the 250th anniversary year of American independence, it was considered a good time to take the exhibition to Washington and to thank President Donald Trump for all that he has done for Israel and the Jewish People.
Before that, the exhibition will be shown at Yeshivat Mercaz HaRav on Jerusalem Day, and also at the Rabbi Kook Museum, which was previously Rabbi Kook’s home.
No date has yet been set for the exhibition to be shown in Washington, but when the White House sets a date, it will be coordinated with Israel’s ambassador to the US, Yechiel Leiter, and the Ashkenazi and Sephardi Chief Rabbis of Israel, Kalman Ber and David Yosef.
At the unveiling ceremony, President Herzog, who is a history buff, particularly when it comes to his own family, which includes several iconic personalities, appeared to know more than the JCAP person who was explaining the background to each of the items on display, and simply took over. He also pointed out that at dinners organized for the rabbis by the Jewish community, men and women sat together, as shown in one of the photographs. To which Yaakov Hagoel, the Chairman of the Executive of the World Zionist Organization, added: “They were also together at the Western Wall.”
The Third Chief Rabbi to visit the White House was Rabbi Avraham Shapira, whose son, Rabbi Yaakov Shapira, the head of Mercas HaRav, said that at his father’s March 1992 meeting with President George H.W. Bush, the two men discussed the importance of faith not only in spiritual leaders but also in heads of state. They also spoke of American assistance in enabling the exodus of Soviet Jews.
Ashkenazi Chief Rabbi Kalman Ber, in speaking of the strong bond between the Chief Rabbinate and the State of Israel with the Presidents of the US, voiced the belief that President Donald Trump has a special mission to help the Jewish People and the State of Israel. “He just continues helping again and again,” said Ber.
David Brownstein, the Deputy Head of Mission at the US Embassy, noted that there are many miracles taking place in contemporary Israel.
The exhibition includes communication exchanges between the offices of the Chief Rabbis and the White House. The technological advances in communications that are taken for granted these days did not exist till three quarters of the way through the last century. Letters and telegrams were the essence of long-distance communication. Copies of the originals were reproduced for the exhibition.
Herzog took time out from preparations for a busy week before departing on an official visit to Kazakhstan, which is a member of the Abraham Accords and the Board of President Trump’s Peace Council. During his stay there, Herzog will attend an Israel Independence Day reception hosted by the Israeli Embassy. Accompanying the president on his visit will be representatives of several Israeli authorities and organizations, in particular those whose focus is innovation and technology.

Yes just announced that the new season of Fauda, one of the most popular Israeli series of all time, will premiere on Yes Action and Yes VOD in Israel on May 18, when the first two episodes will drop.
After that, one episode of the 11-episode fifth season will be released per week. The season will later be shown internationally on Netflix, on a date yet to be announced.
What we know about the closely guarded plot of the upcoming season of the series about an Israeli counter-terrorism unit headed by fearless commando Doron Kavillio (Lior Raz, who co-created the series with Avi Issacharoff), is that regulars Itzik Cohen, Doron Ben-David, and Yaakov Zada Daniel will be returning. They will be joined by a new big name, French star Mélanie Laurent, who played Shoshana Dreyfus in Quentin Tarantino’s Inglourious Basterds.
One familiar face who will not be returning, except possibly in a cameo, is actor/singer Idan Amedi. Amedi was called up for reserve duty in the Gaza War and was badly injured in January 2024. He has since recovered but has said that he will give priority to his singing career, rather than to acting.
Raz and Avi Issacharoff have said that they already had a storyline for the fifth season ready to go in 2023, but scrapped it following the Hamas attack on October 7 and rewrote the episodes to reflect the massacre and the subsequent war.
The promise of revenge in new season
Titles in the trailer promise, “Revenge is just the starting point.”
The series, which started mainly on the West Bank in its first season, has moved around geographically a great deal. The third season featured key episodes set in Gaza, and the fourth season moved among Brussels, Lebanon, Jenin, and Ramla. In the fifth season, based on the trailer, Doron and his colleagues head to Marseilles for an off-the-grid mission that officials will deny knowledge of if they are caught. Doron, wrapped in a keffiyeh, joins protesters at a pro-Palestine march, as another character comments, “This isn’t Provence.”
“If we don’t do this, this day will haunt us for the rest of our lives,” says one of the characters as they undertake their latest mission.

How friendship with Russell Crowe led to an MMA fight

Daniel MacPherson and Amy Shark fight for their life in Beast
Beast star Daniel MacPherson was kicked in the head a lot. It was his choice.
The Neighbours alum and Foundation hero was determined his gnarly fight scenes with real-life martial artist and actor Bren Foster would look legit in director Tyler Atkins's viscerally shot MMA film, co-written by Russell Crowe and David Frigerio.
"Bren's an extraordinary martial artist," MacPherson says.
"I'll never forget the pace and ferocity with which he attacks his choreography. He wasn't slowing down for me, so I had to get up to his speed."
The pair worked together on sci-fi movies Infini and The Osiris Child. Now they go head to head in front of a 10,000-strong ONE Championship crowd in Bangkok.
"It was extraordinary," MacPherson says. "Not only visually, but also the spiritual nature that comes with practising martial arts in south-east Asia."
The slog it takes to get Australian indies off the ground bought MacPherson time.
"I had faith that it was gonna get to the screen, and I had to be ready," he says. "I've never pushed myself harder."
His work on action show Strike Back was a great education.
"They didn't like using stunt doubles," MacPherson says.
"They were like, 'We want the camera in your face with you as you're jumping out of a helicopter, rappelling down a building, or running out of an exploding car'.
"And look, I'm an actor. I like having my face in there."
Hit and run
Giving good bloody face in Beast, MacPherson plays Patton, a fisherman battling the elements after walking away from the ring following a stint in prison.
With another kid on the way, bills piling up and his brother, Shayda actor Mojean Aria's Malon, in debt to dubious characters and badgered by his manager (Luke Hemsworth), Patton reluctantly commits to one more fight.
But his disappearing act wounded former coach and father-figure, Sammy, played with a gruff but gooey heart by Gladiator star Russell Crowe.
"Acting opposite Russell tested me as much as fighting with Bren," MacPherson says.
"All while having to look like Chris Hemsworth with my shirt off as best I could and with picking my son up from school.
"But I thrive under pressure, and it worked well for a character like Patton, who's meant to look exhausted, frustrated, and a little pissed off with just about everybody."
MacPherson first encountered Crowe on Dancing with the Stars. Their next meeting was a little more fraught.
"I nearly ran him over in LA while I was on the way to an audition, and he was riding his mountain bike on Melrose Avenue," MacPherson laughs.
A humorous back-and-forth erupted on Twitter. Soon, MacPherson was invited to Crowe's farm.
"I was convinced he'd heard how wonderful my performance was in Infini," he says.
"Then Russell introduced me to his parents and Dancing with the Stars is his mum's favourite show. So we became instantly family, but it wasn't because of my acting."
Crowe must rate MacPherson right enough in that department, as the pair have since worked together on Poker Face (2022) and Land of Bad (2024), as well as Beast.
"He was there to support me, while mentoring a new breed of filmmakers and performers."
Cinderella story
That next generation includes ARIA Award-winning singer-songwriter and Australian Idol judge Amy Shark, whose fourth studio album, Soft Pop, releases on July 31.
With Beast her debut feature, she plays Rose, Sammy's daughter, who steps up to retrain Patton when her dad washes his hands of him.
"What a lot of people don't know is that acting was my first love," Shark reveals.
"I did a lot of theatre when I was a kid and was always making videos, doing the odd commercial here and there. As I got into my teen angsty years, music took over and I was obsessed with playing guitar."
While Shark shares Crowe's passion for NRL, it was their mutual love of music that forged their friendship.
"Obviously he's a musician himself and a real backer of Aussie music," Shark says. "He liked my albums, came to some of my shows and we jammed together a few times.
"Then we started talking about music, footy and movies, and I would always tease him, 'When are you gonna tap me in, coach?' And he kinda called me on my bluff."
Teaming up with the Cinderella Man in her debut film is a dream come true for Shark.
"I grew up loving Jean-Claude Van Damme and Bloodsport," she says. "So I feel very blessed."
Christmas come early
Like MacPherson, Shark threw herself into training, picking up on childhood Taekwondo lessons: "I never got my black belt, which I'm really pissed about."
She enjoyed swimming in the spikiness between Crowe's Sammy and MacPherson's Patton.
"I've had plenty of practice in my own family dynamic," she chuckles.
"It's just a normal Christmas. And that's what I loved about Beast. We get fed a lot of Disney stuff, but this movie is so real. Everyone's got their own issues, layers and agendas."
Alongside her co-stars, Atkins made Shark feel at ease on set.
"Tyler's demeanour is so calm," she says. "I never felt overwhelmed or that he didn't have the time for me to approach him to talk about anything."
MacPherson says she's a natural.
"Amy came in with one of the most ferocious auditions I've ever seen," he recalls. "Then she came on set prepped and ready, with a freshness and an enthusiasm, excited to do good work.
"When I'm there every day, two hours in makeup every morning, that type of energy is really important. And at the LA premiere, Amy got some of the biggest laughs."
Beast is in cinemas April 23, then streaming on Stan from May 27.
