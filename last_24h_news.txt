Is the government really overhauling employment services, or tinkering at the edges of a broken system?
Sun 31 May 2026 at 5:00am
Is the Albanese government planning the biggest shake-up to the employment services system in 30 years?
That's what it claimed last week.
With big fanfare, its announcement gave the impression that something big and positive was happening, that finally a government in Canberra was going to fix a broken system.
But when Amanda Rishworth, the federal employment minister, delivered a speech to the National Press Club about it, it was unclear if the government's major "shake-up" of employment services was going to be as momentous as claimed.
Under its proposed plan, the long-term unemployed will hopefully get more intensive support to help them get back into the labour force.
But its plan won't require legislative change. It won't abolish mutual obligations. It will still rely on private job agencies to find work for jobseekers (while paying them "incentive payments" to put effort into the task).
So, major elements of our employment services system won't be changing at all.
Like the ancient paradox about the Ship of Theseus, if you replace every part of a ship over time (its mast, sails, ropes, and hull), but you adhere to the original design of the ship, can you say the ship is new?
Isn't it the same ship?
The 'radical transformation' of the system in the 1990s
Do you want to see what a genuine shake-up of the system looks like?
Australia used to have a federally funded "employment service" that found jobs for people across the country.
It was called the Commonwealth Employment Service (CES). It was established in 1946. Its staff did not need "incentive payments" to encourage them to help the unemployed find work. They helped people find work because it was their job. And they helped successive governments deliver full employment for three decades after World War II.
But that publicly run job brokerage was eventually privatised.
In 1998, the Howard government introduced the Job Network, which downsized labour market programs, deregulated the training market, and encouraged providers from the private and community sectors to compete to find jobs for the unemployed. It deliberately undermined the CES, which died a slow death.
It also introduced Work for the Dole programs, and it intensified the use of "mutual obligations", which forced unemployed Australians to do often pointless activities in return for receiving unemployment benefits.
In 2001, the OECD said it was a "radical transformation" of employment service delivery "without parallel in OECD countries".
That was a genuine reform to Australia's system. It ushered in a completely different model for governments to "manage" the large number of unemployed people who needed unemployment benefits.
But was it a positive reform? That's a different question.
It sometimes gets someone a job, if they're lucky
In 2023, a Labor-dominated parliamentary committee looked back at that multi-decade experiment with privatisation and an outsourced quasi-market system for employment services.
And it called for the system to be completely rebuilt.
It said after decades of outsourcing, the Australian public service now had little corporate memory or experience of the complexities of employment service delivery, so it couldn't even judge if the billion-dollar contracts it was awarding to private sector job agencies were buying value for money.
"We have an inefficient outsourced fragmented social security compliance management system that sometimes gets someone a job against all odds," it said.
"Every country that has experimented with extensive outsourcing has eventually realised that it does not work without a public sector core, as some companies will profiteer."
It recommended bringing the public service back into the fold.
"The Committee does not propose the creation of a giant bureaucracy that does everything, nor the recreation of the old monolithic Commonwealth Employment Service (CES)," the report said.
"However, it is critical that the government play a much more active role."
The report said the system was being driven by the pernicious myth of the "dole bludger," which frames unemployment as a personal choice, and that that culture had to change.
And it acknowledged that many experts had been calling for mutual obligations to be abolished, with evidence showing that they can worsen a jobseeker's employability because they take time away from genuine job search efforts and training.
However, it said mutual obligations should be significantly reformed, rather than abolished.
A three-tiered model with tailored services
So how big were Ms Rishworth's proposed reforms? Will they be as significant as the Howard government's "radical transformation" of the system?
Ms Rishworth said she wanted to build a "new employment service". She released a discussion paper that provides details about what she has in mind.
Under the redesign, people seeking employment will be funnelled into three distinct tiers:
- Online and brief intervention services for people closely connected to the labour market (recently unemployed)
- Targeted provider services for people with employment gaps (unemployed for a while), lower educational attainment, or cycling patterns of work
- Intensive services for people furthest from the labour market, including people with complex barriers (long-term unemployed)
The online service for the first tier will be "predominantly delivered by government" via a digital platform and Australian Public Service-led support.
The second tier will be managed by contracted service providers, who will be expected to prioritise activities that are proven to get people into suitable jobs.
How will the government make that happen?
It says it could use a range of up-front payments, progress payments, outcome payments, bonuses, and performance and accountability measures to ensure that private service providers put genuine effort into helping unemployed Australians find suitable jobs.
Different contractual arrangements could also be used to make providers more accountable for poor service delivery, with options available to the government to amend or terminate contracts if necessary.
And the third tier (for the long-term unemployed) will likely be managed by community organisations with skilled case managers that have a strong local presence and deep connections to local services, supports, employers and communities.
Ms Rishworth says the new system will still use mutual obligations, too. But she says they should be individually tailored to actually help people to find work, rather than to punish people for being unemployed.
Overall, the government says its new three-tiered model would be a big improvement on the current system, where a one-size-fits-all approach is thoroughly unsuitable for people in long-term unemployment.
Where is the system heading?
When reading the discussion paper, one is left with the feeling that the government thinks the ideal employment service would be capable of delivering the type of service that the old CES used to provide, in many cases.
For example, it says a proper system would have stronger connections between regional employment services and local employers and labour markets, and stronger links to the skills and training system.
It says recent experience has shown that local solutions, led by local service providers, engaging local businesses and community partners, can achieve stronger outcomes for jobseekers by tailoring services to local needs.
"Hallmarks of a quality service include frontline staff (whether from the Australian Public Service or contracted providers) who deliver personalised services, the capability to respond to a diverse range of needs, and strong connections to employers and other services," it says.
Talk about appreciating what you once had.
So it's interesting to see that the government is still unwilling to completely turn its back on mutual obligations and the quasi-market system of contracted service delivery.
And that, in turn, means that its "new employment service" won't be completely new either.
The new system may have a larger role for public service delivery, and it may make a genuine effort to re-humanise and personalise the services for unemployed individuals, but it will still retain elements of privatisation and Howard's "market-based solutions" that have done demonstrable damage.
Will it be the Ship of Theseus with new masts and sails? Or will it be a completely new ship? It feels like it will be a mix.
