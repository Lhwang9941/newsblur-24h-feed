11 best cylinder vacuums to combat dirty floors, tried and tested
Ditch big heavy vacuums for a cylinder that flies round with ease
- Bookmark
Cylinder vacuums remain a popular choice thanks to their strong suction power, lightweight designs and easy manoeuvrability. While they lack the convenience of cordless vacuum or robot vacuums, they often deliver excellent cleaning performance and are available in options to suit everything from small flats to busy family homes.
It’s no surprise that professional cleaners often turn to cylinder vacuums. Cleaning expert and author Laura Mountford says their appeal lies in their versatility and powerful suction. “Cylinder vacuums are lightweight but with heavy duty power, because they focus on pure suction rather than rotating brushes,” says Laura. “Most models come with lots of tools and attachments that can be used to clean all around your home. They are basically the Swiss Army knife of vacuum cleaners – great for vacuuming different surface types, the stairs, getting into tight spaces, sofas, curtains and rugs.”
With years of experience testing vacuum cleaners, I put a range of cylinder models through their paces to find the ones truly worth buying. From bagged and bagless designs to options for allergy sufferers or one of the best pet vacuums, this guide covers the best cylinder vacuums for every type of household.

1Miele boost CX1 parquet powerline
- Best cylinder vacuum cleaner overall
- Why we love it - Superior suction
 - Great manoeuvrability
 - Elegant design
 - Standard floorhead suits all floor types
 
- Take note - Bin isn't as large as other cylinder vacuums
 
There’s a reason Miele vacuums remain perennially popular, with models that last and accessories that work. Bagless and compact, the boost CX1 vacuums are slightly cheaper options in Miele’s lineup, with several varieties to choose from. The best cylinder vacuum I tested, the parquet powerline, is part of the range, and comes with a specialised ‘parquet twister’ head for delicate hard floors.
I loved how quickly this whizzed over my ancient hardwood floors, picking up dirt and debris without any risk of scratching, when using the standard floorhead. We foster cats for a charity, so the house usually looks like several tigers have been shedding their fur on an hourly basis, but Miele’s cylinder vacuum made light work of hair (including my own very long additions), pulling it out of both short and deeper pile carpets with ease.
If I’m being ultra picky, I would love the addition of an anti-hair wrap to the main head and the 1l bin isn’t as large as other vacuums I tested, such as the Dyson cinetic big ball animal 2 (1.7 litres). But aside from these minor quibbles, this cylinder vacuum is lightweight, easy to manoeuvre and gives excellent results.

Still, the standard attachment is exceptionally good on hard floors, so this is a small price to pay for brilliant results. The integrated crevice and upholstery tools and the dusting brush, combined with the Sebo’s ability to get into edges, makes removing fiddly fine dust and debris a breeze.

Your questions about cylinder vacuums answered
What is the best cylinder vacuum?
The Miele boost CX1 parquet powerline was my best overall cylinder vacuum cleaner, thanks to its ability to handle all the dirt, debris and pet hair I chucked at it. If you’re a pet owner and you have the budget, the Sebo airbelt E3 premium + boost 2.0 and its electric power head will be hard to beat for sheer suction power. Those on a budget won’t go wrong with the compact Morphy Richards MRXCYLINDERFLEX powersphere cylinder vacuum, which I thought had really impressive suction and a decent bin capacity for its price.
How I tested cylinder vacuum cleaners
I put every cylinder vacuum in this guide to the test in my busy family home with two cats, and assessed each one on the following factors:
- Suction power: Strong suction is one of the main reasons people choose cylinder vacuums, so I tested each vacuum with a variety of substances including cereal, flour and pet hair to see how well it lifted debris. This was combined with general day-to-day vacuuming.
- Manoeuvrability: I looked at how easy each vacuum was to move around, paying particular attention to how it got into and around tight corners, as well as watching how well the wheels moved.
- Power cord length: Because cylinder vacuums are corded, I paid particular attention to both the length and design of each cord, noting whether it was a help or a hindrance.
- Dust bin size and ease of emptying: Big isn’t always better, depending on the size of your home, but I noted how large each bin was, how quickly it filled up and how easy it was to empty.
- Versatility: I tested the vacuums on laminate, hard wood, carpet, rugs, upholstery and stairs to see how well they performed in different cleaning scenarios.
- Value for money: I wasn’t looking for the cheapest vacuum cleaner on the market, but I ensured that all my inclusions are worth the money you’ll pay for them.
Which is better, a cylinder or an upright vacuum cleaner?
According to Kiril Natov, carpet and upholstery cleaning technician at Premium Clean, there are pros and cons to both varieties of vacuum:
Cylinder vacuum cleaners
- Pros: Versatile, more attachment options, lighter attachments, good for manoeuvring in tight spaces, quieter
- Cons: Bulkier to store
Upright vacuum cleaners
- Pros: Better for open spaces, work well on large carpeted areas, easy “push and go design”
- Cons: Heavier on stairs
What are the different types of cylinder vacuum?
As well as corded and cordless models, Natov breaks down the key cylinder varieties into four categories:
- Bagged: Securing your dust and debris within a fitted, replaceable bag, “these models are cleaner to empty and better for allergies”.
- Bagless: Cutting costs, “bagless vacuums save you from buying replacement bags but release more dust.”
- Allergy vacuums: “These are fitted with a (HEPA) filter to trap fine particles.”
- Pet model vacuums: Anticipating pet dander and the smell of wet dog, “these add turbo brushes and odour filters.”

United Airlines rep appears to threaten to call ICE on customer
The dispute began after the family spent hours at San Francisco International Airport trying to fix a middle-name error on a ticket
- Bookmark
- CommentsGo to comments
A United Airlines employee was captured on video appearing to threaten to call U.S. Immigration and Customs Enforcement on a passenger during a ticket counter dispute last week at San Francisco International Airport.
“Maybe we should call ICE on you,” the unidentified staff member is heard saying in the recording.
The altercation began when 54-year-old East Bay resident Julio Varela arrived at the airport with his family to resolve an issue with one of his daughters’ tickets, which had mistakenly listed her middle name as her last name.
According to Varela, the family had spent hours attempting to fix the error when the employee told them she could not assist and that they would miss their flight.
“I say, ‘well, my wife made the mistake, but we’ve been trying to correct it,’” Varela told CBS News. “So that's what we’re here for, so you can help us. She says, ‘well, I don't care if you fly or not.’ So that’s what got me upset. I said, ‘what’s your name?’ She said, ‘I’m not going to give you my name.’”
When the staff member refused to identify herself, Varela pulled out his mobile phone to record the interaction, prompting the worker to do the same.
Footage posted online shows the employee holding her phone directly in front of her face in an attempt to block her appearance while seemingly recording back.
From behind the camera, Varela responds to the initial statement: “You're going to call ICE on me?”
“Maybe you need to be,” the employee can be heard saying when Varela tells her to repeat herself, “because you don't act like a citizen. Get away."
The video shows the employee repeatedly telling Varela to “get out my face.” Varela responds by telling her, “you got some guts lady,” and threatens legal action, to which she replies, “okay, you sue.”
As the confrontation continues, Varela shouts, “United Airlines has a racist customer service lady,” and tells her, “you're lazy, and you're rude and you are racist.”
Varela, who was traveling with his wife and three daughters, noted that all five family members are U.S. citizens.
“I was so shocked, I was angry,” Varela told CBS News. “It’s intimidating, because my kids didn’t know, you know, if when we get back, are we, am I going to be arrested? Is ICE going to be there?”
He added: “My 14-year-old daughter is crying on the side, and she’s screaming. My daughter’s, like, ‘Why would you say you were going to call ICE?’ That's so rude and disrespectful, you know?”
Despite the statement from the employee, Varela said he refused to drop the matter.
“She assumed that I was just gonna be some idiot that was gonna, you know, just be quiet and shut up,” Varela told the outlet. “So I said, ‘No, call ICE.’”
The ticketing issue was eventually resolved minutes after the exchange, allowing the family to board their scheduled flight.
In a statement emailed to The Independent, United Airlines said it was investigating the incident but offered no further details.
