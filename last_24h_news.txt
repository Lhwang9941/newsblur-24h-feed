Bnei Brak’s leaders should "focus more on providing basic municipal services rather than policing public behavior," city council member Yaki Vider told 103FM on Sunday.
His criticism came after the municipality’s controversial decision to separate men and women on sidewalks near wedding halls
Vider, who heads the Likud faction on the Bnei Brak City Council, also criticized the Interior Ministry’s decision to suspend assistance for residents affected during Operation Lion's Roar until the municipality condemned an attack on city inspectors, calling the move unacceptable collective punishment.
About a week ago, the Bnei Brak municipality announced an unusual measure to separate men and women on sidewalks in the area surrounding the city’s event halls.
“There is a municipality in Bnei Brak where dealmakers think they own the public,” Vider said. “Their job is to clean, handle infrastructure and transportation. There are so many problems in this city that they are not dealing with; they are dealing with things they are not supposed to touch.”
Asked about the street at the center of the controversy, Vider said it is “a crowded street in Bnei Brak, in an area of wedding halls,” but argued there were better ways to address the congestion.
“Turn it into a pedestrian street in the evening. There are many transportation solutions that can address the crowding problem. The considerations are irrelevant,” he said.
Vider also criticized the municipality’s refusal to establish a municipal WhatsApp hotline.
“There is no such hotline in Bnei Brak. One of the mayors said, ‘We will not encourage residents to have a smartphone.’ Forget the hypocrisy, they have smartphones, and with their nice jeeps they travel all over the world, but that is not their job,” he said.
Asked how residents viewed the municipality’s conduct, Vider said they had not been consulted.
“The residents were not asked. Many things that can be solved go unaddressed because of irrelevant considerations. You are not the public’s modesty patrol; your job is to serve and provide an answer for infrastructure, and you are not doing that,” he said.
“Some residents do not leave the city,” he added. “I do not know if you are aware of the level of isolation; they read HaModia, Yated Ne’eman and Hamashbir, they praise the dealmakers and do not voice criticism. There is a rat infestation in the streets, but the municipality does not hear about it. Those who mainly suffer from it are the ultra-Orthodox public; they are the ones who live in Bnei Brak.”
Despite his criticism, Vider said municipal politics in the city had begun to change.
“The current mayor in the previous term talked about drafting yeshiva students. He said, ‘If you don’t vote for me, Yair Lapid will come.’ This time it was about infrastructure and cleanliness. They did not do what they promised, but they talked about it. There is one change, and we hope it will expand.”
Vider also condemned the Tax Authority’s decision to suspend treatment of compensation claims for Bnei Brak residents harmed during Operation Lion's Roar after the municipality declined to condemn the attack on its inspectors.
“Those violent rioters should have been arrested long ago by the Israel Police, but a clerk cannot punish an entire population that has done no wrong because of a radical group,” he said.
He compared the decision to a hypothetical scenario in which the IDF chief of staff halted protection for Arab communities because Arab Knesset members did not condemn Islamist terrorism.
“This is aggressive punishment against an entire public, and I cannot understand how this is going through quietly in the State of Israel. I promise it would not be accepted in silence for any other population,” he said.

Chef Aviv Moshe returns to Tel Aviv with kosher Italian restaurant Bella - restaurant review

Aviv Moshe is one of Israel’s most celebrated chefs, dating back to his days at the fine dining restaurant group Messa in downtown Tel Aviv.
In recent years, Moshe began cooking only kosher cuisine, first at Poupee in Tel Aviv and then at Maree Sky Lounge in Netanya. The good news is that Chef Moshe is officially back in Tel Aviv, now at the helm of Bella, a restaurant defining itself as a nuova osteria, serving traditional Italian food with contemporary interpretations.
As a kosher dairy restaurant serving classic Italian dishes using cheese, the main courses eschew meat and poultry, replacing them with fish.
Bella Mia is the centerpiece of a new culinary compound at the eastern end of Einstein Street, in the upscale Tel Aviv suburb of Ramat Aviv. This boulevard now boasts two kosher culinary destinations – the second one a new rooftop food court in the Ofer Ramat Aviv Mall.
Bella Mia will soon be joined by a Japanese izakaya restaurant downstairs, with an adjacent third eatery – a neighborhood fish bistro – set to complete the tripartite “Bella Mia” gastronomic complex. Chef Aviv is one of the owners of the grand project, in partnership with restaurateur Shuki Biton and his celebrity wife, Eden Ben Zaken.
The interior design of Bella has changed little from its previous Serafina incarnation, retaining its elegant ambiance and handsome bar, as well as the semi-open kitchen dominated by its white Italian pizza oven. The one prominent addition to the latter is a mountain of truffle butter, a testament to the role of one of the restaurant’s gourmet ingredients.
Bella is open primarily for dinner, on weekdays from 5 p.m. to midnight and on Saturdays after Shabbat (it opens briefly for lunch on Fridays). Notably, the restaurant offers a Happy Hour: Sunday-Thursday between 5 p.m. and 7 p.m., with a 20% discount off the entire menu.
English menus available
The current “soft opening” bilingual printed menu comprises six sections in English. Meanwhile, the wine, alcohol, and cocktail lists are only in the Hebrew version.
The full bar serves six specialty cocktails (NIS 62), as well as the classics. The Margo Milano is Bella’s take on a spicy Margarita, while La Palma – one of two vodka-based creations in this category – is a perfectly balanced, fruity cocktail.
The adequate, although not extensive, kosher wine list features exclusively Israeli vintages, except for sparkling wine and champagne from Europe. There is a rather limited selection of wines available by the glass.
Not including the house bread (NIS 37) – wood-fired focaccia, served with infused olive oil and truffle butter – the food menu’s six sections are: Crudo – Raw Fish (six entrées, NIS 72-NIS 87); Starters (including the house salad, NIS 58-NIS 76); Classic Italy – Sharing Plates and Appetizers (NIS 56-NIS 88); Main Courses – Pasta (NIS 88-NIS 96); Main Courses – Fish (NIS 158-NIS 168); and From the Oven, comprising three pizzas (NIS 83-NIS 88).
There are plenty of vegetarian and gluten-free choices (excluding the pastas and pizzas), but few vegan options.
The raw fish course contains more dishes than any other category. Those who like a bit of heat will not be disappointed by the jalapeno sashimi, six pieces of extremely fresh amberjack swimming in a piquant chili pepper and herb emulsion.
The burrata cuore di bue features a plump ball of the creamy mozzarella cheese perched on a bed of chopped heirloom tomatoes. When cut, the burrata oozes a delicious river of stracciatella over the delicately seasoned red tomatoes.
Bella Greens is a vertically presented salad of mixed leaves, soft buche cheese, and toasted matchstick almonds in an understated white balsamic vinegar dressing. This pleasing salad yields a refreshing interplay of flavors and textures.
Our Classic Italy sharing plate featured lemon arancini: three balls of pearl rice, deep-fried to a perfect shade of golden brown, drenched in a delicious cheese sauce and powdered with shavings of aged pecorino. The only problem with this excellent dish was that we wished there were more of it.
The main course
As a main course, we chose the only one that combined both fish and pasta: bisque gnocchi, a red drum steak served on a bed of potato dumplings in a very rich brown-butter bisque. The white sea fish was cooked to perfection, while the light, fluffy gnocchi pillows were superb.
A separate dessert menu listed four final courses (NIS 54-NIS 64), with more choices slated from the restaurant’s talented pastry chef. A mouthwatering chocolate creation combining mousse, fudge, and a cocoa tuile was sinfully decadent, while a large portion of that iconic Italian sweet – chiffony tiramisu – was a worthy finishing touch to a memorable meal.
Strangely for any Italian restaurant, no espresso is served. In fact, stranger still, no hot drinks are available. The only explanation given was that it was the owner’s decision.
Bella
10 Einstein St., Tel Aviv
Tel: 055-990-9804
Hours: Sun.-Thurs., 5 p.m.-midnight (Happy Hour 5-7 p.m.)
Friday, 12 p.m.-4 p.m. (Happy Hour)
Saturday, 9:30 p.m.-midnight
Kashrut: Dairy Mehadrin

Chocolate De Luxe brings handmade kosher luxury to Netanya - restaurant review

If you walk through the streets surrounding central Netanya’s Independence Square, you will come across Chocolate De Luxe, one of the city’s newest boutique chocolate stores and cafés, founded just over a year ago by entrepreneur Esther Arbov.
When I stepped inside, the first thing I noticed was the shop’s sleek modern design, immediately giving it the feel of a high-end boutique. Colorful displays of handcrafted chocolates, pralines, and other carefully curated creations lined the counters and display cases, making it difficult to know where to look first.
I was greeted by Arbov herself, who warmly welcomed me in, prepared a steaming cup of coffee, and brought out a selection of the shop’s signature creations: Dubai chocolate, Ferrero Rocher-style chocolate, and a slice of cheesecake topped with Lotus biscuit spread.
We took a seat outside alongside other customers as she began telling me about the business and the journey that brought her here.
Arbov, 57, an ultra-Orthodox woman from Tel Aviv, worked for many years as a kindergarten teacher before eventually opening and managing her own preschool. But five years ago, she decided it was time for a change.
A mother of seven and grandmother to 22 grandchildren, Arbov says she reached a point where she felt she had simply done enough in education. Yet retirement did not suit her either.
“I’m not someone who can just sit at home and do nothing,” she told the Magazine.
The influence of COVID-19
Around that same time, the COVID-19 pandemic had broken out, bringing everyday life to a standstill and leaving her with something she had not had in years: time.
As a mother and grandmother, Arbov always enjoyed cooking and hosting family gatherings. She started experimenting in the kitchen and soon developed an interest in artisanal chocolate-making.
Determined to master the craft, she enrolled in professional training with chocolatier Ronen Aflalo and quickly fell in love with the process.
She began testing and perfecting her recipes from home, producing handmade pralines that quickly became popular among family and friends.
But it was her son, who owns a restaurant of his own, who encouraged her to think bigger and consider opening a boutique store.
The response from early customers was immediate, and demand steadily grew. Encouraged by the enthusiasm surrounding her creations, Arbov eventually opened Chocolate De Luxe, combining handmade craftsmanship, premium ingredients, and strict mehadrin kosher certification.
The boutique is among the first in the region to offer premium handmade chocolates under Badatz Eda Haredit Jerusalem certification, catering to a market that Arbov says has long been underserved.
Limited luxury options in haredi community
While gourmet chocolate has become increasingly popular in Israel, luxury food products with high levels of kashrut remain relatively limited, particularly within the haredi community. Arbov saw an opportunity to fill that gap.
Arbov works together with fellow haredi chocolatier and shop manager Hannah Ohayon, whom she trained in the craft, and the duo now creates an extensive assortment of handmade Belgian chocolates on-site.
Their in-house production includes pralines, filled chocolate bars, truffles, gourmet chocolate bites, Ferrero Rocher-inspired confections, crisp layered chocolate bars, chocolate coins, and a variety of additional sweets.
The praline range is offered in dark chocolate (parve), milk chocolate, and sugar-free options. The dark chocolate pralines, made with 70% cocoa, come in flavors such as lemon, strawberry, mixed berry, coffee, nougat, hazelnut, walnut, and rum. Milk chocolate versions feature fillings such as pineapple, nougat, coffee, strawberry, lemon, vanilla, peanut butter, caramel, and walnut.
The boutique also produces specialty chocolate bars in varieties including classic dark chocolate, hazelnut, coffee, nougat, and the increasingly popular Dubai-style chocolate.
Prices range from NIS 81 for a nine-piece luxury praline box, NIS 20-45 for chocolate bars, NIS 35-72 for specialty chocolate snacks, and NIS 50-98 for premium Ferrero Rocher-style gift boxes.
Aside from the café, a major part of the business, Arbov explains, is its carefully curated gift packages designed for special occasions, which customers can preorder. According to Arbov, these gift boxes have become one of the shop’s main sources of income.
During my visit, I was pleased to receive one of these beautifully arranged boxes, filled with a selection of pralines, chocolate bars, and other handmade treats.
Arbov explained that many customers come searching for something unique for a loved one or a special event, whether it be a bar or bat mitzvah, birthday celebration, wedding gift, holiday package, or Shabbat hosting gift.
Customers can order custom arrangements that combine the boutique’s chocolates with luxury home accessories, premium textiles, kitchenware, and bottles of wine or specialty liqueurs, creating a gift experience that goes far beyond traditional confectionery.
The boutique is not just a shop, however. The venue also operates a café serving handmade desserts alongside premium coffee. Customers can choose from a range of pies, including apple, lemon, chocolate, and strawberry cheesecake, while custom mousse cakes and chocolate krembo cakes can be ordered in advance with personalized inscriptions for special occasions.
Arbov has also expanded the business by offering chocolate workshops for both children and adults, transforming a visit to the boutique into an experience rather than simply shopping.
For Arbov, the opening of Chocolate De Luxe represents far more than a business venture. After decades spent working in education, she has successfully reinvented herself, transforming a late-life career change into a thriving enterprise – proof that passion, creativity, and entrepreneurship can begin at any stage of life.
