AI company hit by hack entirely carried out by artificial intelligence
Hugging Face says it was able to repel the attack using its own AI systems
- Bookmark
- CommentsGo to comments
AI company Hugging Face says that it has been hit by a hack carried out entirely by an artificial intelligence system.
Artificial intelligence is increasingly used by hackers to automate the work of finding security failings or exploiting them. But the company said that the new attack went further, even being started by an AI tool: “it was driven, end to end, by an autonomous AI agent system”, it said.
The company was also able to fight against the attack using its own AI tools, it said. It was able to use a large language model to analyse the attacks and find how it had been able to happen, Hugging Face said.
Hugging Face is an AI company that makes tools that let developers host and share their AI tools. Using its services, developers and researchers can work on AI models as well as making them available for use.
It was through those tools that the AI was able to hack the company. Hugging Face said that a dataset was uploaded to its system that was then able to abuse a security vulnerability and run code on its own servers.
Because the attack was being conducted by and done using an AI, the system was “executing many thousands of individual actions” during the attack, it said. It is still unclear where the attack came from or what AI system was conducting it, Hugging Face said.
The company said it was still working to understand whether any of its customer’s data was stolen in the attack, but committed to sharing details with anyone affected. It urged users to check for any suspicious behaviour on their accounts in the meantime.
Hugging Face said that it initially spotted the attack using its own AI systems. Automated tools look through its security logs and look for anything suspicious, and it was then able to use separate AI systems to understand the attack after it had happened.

“This allowed us to reconstruct the timeline, extract indicators of compromise, map the credentials touched, and separate genuine impact from decoy activity,” it said. “Thanks to this approach, we were able to do in hours what would usually take days, and match the adversary's speed.”
Hugging Face did however note that its defensive work was constrained because it was unable to use the most powerful models, because of restrictions that are intended to keep them from being used for unsafe purposes. It said this led to an “asymmetry”, since “the attacker was bound by no usage policy, while our own forensic work was blocked by the guardrails of the hosted models we first tried”.

Moscow’s leaked media playbook: spin Ukraine’s refinery hits as an attack on ‘car owners’
Amid Ukrainian strikes on Russian oil refineries and Wildberries warehouses, Russia’s presidential administration advised state and pro-Kremlin media to persuade citizens that “rallying society around the President is the most important factor in the country’s stability.”
Overnight on July 18, Ukrainian forces struck Wildberries logistics centers in Elektrostal, outside Moscow, and in Kotovsk, in the Tambov region. Official figures put the death toll at eight, with about 80 injured across the two warehouses. Ukrainian drones also hit an oil depot in Noginsk, in the Moscow region.
It was one of the largest attacks on civilian infrastructure in Russia — yet state television ignored it.
Ukrainian President Volodymyr Zelensky later said the operation was a response to Russian military strikes on civilian infrastructure. On the morning of July 17, Russian drones struck a Nova Poshta sorting terminal in Sumy twice; no casualties were reported.
In the early hours of July 20, Ukrainian drones struck the Yuzhnyye Vorota industrial park in Domodedovo, outside Moscow, where a Wildberries warehouse is also located. Wildberries said the warehouse remained operational.
“The main purpose of such strikes is to demoralize people and try to use their anxiety against the country’s leadership and our army. Anxiety doesn’t reduce the likelihood of the conflict dragging on — it increases it: the enemy uses anxiety to gauge the effectiveness of its strikes,” read the guidance circulated to state media employees by the political bloc of Russia’s presidential administration.
According to the document, strikes on oil refineries were intended “to create difficulties for car owners,” while strikes on Wildberries warehouses were aimed “at damaging Russian small business.” The document described the strikes themselves as “PR attacks.”
To distract Russians from the Ukrainian strikes, the document’s authors urged editors and broadcasters to emphasize that “Vladimir Putin has effectively cut Ukraine off from the sea.”
They also recommended covering Russian military strikes on Ukrainian cities. The document urged pro-Kremlin outlets, for instance, to highlight that overnight on July 19, Russia carried out “the most massive ‘ballistic’ strike on Kyiv.” The strike allegedly destroyed munitions that “the enemy wanted to unleash on Russia.” The document also mentions unspecified “drone warehouses,” as well as “factories producing components for the ‘Flamingo’ and ‘Neptun-MD’ missiles.”

The format of the strikes shows that the Russian army aims to finish off Ukraine’s transportation and fuel system — dark times await the enemy’s logistics.

“A hard war is underway, but in it we are grinding down the enemy and have no intention of letting up. The enemy faces new strikes on Kyiv, the most powerful yet,” the document’s authors said.
