Mystery of missing mom-of-two Angelica Brophy who vanished after dropping daughter off at summer camp
Angelica Brophy ‘spent her life caring for others during their most difficult moments,’ a family statement read
- Bookmark
- CommentsGo to comments
A California mom-of-two has vanished after dropping her daughter off at summer camp more than a week ago. 
Investigators say Angelica Brophy visited a casino in Valley Center on July 6 before traveling to Palomar Mountain State Park later that day but has not been seen since. 
A huge effort to find the 44 year-old was launched, with the park and the surrounding area searched. On Tuesday, Oceanside Police Department said that its participation in a physical search for the mom was being paused, but her family is still waiting for answers.
“We are reaching the point where all available search and rescue resources have been utilized," the department told Fox 5/KUSI. 
The search so far has involved dive teams, the police department, the San Diego County Sheriff’s Department and community volunteers.
Below is everything we know so far.
Who is Angelica Brophy?
Brophy has been described as a dedicated mom-of-two and a hard-working ER nurse, as well as a keen hiker who had visited Palomar Mountain State Park previously.
The Oceanside mom works at Kaiser Permanente in San Marcos, where her colleague Ashley Seitz says she was known for bringing levity to tense moments while at work.
“She's just a goof ball,” Seitz told NBC7. “She's the goofball of the group. She is silly. She is fun.”
Brophy’s co-workers, her family and friends have all joined the effort to find her.
As the search continues, Brophy’s husband Brent has been caring for their children. Meanwhile, the couple’s friends have taken over talking to the media, Seitz told The U.S. Sun.
A vigil for Brophy was held last week at Lakeview Park in San Marcos, with one of her five brothers, Martin Fabregas, urging anyone with more information about the case to come forward.
A family statement read aloud at the vigil described Brophy as having “spent her life caring for others during their most difficult moments.”
Brophy’s last known movements
Seitz wrote on Facebook that the missing mom dropped her daughter off at a summer day camp in Oceanside at 9.30 a.m. on July 6. She was scheduled to work later at 7 p.m. in San Marcos. 
The Oceanside Police Department confirmed in a press release that Brophy was at Harrah’s Resort in Valley Center at around 3.30 p.m. that same day.
Around 50 minutes later, she entered Palomar Mountain State Park after buying a parking ticket from park staff.
During a press conference, Oceanside Police Chief Taurino Valdovinos confirmed that Brophy entered the park alone. She was not seen again.
The mom was reported missing at around 9.30 p.m., police say. 
Chief Valdovinos says that police have no evidence to suggest that foul play took place.
Clues as the search continues
Police say detectives pinged Brophy’s phone on July 8, but no information was returned. They believe the device is either turned off or is out of battery.
On July 9, Brophy’s vehicle was found in the Palomar Mountain State Park, although the mom was nowhere to be seen. 
Seitz confirmed on Facebook that the mom’s purse, wallet, driver’s license, passport, phone and cash were all discovered inside the vehicle. 
Brophy’s Apple Watch last pinged, near the car, which is how the vehicle was found, Seitz says. 
California State Park officers, supported by the San Diego County Sheriff’s Department’s Search and Rescue team, conducted searches on July 9, 10 and 11, police noted.
Dive team members with California State Parks and Port of San Diego Harbor Police searched a pond near Palomar Mountain State Park on July 12 and 13. The search ended with “negative results.”
Search efforts by the San Diego County Sheriff's Department Search and Rescue Team resumed on July 14. 
There are more questions to answer, her friends and family say
Seitz told The U.S. Sun that her friend often carried her passport with her.
However, Seitz says that it is “odd” that Brophy would have left her phone behind. 
“If she was going hiking, she’d normally have some kind of safety net,” she told the outlet. “Then again, maybe she’d had a bad day and wanted to clear her head…just leave everything behind and go for a walk.”
Rafael Fabregas, one of Brophy’s brothers, says that the family is considering “every possible outcome.”
The brother told NBC7: “She could be in need of rescue…she could be, you know, abducted.”
Rafael said that he does not believe that his sister would have caused harm to herself, though. 
The Independent has contacted the Oceanside Police Department for comment.

Students challenge Mahmood’s visa ban on overseas applicants
Study visas for people from Afghanistan, Cameroon, Myanmar and Sudan were halted in March by home secretary Shabana Mahmood, who claimed some were exploiting the asylum system
- Bookmark
- CommentsGo to comments
Three Sudanese students are challenging the Home Office’s ban on certain visas for foreign nationals from four countries in the courts, The Independent can reveal.
Home secretary Shabana Mahmood halted study visas for people from Afghanistan, Cameroon, Myanmar and Sudan in March, accusing some of exploiting the system by using it as a gateway to enter the UK before later claiming asylum.
The drastic measure drew criticism from the university sector, with the vice-chancellor of the University of Sussex Sasha Roseneil saying the move had profound consequences “for the UK’s global reputation and soft power”.
Now, three Sudanese women in their 20s, supported by law firm Duncan Lewis, are seeking to bring a legal challenge at the High Court to overturn the ban. They will argue that the way the Home Office selected the nationalities to ban is flawed, and that officials failed to properly investigate whether widespread visa abuse was occurring before they imposed a blanket ban.
Two of the women are doctors and one is a pharmacist. They had offers to study masters at Oxford University and University College London (UCL) in Digital Health, International Health and Tropical Medicine, and Public Health.
One of the women, who wanted to remain anonymous, said that the legal challenge was about ensuring “fairness”. She explained: “When I received my offer from Oxford, I saw it as an opportunity to gain the knowledge and skills needed to help rebuild health systems in a country long devastated by war.
“As a Sudanese doctor with an established international career, I have spent years working on reproductive health and supporting communities affected by displacement. The idea that I am now being treated as a potential abuser of the immigration system, simply because of my nationality, is deeply painful.”
She added: “Sudan has already lost universities, hospitals, and countless opportunities to this war. Many of us have lost friends, family members, and any sense of normality. Education is one of the few remaining pathways available to young Sudanese who want to contribute, lead, and help rebuild our country. This policy does not just affect visa applications; it risks taking away the future of a generation that has already lost so much.”
Announcing the ban in March, the Home Office said they needed to “protect UK border security from these unsustainable levels of visa-linked asylum claims”, saying that students who arrive in the UK were going on to claim asylum.
The Home Office estimated that the 18-month ban would impact 4,300 students from the four countries. In the year ending September 2025, there were 120 asylum claims from Sudanese nationals on study visas in the UK - compared to a total of 5,869 Sudanese asylum claimants in 2025.
The four countries affected by the ban made up just 0.7 per cent of study visas issued in 2025. Together visas granted to foreign nationals from Myanmar, Cameroon, Afghanistan and Sudan made up 3,142 visas out of the 429,254 study visas issued in 2025.
Manini Menon, solicitor at law firm Duncan Lewis, told The Independent: “Our clients are talented, high-achieving women who have secured admission to some of the top universities in the world.
“It is disappointing that the home secretary has decided that they should be deprived of the opportunity to take up their offers of study, solely on account of their nationality, without any assessment of the individual facts of their cases.
“This is particularly so in circumstances where our clients are all medical professionals working on issues of public health and sexual and reproductive rights (including eradication of the practice of FGM), all of which HM Government has recognised in its foreign policy as being key UK priorities in Sudan.”
Earlier this year, leading universities urged Ms Mahmood to exempt outstanding students from the ban. Some 34 institutions, represented by higher-education associations the Russell Group and ResearchPlus, called for an exception for Chevening scholars, who are part of a government-funded programme that enables outstanding emerging leaders from around the world to do a one-year master’s degree in the UK.
In June, the Home Office announced in a further crack down, saying universities would be stripped of the right to recruit international students if too many drop out over fears they are being exploited for visa abuse.
The claim brought by the three Sudanese women is due to be heard at the High Court at the beginning of next week.
The Home Office has been contacted for comment.
