Hong Kong tourist left in coma after quad bike accident in Japan
Woman, 69, was reportedly in a group taking a class on operating an all-terrain vehicle at a racetrack in northern Okinawa
An elderly Hong Kong tourist has been left in a coma after falling from a quad bike in Japan.
The Immigration Department said on Sunday that it had been in contact with the Chinese foreign ministry’s arm in Hong Kong and the Chinese consulate in Fukuoka to understand the situation after receiving a request for help from the woman’s family. It added that it was also providing support.
The accident reportedly occurred at around 11am on Saturday, when a group of six tourists – including the 69-year-old woman – were taking a class on operating an all-terrain vehicle (ATV) at an outdoor racetrack in Nago City, northern Okinawa.
The woman reportedly hit the throttle by accident, causing the vehicle to accelerate, lose balance and overturn.
She suffered a severe blow to the left side of her head and was bleeding heavily.
The woman was unconscious when airlifted to hospital and remains in a critical condition.

Ukrainian President Volodymyr Zelenskyy said he held another round of meetings with senior Ukrainian military commanders on 19 July, as reports continue to swirl that he is considering replacing Commander-in-Chief Oleksandr Syrskyi.
The meetings come amid growing speculation over the future of Ukraine's military leadership after former Defense Minister Mykhailo Fedorov publicly revealed disagreements with Syrskyi and said he had urged Zelensky to replace him. The reports have emerged alongside political tensions and protests across the country, intensifying scrutiny of the government's wartime leadership.
The discussions come after the Financial Times, citing a source in the Presidential Office, reported that Zelenskyy planned to meet military commanders over the weekend to hear battlefield assessments and interview potential successors to Syrskyi.
Zelenskyy meets three senior commanders
In a statement, Zelenskyy said he spoke with Joint Forces Commander Mykhailo Drapatyi, Deputy Chief of the General Staff Volodymyr Horbatiuk, and Air Assault Forces Commander Oleh Apostol.
"Each of them is well known in the army," Zelenskyy said. "They reported on the situation in the relevant sectors, and we also discussed the overall course of the war."
According to the president, the commanders briefed him on the situation in Sumy, Kharkiv, Luhansk, and Donetsk oblasts. They also discussed strengthening Ukraine's air defenses, fulfilling contracts for the production of all types of drones, and operations on the Oleksandrivsk direction involving the Air Assault Forces.
"It is important to assess our strategy going forward and Ukraine's proactive steps – how to force Russia to end this war with a dignified peace for Ukraine and guaranteed security," Zelenskyy said. "There are various components to this, and I want to hear how commanders at different levels across Ukraine's Defense and Security Forces see it."
He added that he would hold further "key talks" on 20 July.
Consultations follow reports of possible leadership changes
The latest meetings follow several days of consultations with commanders across Ukraine's military.
On 18 July, Zelenskyy said he spoke with the commanders of six army and Air Assault Forces corps fighting on some of the most active sectors of the front. Earlier, he also held discussions with Unmanned Systems Forces Commander Robert Brovdi, National Guard Azov Corps Commander Denys Prokopenko, National Guard Khartiia Corps Commander Ihor Obolenskyi, Air Assault Forces Deputy Commander Andrii Tkachuk, acting 19th Army Corps Commander Valerii Skred, and Third Army Corps Commander Andrii Biletskyi.
The broad consultations have attracted attention after recent reports suggested Zelenskyy was reviewing Ukraine's senior military leadership.
Former Defense Minister Minister Mykhailo Fedorov recently said he had clashed with Syrskyi over military leadership, claiming he had proposed replacing both the commander-in-chief and Chief of the General Staff Andrii Hnatov.
Ukrainska Pravda, however, cited sources who said the 18 July discussions with frontline commanders did not involve Syrskyi or his possible dismissal.
Neither Zelensky nor the Presidential Office has publicly confirmed that Syrskyi's position is under review.
Air Assault commander defends military leadership
The same day, Air Assault Forces Commander Oleh Apostol, one general with whom Zelenskyy met, published a lengthy statement defending the military chain of command and urging soldiers to stay out of political disputes.
Apostol, a career officer who has fought since 2014, said Ukraine's armed forces "were, are, and will remain outside politics" and stressed that commanders bear personal responsibility for the lives of their troops and the decisions they make on the battlefield.
"Where discipline ends, chaos begins. And chaos always works in the enemy's favor," he wrote.
Without naming anyone directly, Apostol criticized sweeping public attacks on Ukraine's senior military leadership by people who have never commanded troops in combat.
"Criticism can be substantive, professional, and well-founded. But humiliation, baseless accusations, and public insults against the honor and dignity of the highest military command are unacceptable," he wrote, warning that such attacks undermine trust in the armed forces and benefit Russia.
While Apostol did not mention Syrskyi by name, his statement appeared to respond to the growing public debate over the commander-in-chief's future.
Zelensky has publicly framed the meetings as part of broader consultations on battlefield conditions, military strategy, force requirements, and strengthening Ukraine's defense capabilities, rather than discussions about changes in military leadership.

‘He’s a war criminal’: Mamdani exploring whether he can arrest Netanyahu if he visits New York

New York City mayor Zohran Mamdani has said he was reviewing whether his administration could arrest Benjamin Netanyahu if as expected the Israeli prime minister visits the city for the UN general assembly in September.
Mamdani’s comments on Saturday’s episode of the New York Times’s the Interview podcast echoed ones he had made to the publication as he successfully ran for mayor last fall.
“I believe that prime minister Netanyahu belongs in The Hague,” Mamdani said, referring to the Dutch city that is home to the international criminal court, which issued an arrest warrant for Netanyahu in November 2024 over alleged war crimes in Gaza. “He’s a war criminal who has been charged by the international criminal court, and what you will find is that is an opinion that is held by many, purely because of what his actions have wrought over these past many years.”
Mamdani said it was unclear to him whether he has the legal authority to order the New York police department (NYPD), which he oversees, to detain a foreign leader like Netanyahu. But he said he was in “an active conversation” with the city’s law department about the matter.
“Whatever the law allows me to do in New York City, that’s what we will do,” Mamdani added. “But we won’t be writing our own laws to that end.”
The warrant which the ICC issued for the arrest of Netanyahu and other Israeli leaders accused them of war crimes and crimes against humanity in Gaza, including “starvation as a method of warfare, and … murder, persecution, and other inhumane acts”.
During his mayoral campaign, Mamdani characterized Israel’s military actions in Gaza as a genocide and Netanyahu as a war criminal, telling the New York Times in September that he would order NYPD to arrest Netanyahu in the event he ever set foot there. He also condemned the 7 October 2023 Hamas attacks on Israel as a “horrific war crime”.
The Times report back then cited legal experts who judged that having Netanyahu arrested “would be a practical impossibility” for Mamdani – and could bring him into direct conflict with the US’s federal government.
The US is not a party to the ICC and does not recognize its authority. And in February 2025, early in his presidency, Donald Trump ordered economic sanctions against the court, maintaining that it lacked jurisdiction over the US or Israel.
Trumps’s US secretary of state, Marco Rubio, then recently vowed to “dismantle” the court, accusing it of “waging a war against our country, not with bullets or missiles” but with “the force of so-called international law”.
Netanyahu, who faces a tough re-election campaign in October, brushed off Mamdani’s arrest threats in a recent radio appearance, saying he was not concerned.
Though Mamdani denies the accusation, Netanyahu also alleged Mamdani supports Hamas.
“He’s with the terror actors,” Netanyahu told New York’s WABC. “And I think the problem is that he doesn’t recognize and he doesn’t care that those who hate the Jews and Israel ultimately hate America.”
Israel’s ambassador to the UN, Danny Danon, accused Mamdani – New York’s first Muslim mayor – of failing to confront rising antisemitism in his city. Mamdani has countered such accusations by noting that he has increased funding for an office to combat hate crimes.
“Israeli Prime Minister Netanyahu will come to New York, address the United Nations General Assembly with pride, and stand before the world to state Israel’s truth and its unwavering right to defend its citizens,” Danon said.
Trump’s ambassador to the UN, Mike Waltz, also dismissed Mamdani’s threat as “pure political theater”, saying the US is not a member of the ICC.
World leaders from United Nations member-states are expected to travel to New York in September for the body’s annual session. Netanyahu has often participated in this event in previous years.
“We’re not talking about a personal assessment of Benjamin Netanyahu,” Mamdani told the Times’ podcast. “We’re talking about the international criminal court and the fact that they have a warrant out for this prime minister’s arrest, and I think it’s important to talk about the weight that that has as a charge and that also, as the mayor of New York City, I’ll be following the laws of New York City.”
Mamdani’s comments on Saturday came as his stance on Israel is becoming less fringe within the Democratic party.
Close to half of US House Democrats on Wednesday voted to strip $3.3bn in military aid to Israel. It was not enough to pass the measure. But it signified a buckling of unconditional support for Israel, a shift that is also reflected in public opinion polling.
Asked by the Times about the political weight he places on Israel and support for it by the US, Mamdani said ongoing war in Gaza was motivating voters throughout the country, including when his endorsed candidates swept House races in New York in June.
“It is hard to find a more bankrupt policy approach than what our country has done to Gaza and to Palestine,” he said.
