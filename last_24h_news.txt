Lindsey Graham’s sister officially announces candidacy for full term to fill late brother’s shoes
Darline Graham Nordone was appointed as her brother’s interim replacement last week after the senator died of an aortic tear
- Bookmark
- CommentsGo to comments
Darline Graham Nordone, the younger sister of the late senator Lindsey Graham, has officially announced she will run for a full six-year term to fill her brother’s shoes.
Graham, a South Carolina Republican and a close ally of President Donald Trump, died at the age of 71 on July 11. He died of an aortic tear due to cardiovascular disease shortly after returning from a trip to Ukraine.
In the wake of his death, a devastated Graham Nordone, whom the late senator raised after their parents died at a young age, agreed to serve as his replacement until next year, when the seat will be permanently filled.
Now, she tells Fox News that she will run in next month’s special primary election in hopes of continuing to serve the South Carolinians that her late brother represented for decades, telling Fox News, “I’m in.”
Graham Nordone’s announcement comes as Republican Representative Nancy Mace of South Carolina announced that she will not be running for Graham’s seat.
In a teaser from Graham Nordone’s interview with Fox News’ Sean Hannity airing Monday night, the network host asked about Trump’s request that she run for a full term.
“It is a tremendous amount of pressure,” Graham Nordone told Hannity in a clip posted to X Monday afternoon. "I have been praying a lot, talking to my family. But with the love of my family, with the support of his staff…I feel like I can do it. I feel an inner peace about it. Will it be difficult? Yes. Absolutely.”
When Hannity asked if she has made a final decision about the race, Graham Nordone said, “I’m in.”
On Friday, Trump urged Graham Nordone to run in the August 11 special primary, calling her “a spectacular person, and a true American Patriot.”
He said that during a recent visit to the Oval Office, he asked the interim senator to run for a full term.
“I hope Darline does this, in that there would be nobody better to honor the legacy of her beloved brother, Lindsey. Darline, who comes from an absolutely incredible family, has been a WINNER all of her life and, should she accept, has my Complete and Total Endorsement in the Special Election for U.S. Senate in South Carolina — SHE WILL NEVER LET YOU DOWN. RUN, DARLINE, RUN!” Trump wrote on Truth Social.
Graham Nordone was sworn into office Tuesday, a day after South Carolina Governor Henry McMaster announced at a press conference that he had appointed her to finish her late brother’s term.
“It is such a privilege to get to finish some of his important work, and I promise to work hard over the next several months to support the president and carry forward the efforts of my brother on behalf of the citizens of South Carolina and the United States,” Graham Nordone said at the time.
Mace wrote in an X post late Monday afternoon that she was not entering a bid for Graham’s seat because she has “unfinished business” in the House.
“I’m not running for the U.S. Senate, not because I’m backing down from a fight but because the one I’m already in matters right now,” the congresswoman said in a video attached to the post.
Mace’s decision comes after finishing fifth in the Republican primary for South Carolina governor last month.

Road deaths have declined globally since 1990 except in poorer countries, study finds
New estimates show road deaths are declining in the U.S. and globally
- Bookmark
Road deaths are declining in the U.S. and worldwide, but lower-income countries aren't experiencing much of the improvement, according to a study published Monday.
Global rates of road injuries and deaths each fell more than 30% between 1990 and 2023, according to the study published in Lancet Public Health, which noted the progress varied from country to country.
Pedestrian and vehicle occupant deaths rose over that time in six countries — including the United States. However, more recent data shows road deaths fell in the U.S. in 2024 and 2025.
The World Health Organization put out its own numbers Monday, finding that road traffic deaths declined by 21% between 2011 and 2025. However, traffic crashes still claimed more than 1.1 million lives last year, and they remain the leading cause of death among children and youth aged 5 to 29 years, the WHO said.
Road deaths involve fatal injuries that happen to pedestrians, cyclists and people riding in cars, trucks and other vehicles. Complete data is lacking in some countries, so the researchers behind the new report made estimates based on what data is available.
The researchers, from the University of Washington’s Institute for Health Metrics and Evaluation, found that road injury rates are higher in high-income countries, but 90% of road deaths occur in low-income countries.
In many low-income countries, safety measures have not kept up with rapid growth in cities and use of motorized vehicles. For example, seat belt mandates are more common in high-income countries, as are car safety features such as air bags.
“Road injury outcomes are shaped not only by crashes, but by the systems designed to prevent them and protect people when they occur,” said Liane Ong, the new paper's senior author, in a statement.
However, there have been conflicting trends in the U.S., including the proliferation of taller and larger passenger vehicles, the authors wrote. U.S. officials also have said thousands of deaths each year can be attributed to “distracted driving," including drivers looking at their phones.
In the U.S., motor vehicle traffic fatalities rose in the last decade and peaked at more than 43,000 in 2021 before beginning to decline, according to the National Highway Traffic Safety Administration. The agency estimated the count fell to about 36,600 last year.
Meanwhile, the Governors Highway Safety Association recently reported that U.S. drivers hit and killed 7% fewer people walking on U.S. roads in 2025 compared with the year before. It was the largest annual reduction since pedestrian deaths began falling three years ago, but it was still higher than what the numbers were before the COVID-19 pandemic.
The WHO identified road safety as an international public health priority more than two decades ago. The Lancet report's release coincides with the start of a two-day United Nations high-level meeting on improving global road safety, being held in New York City.

Robbie Williams addresses World Cup final TV incident after white object falls from mouth
The s later took to social media to share a video explaining the mysterious item, clarifying the nature of the unexpected moment
- Bookmark
Robbie Williams has addressed a "deeply unprofessional" on-screen gaffe during the World Cup final in which he accidentally spat a small white object onto his microphone.
The 52-year-old singer, who had performed alongside Nicole Scherzinger and Laura Pausini at the closing ceremony on Sunday, drew attention during an appearance on German TV channel Magenta Sport.
While speaking to pundits, the object emerged, which he then picked up, held, and subsequently rubbed onto his head, where it stuck to his forehead.
The incident quickly garnered attention. Williams later took to social media to share a video explaining the mysterious item, clarifying the nature of the unexpected moment.
Filming himself lying in bed, the performer says: “I’d just like to apologise for the object that fell on to the microphone yesterday.
“I was deeply unprofessional, and people worried about me.”
“But it’s OK. Because I had several more,” he continues, before sticking his tongue out and revealing two circular mints.
Williams breaks out laughing before the video cuts off.
Captioning the Instagram post, he wrote: “Just woke up and feeling…. Mint x.”
On Sunday night, the first World Cup half-time show was also held.
The final between Argentina and Spain featured an 11-minute spectacle with stars from around the world including Madonna, Shakira, BTS and Justin Bieber.
