The foreign ministers of eight countries -- Türkiye, the United Arab Emirates, Indonesia, Qatar, Egypt, Pakistan, Saudi Arabia, and Jordan -- on Thursday strongly condemned the mass incursions into Al-Aqsa Mosque/Al-Haram Al-Sharif by Israeli occupiers, led by extremist Israeli Cabinet ministers and carried out under the protection of Israeli security forces.
"The foreign ministers of Türkiye, the UAE, Indonesia, Qatar, Egypt, Pakistan, Saudi Arabia, and Jordan strongly condemn Israel's escalation at Al-Aqsa Mosque/Al-Haram Al-Sharif, particularly the continued mass incursions by settlers led by extremist Israeli ministers and protected by Israeli security forces, the raising of the Israeli flag within the courtyards of Al-Aqsa Mosque/Al-Haram Al-Sharif, the establishment of two tents by Israeli police, and all other provocative actions," they said in a joint statement.
The foreign ministers underlined that these "provocative and unacceptable actions" constitute a clear violation of international law, relevant UN resolutions, and the historical and legal status quo governing the holy sites in occupied East Jerusalem.
They also strongly condemned the illegal and extremist provocations, calls for incursions, and acts of violence carried out by extremist Israeli Cabinet ministers and groups.
The statement warned that such provocative actions fuel hatred and extremism and undermine efforts to achieve a just and lasting peace based on the two-state solution.
The foreign ministers further stated that restrictions on access to Jerusalem's Old City and its places of worship, as well as discriminatory and arbitrary limitations on access to other religious sites in the Old City, constitute clear violations of international law, including international humanitarian law, and represent unlawful attempts to alter the historical and legal status quo.
The statement reiterated that Israel has no sovereignty over occupied Jerusalem or its Muslim and Christian holy sites. It condemned what it described as Israel's systematic violations and practices, as the occupying power, aimed at altering the historical, legal, and demographic character of occupied East Jerusalem and undermining the sanctity and status of its Muslim and Christian holy sites.
The foreign ministers reaffirmed their categorical rejection of any attempts to alter the historical and legal status quo of Jerusalem and its Muslim and Christian holy sites.
They also underscored the importance of preserving this status quo, while recognizing the special role of the Hashemite Custodianship.
The joint statement further reaffirmed that the 144-dunum (144-hectare) compound of Al-Aqsa Mosque/Al-Haram Al-Sharif is an exclusive place of worship for Muslims, and that the Jerusalem Awqaf and Al-Aqsa Affairs Department, operating under Jordan's Ministry of Awqaf and Islamic Affairs, is the sole legal authority responsible for administering the site and regulating access to it.
The foreign ministers called on Israel, as "the occupying power," to immediately lift restrictions on access to Jerusalem's Old City and end measures preventing Muslims from reaching Al-Aqsa Mosque.
They also urged the international community to adopt a firm stance compelling Israel to halt its ongoing violations and unlawful practices against Muslim and Christian holy sites in Jerusalem and to cease actions that infringe upon their sanctity.
The statement came after another incursion into the Al-Aqsa Mosque compound by Israel's extremist National Security Minister Itamar Ben-Gvir, who entered the site under police protection accompanied by groups of Jewish activists in occupied East Jerusalem.

Israel signals possible military action against Iran amid uncertainty over US stance

Israeli officials signaled Thursday that the country is preparing for the possibility of renewed military action against Iran, as security authorities raised alert levels amid continued hostilities between Tehran and Washington.
According to Israel’s public broadcaster KAN, Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu will convene his Security Cabinet on Friday to discuss military preparedness in light of what it described as a possible escalation over the weekend.
Israeli media reported that the country’s security establishment has increased its level of readiness amid concerns that regional tensions could rapidly intensify.
Defense Minister Israel Katz said during security consultations, cited by the Yedioth Ahronoth daily, that Israel was “prepared for all scenarios.”
He warned that Iran would receive a “devastating blow” if it launched an attack against Israel.
Energy Minister Eli Cohen also urged readiness for all possible scenarios, saying the regional situation required heightened vigilance.
“We see what is happening in the region. The United States is attacking Tehran, and Iran is attacking countries in the region. This requires vigilance and raising the level of alert,” Cohen told Army Radio.
“If Iran acts against Israel, we will respond forcefully. We have significant intelligence and offensive capabilities,” he added.
Western intelligence assessments
Alongside the official statements, Yedioth Ahronoth cited what it described as Western intelligence experts as saying Iran could attempt to broaden the confrontation by drawing Israel into the ongoing conflict.
According to the newspaper, the assessment is based on Iran’s belief that differences exist between Washington and Tel Aviv over how to manage the crisis, which Tehran could seek to exploit.
Likud lawmaker Moshe Saada further fueled speculations by telling Israel’s Channel 14 that Israel was “preparing to launch an attack on Iran, perhaps as early as this weekend.”
However, officials from Netanyahu’s Likud party quickly downplayed the remarks, describing them as Saada’s personal assessment rather than information based on official briefings.
Yedioth Ahronoth quoted Israeli officials as saying Saada had not participated in any recent security consultations and that his comments were based on media reports. Netanyahu’s office also expressed dissatisfaction with the remarks, according to the newspaper.
Trump’s decision
Despite the rhetoric, Israeli officials indicated that major developments would largely depend on decisions in Washington.
“The key to developments in the coming days remains in the hands of US President Donald Trump,” an unnamed Israeli official told Yedioth Ahronoth.
The official said the US administration was continuing to pressure Iran by maintaining the threat of military escalation while simultaneously attempting to push Tehran back to negotiations.
The official added that Israel was not expected to initiate an attack unless it came under direct Iranian strikes, although Washington could later request Israeli support or participation.
Regional tensions have escalated over the past two weeks after Trump announced on July 8 that the ceasefire outlined in the June 18 US-Iran memorandum of understanding was no longer in effect.
Since then, the US and Iran have exchanged strikes, with Washington targeting sites inside Iran while Tehran says it has attacked US military facilities and equipment across several countries in the region.
The confrontation has disrupted shipping through the Strait of Hormuz, threatened global energy supplies and prompted intermittent airspace closures and growing warnings of attacks on US facilities and interests beyond the Middle East.

Frustrated Thune tells Trump to ‘get on the phone and get the votes’ after Leavitt swipes at Senate over voter ID
White House escalates its issues with Majority Leader John Thune after declaring that the Senate is ‘where you send things when you want them to die’
- Bookmark
- CommentsGo to comments
Senior Republican senators vented their frustrations at the White House on Thursday after Press Secretary Karoline Leavitt called on the Senate to “do its job” at her second press briefing since returning from maternity leave.
The press secretary’s rebuke of the upper chamber of Congress came in response to a reporter’s question regarding whether President Donald Trump was losing his patience with Senate Majority Leader John Thune as the days ticked down to the August recess with no signs of progress being made towards the passage of the SAVE America Act, Trump’s signature piece of voter ID legislation.
The Senate is due to leave town next week and, as it stands, has no plans to put the SAVE America Act on the floor — it lacks the support of a handful of Republican members, and would require six Democrats to break ranks for it to withstand a filibuster. The Democratic caucus is unified against the legislation.
Leavitt told reporters on Thursday that Trump’s “patience is running out” as it pertains to voter ID’s path to becoming law on the Hill, adding: “The House did its job; the Senate needs to do its job, too.” Her remarks were retweeted by the White House’s rapid response X account.
Thune fired back shortly afterwards in a gaggle with reporters on Capitol Hill: “She or somebody else ought to get on the phone and get the votes, right?”
“Instead of pointing the finger at Republicans, they might think about going after the people who are stopping it on the floor, which is the Democrats. And if there are Republicans that they think are gettable, get on the phone,” said the majority leader. “Let’s get them to yes.”
Other senators were just as frank in their assessments of why the legislation was not moving forward on Thurdsay.
John Cornyn echoed Thune’s response to Leavitt, telling reporters: “We just don't have the votes...there's nothing that Senator Thune can do to change that.”
Another senator in the Republican caucus who has called the SAVE America Act and its various other iterations “fundamentally flawed” argued that the White House was misplacing its anger altogether.
“He’s not responsible for the SAVE Act not passing. People like me are,” said Sen. Thom Tillis, according to Punchbowl. “But he gets criticized for it. And you can see some opportunistic Republican candidate using that to build a wedge. So I think he’s just showing some wisdom.”
“There’s no way on God’s green earth it can be implemented by this election.”
But the White House is showing no signs of letting up the pressure on Thune, who they clearly believe has the power to unify his caucus around a push to scrap the legislative filibuster and pass the SAVE America Act on a party-line vote. The president pushed for the act’s passage during a primetime speech from the White House last week, and on Wednesday he took aim at the Senate directly.
“It’s like the Senate is where you send things you want them to die,” said Trump as he campaigned for a right-winger, Mike Collins in the Georgia Senate race.
“Mike Johnson and Congress have done a great job, but we’re having a hard time in the Senate,” the president added. “The senate, what they’re doing, it’s just not right.”
It’s unclear how far Trump will take his war of words with Senate leadership if Thune, as expected, allows Trump’s arbitrary deadline of the August recess next week pass without significant progress on the voter ID bill. The Senate is already talking about pushing a planned third reconciliation package past the November election, making that an exceedingly unlikely vehicle for its passage and one Trump would likely oppose given his desire to see the act begin to take effect before the midterms.
The president has already played chicken with Congress once over the bill, refusing to sign a housing affordability bill that became law without his signature regardless. There are few other pieces of legislation the president could theoretically hold hostage until his desired voter ID bill makes it to his desk.
More than likely the president’s next course of action will be more of the same: namely, rhetoric and actions that make the Senate GOP leadership’s job of holding together its slim majority an even tougher prospect this fall.
