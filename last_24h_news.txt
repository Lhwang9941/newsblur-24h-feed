Jake Weatherald has been included in Australia's 13-man squad for the two-Test series against Bangladesh.
Australia has also recalled skipper Pat Cummins and veteran duo Josh Hazlewood and Nathan Lyon after their Ashes campaigns were ruined by injury.
What's next?
Australia is yet to settle on a playing XI for the first Test against Bangladesh in Darwin, which begins on August 13. ABC Sport will live blog the entire match.
Opening batsman Jake Weatherald has retained his spot in Australia's Test squad for the looming series against Bangladesh.
Despite a middling Ashes campaign last summer, Weatherald is among a 13-man squad bolstered by the return of a trio of big guns.
Captain Pat Cummins, paceman Josh Hazlewood and spinner Nathan Lyon all return after recovering from injury.
"They have worked extremely hard, individually and with the [fitness] teams, over the past few months to return from their respective injuries," Australia's chairman of selectors George Bailey said.
"While this is a 13-player squad, we have players prepared and available should any adjustments be required in the lead-up to these matches.
"The volume of cricket and schedule across the next 12 months are likely to present further opportunities across all formats for many players."
Missing from Australia's squad for the final Test against England last January are Usman Khawaja (retired), Michael Neser, Todd Murphy and Brendan Doggett.
The Australians host Bangladesh in Darwin from August 13-17 and Mackay from August 22-26, the start of a gruelling 12 months featuring 20 Test matches.
Weatherald will resume his opening batting partnership with Travis Head after playing all five Tests last summer against England.
The Darwin-born left-hander made only 201 runs at an average of 22.33 but has been given another chance to enhance his Test credentials.

Trump slaps new tariffs on Canadian imports
- President Donald Trump has imposed steep 50 percent tariffs on a range of Canadian imports, including autos, alcohol and dairy products, set to go into effect in 30 days.
- The tariffs were enacted through three proclamations under Section 338 of the 1930 Trade Act, with Trump citing "continued discrimination" against American industries and Canada's retaliation to previous U.S. tariffs.
- This aggressive move threatens significant economic instability, potential inflation and a wider trade war, prompting calls from Canadian officials for reciprocal tariffs if the measures proceed.
- The tariffs carry serious political and economic risks for Trump ahead of the November elections, especially given past Supreme Court rulings against his tariff authority and concerns about inflation.
- The action further strains the relationship between the two nations, with Trump also requesting additional tariffs over Canadian wildfires affecting U.S. air quality and a frosty relationship with Canadian Prime Minister Mark Carney.
