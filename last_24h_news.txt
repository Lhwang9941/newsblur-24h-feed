Madonna takes over Times Square for surprise Pride concert
The event, which was streamed on YouTube, was in anticipation of Madonna’s new album ‘Confessions II’
- Bookmark
- CommentsGo to comments
Madonna kicked off Pride Month with a surprise performance in New York’s iconic Times Square, treating fans to a preview of her forthcoming album, Confessions II.
The event, announced just a day prior, was streamed live on YouTube, building anticipation for the sequel to her acclaimed 2005 record, Confessions on a Dance Floor.
During the six-song set, the 67-year-old pop icon performed hits from her Grammy-winning 2005 album alongside new tracks. Addressing the crowd, she declared, "Hi, gays," before launching into the show.
The performance was a vibrant spectacle, featuring flashing rainbow lights and screens displaying images of LGBT+ trailblazers, including activist Marsha P Johnson, artist Keith Haring, and photographer Robert Mapplethorpe.
The setlist included fan favourites such as Hung Up, Get Together, and I Love New York, alongside new material like I Feel So Free, Bring Your Love, and Love Sensation from Confessions II. Bring Your Love, which features Sabrina Carpenter, was released following Madonna’s surprise appearance at Carpenter’s Coachella set in April.
True to form, the performance included several steamy moments, with Madonna straddling a speaker on a rotating stage and draping a leg over a safety barricade.
This event is part of Madonna’s ongoing partnership with LGBT+ dating app Grindr, which began in April and has included exclusive merchandise. Last month, Madonna described her upcoming album as a "love letter to dance music and to all the spaces and communities that create it or enjoy it."
She added on Instagram: "The dance floor isn’t about a specific space or building, it’s wherever we gather to dance, celebrate and connect. Thank you for being a part of this moment and for supporting my music. See you on the Dance Floor."
Teasing her new album in April, Madonna said that it’s best summed up with the first few lines of her song, “One Step Away”, adding: “People think that dance music is superficial, but they’ve got it all wrong. The dance floor is not just a place, it’s a threshold: A ritualistic space where movement replaces language.”
She continued: “When Stuart Price and I first started working on this record, this was our manifesto: ‘We must dance, celebrate, and pray with our bodies. These are things that we’ve been doing for thousands of years — they really are spiritual practices. After all, the dance floor is a ritualistic space. It’s a place where you connect.
“’With your wounds, with your fragility. To rave is an art. It's about pushing your limits and connecting to a community of like-minded people.’”
Confessions II is set for release on 3

Giddy up! Taylor Swift fans go buck wild for her return to country on Toy Story 5 song
Pop superstar revisits her Nashville roots on new track
- Bookmark
- CommentsGo to comments
Taylor Swift has said creating a new song for Toy Story 5 was a “new challenge” – but that it ultimately felt like “coming home”.
To the delight of both her fans and Pixar’s, the pop superstar released “I Knew It, I Knew You” on Friday 5 June. A joyous country-pop ditty about love, the song was written for Jessie, the rough-and-tumble cowgirl character voiced by Hollywood icon Joan Cusack.
Swift, 36, announced the track’s release in an emotional post on Instagram, in which she shared a home video of herself as a young girl dressed up in a red and white cowgirl outfit.
“Writing this song felt like a musical departure and coming home at the same time,” she began the post, in a reference to her origins as a country artist with early hits like “Tim McGraw” and “Mean” using a distinct southern twang.
The latter won the Grammy for Best Country Song in 2012. Swift, who co-wrote the track with her frequent collaborator and Bleachers frontman Jack Antonoff, continued: “Creating something for Jessie was a new challenge and also felt like second nature all at once.
“And being a @toystory kid from the age of 5 til now... is an adventure I plan to be on, to infinity and beyond.”
Swift went on to thank the “brilliant” Toy Story creator Andrew Stanton for involving her in the forthcoming film, as well as the “incomparable” Randy Newman “for the gorgeous sonic tapestry of songs and scores you've meticulously woven over the years”.
“You created the Toy Story musical world, and we are lucky to get to live in it,” Swift said of Newman, the legendary songwriter behind such indelible Toy Story hits as “You've Got A Friend In Me” and “We Belong Together”.
“We wrote this with so much adoration for these characters that made us laugh and helped us learn lessons and think outside the backyard all throughout our childhoods,” she concluded.
Swift fans already can’t get enough of the track, with one writing: “This song is going triple platinum in my house rn.” Another celebrated her return to her country roots, writing on X: “Country Taylor is ALIVE AND WELL.”
“Childhood me is crying happy tears watching baby Taylor in that cowboy hat — Country Tay is BACK and Jessie finally has her perfect anthem!” a third said. “To infinity and beyond with this one.”
The release follows weeks of speculation surrounding Swift’s involvement in the animated sequel, which ramped up last weekend when mystery promotional billboards appeared in the US featuring her initials.
The billboard, stylised in the instantly recognisable Toy Story manner, featured the giant letters “TS” together with 13 clouds – known to be Swift’s favourite number – sending her fans into a frenzy.
Swift confirmed the rumours herself in an Instagram post shared on Tuesday (2 June). “You knew it!” the 14-time Grammy award-winning artist told her fans.
Set for theatrical release on 19 June, the new instalment of the Toy Story saga focuses on technology’s encroachment into childhood as it follows young Bonnie, in thrall to her new Lilipad tablet.
Greta Lee, Alan Cumming, Conan O’Brien and musician Bad Bunny join the all-star cast, with Tom Hanks, Tim Allen, and Cusack reprising their roles as Buzz Lightyear, Woody, and Jessie, respectively.

Ásta Fanney Is Just Beginning: The Oracle Within
Unknown
Unknown Publisher
2026-06-05
10:43:53 UTC
19:43:53 KST

The Venice Biennale has been called the Olympics of the art world. Every two years, artists, curators, collectors and journalists descend on Venice for months of exhibitions, openings and events spread across national pavilions and historic venues. This year, Iceland is represented by Pocket Universe, an evolving exhibition by artist Ásta Fanney Sigurðardóttir.
Poet, performer, musician, filmmaker, visual artist and a possible inventor, Ásta has spent much of the last decade building a body of work that moves freely between disciplines. Her projects often combine poetry, performance, sound, video and participation, drawing audiences into worlds where mythology, humour, coincidence and imagination coexist.
Pocket Universe continues that approach. Combining video, sculpture, sound and performance, the exhibition explores imagination, uncertainty and transformation. Visitors encounter wandering creatures, invented games, acts of collective participation and recurring symbols that seem to belong to the same evolving mythology.
This year’s Biennale unfolds against a backdrop of ongoing debates about politics, conflict and national representation. When I ask Ásta how she feels now that the opening has taken place, her focus is elsewhere. The exhibition, she says, is not fixed, perhaps has no final end. “The exhibition is in fact changing the whole time,” she says. “I view this as the first reveal, the first chapter in a book.”
For many artists, an opening marks the end of a process. For Ásta, it is only the beginning.
“So the opening happened, but there are multiple openings,” she says. “The art is never over, it just continues.” She describes Pocket Universe as a kind of treasure hunt. “And the people that came for the May opening are just getting the first clue.”
When asked how she would describe her artistic world, she begins not with medium or discipline, but energy.
“My work is very fluid,” she says. “It revolves around an energy circuit, using words to bring forth a change in perspective, or make room for something that wasn’t there before.” She compares the process to poetry. “It’s listening to the oracle within.”
Softness is another idea she returns to repeatedly. “Some people think that showing softness is weak,” she says. “But I believe it’s the way to save the world.”
Then she laughs. “There is so much power in optimism, cynicism doesn’t work, it’s like trying to clean mud with mud.”
The longer we talk, the more certain ideas begin to recur: play, transformation, imagination and the possibility of seeing the world differently. Yet for all the angels, invented games and wandering creatures that populate her work, the roots of Ásta’s artistic universe turn out to be surprisingly concrete.
Every day is a button party
When I ask her about her childhood, Ásta Fanney begins with a bucket of buttons.
“My mum had this sewing room in our house, it was full of all sorts of things, garments, pins, buttons, a whole basket of buttons from generations before! They were all mixed up there, different times were blended together into one. I really love that I could see the brand-new ones next to the ancient ones. Seems so surreal. It’s like a treasure. I remember little me sitting on the floor and going through these maternal treasures of buttons, all of them a single button that didn’t manage to enter into a garment.”
What fascinated her was the strange community the buttons seemed to form. “A bit like a bunch of outcasts I guess, but finding a home in this strange button bucket with all the other weirdos and they are just having fun in there, every day is a button party day in that bucket, “ she laughs.
“I like art like that. This strangeness that doesn’t really have a place in the practical world and becomes its own thing somehow and has the capacity to be all-encompassing, but unexpected things happen around it. There are so many things, magical, coincidental, unbelievable things that can happen if you tap into it. I think art is one portal perhaps.”
Her mother, she says, was endlessly resourceful. “My mother is also a very practical and pragmatic person, but she has the ability to make a feast or a ‘veisla’ out of nothing. So she would find games or things to make or do out of something she would find at home. She is very resourceful, and I think I get that from her. But it’s this mentality of creating something from nothing, and that it is just possible.”
Her father was equally creative. “My father was a carpenter. He was this character that always was up for making things, he made swords for us and tiny cabins. Our garage was filled with all sorts of things, he built our house and was always doing something, building somewhere there.”
So the family home was rarely quiet, she says. “Our place was actually constantly under construction. I didn’t really understand homes where everything was super still and perfect. I didn’t understand why they would choose to have everything so boring.”
Looking back, she sees the connection. “In our home there was always something changing which I like and I think it is an extremely good quality. It embraces that life is always this ever-changing stream. It definitely had an impact on my art. I don’t really like it when things are too perfect and closed. I just like them when they move something in you, remind you of how human you are.”
Growing up, however, art itself didn’t feel particularly accessible. “Where I grew up there was mainly just football and a limited amount of culture present.” So the idea of becoming an artist seemed distant.
“It’s funny growing up in a place where you really have to dig for the keys to understand or find art or places that you didn’t really think existed, a way of life you never would have thought possible.”
Yet she remembers knowing surprisingly early what she wanted to do.
“I had a long list of things. I wanted to be an artist and poet, to make films and plays and music, to be a composer. But first and foremost to be an inventor, to bring a different idea to the world.” Looking back, it is hard not to hear echoes of the artist she would become. She was constantly drawing, she says, writing stories and songs.
“I remember doing monologues in drama class in school and I brought my own poetry to read in a kind of strange way and I thought maybe I had done something wrong because people didn’t really know what to think. I didn’t even realise there was such a thing called performance art, or even poetry performances.”
Her memories of Reykjavík’s art scene are equally vivid. “Intimate and chaotic sounds about right,” she laughs at my own description. “I think I understand chaos quite well, both thriving in it but also making order out of it in a very random way. Not only making room for coincidences but making them your stepping stones across the river.”
Pocket Universe
If the button bucket explains part of the artist, Pocket Universe feels like a natural extension of that worldview. The exhibition emerged during a period when Ásta herself was thinking about uncertainty. The ideas that would eventually become the exhibition, she says, arrived naturally.
“They emerged naturally from what I was doing, cause I was myself in this space of uncertainty and realised perhaps the world was as well.”
That led her to think about the things people use to move forward without certainty. “And I started looking at all these things that can change perspectives, what we use to be able to go forward in times of uncertainty, how openness can actually bring forth so much listening.”
“When I was making the video work there were so many coincidences,” she says, explaining some of her research. “I was reading all these different myths, folktales and cosmologies from around the world, looking to blend together things that are in common to make one universal tale, taking these first sentences from stories of how the world was created, how to make a new world.”
From that process emerged Creature Zero.
“This is a character wandering around the world looking for something.”
What exactly it is looking for remains deliberately open.
“Perhaps it is looking for the origin, looking to reconnect to the ancient wisdom of nature or tales, of language written in stones or inside mountains or hidden meaning or the heart of the earth. Perhaps it is looking for its tail to swing to the heavens, perhaps it is looking for the hole or a portal in its hat although the hole is and always has been there or looking for a square one, even square zero. But you never know, maybe there are things that will be revealed about this creature and the universal tale.”
The exhibition itself continues to evolve throughout its run. Asked whether there was a moment when Venice suddenly felt real, Ásta shakes her head.
“Oh not yet. I think because the Pocket Universe is a bit different from the rest of the exhibitions because it changes over time, not just growing but shapeshifting.”
The opening, she says, was only the beginning.
“The opening was not the final thing, it was the first thing, so people were looking at a beginning in a way.”
The New Luck Museum
One of the works that has generated some of the strongest reactions from visitors is The New Luck Museum, where visitors are invited to create new luck by drawing an amulet.
“It’s the most simple thing, almost on the cusp of being way too easy and cheesy, but I love it.”
Ásta says people often respond emotionally. “I think because it comes from a very sincere place, people are generally moved by it, this silly moment of everyone making new luck turns out to be such a beautiful moment.”
Watching strangers participate has become one of her favourite parts of the Biennale.
“I feel sometimes there is a portal opening when people are making these. It’s so simple it’s hilarious, but super effective. I think every single person that takes part walks out with a smile.”
Like much of Ásta’s work, The New Luck Museum sits somewhere between a game, a ritual, and an artwork. Several works in Pocket Universe draw on ancient board games.
“In several art pieces I am taking apart ancient board games, the forms and the shapes, and working with them as sculptures to be activated in a performance. I am looking at old systems and dismantling them and making a new game, mixing games together even and making a different one.”
The idea, she says, is not to reject the past but to reshape it.
“It’s a bit the same as the buttons in the bucket, the ancient next to the new, getting rid of some rules from the past but also taking wisdom we need to create something new for the future.”
Flags are a funny game
The Venice Biennale remains organised around national pavilions. Asked whether representing Iceland felt strange for an artist who often questions categories and borders, Ásta laughs.
“Flags are such a funny game, and so are politics. There are treasures in the time, from a culture that one can show the beauty of, in the land and nature and you can share that with the rest of the world.”
Pocket Universe feels noticeably different from many Biennale exhibitions. Asked whether she was consciously pushing against spectacle, Ásta agrees. “It was a very precise decision to go against the spectacle scale, to go against what you know will get attention and what people want to see.” Instead, she focused on smaller gestures.
“I wanted to focus on the small and the soft, the connection between things and people, the simple important things, and how to tap into an inner world, to listen to the quiet voice when there is so much noise.” She adds, “Why don’t I just do something completely outrageous, showing beauty and softness that melts things, showing optimism amidst the most tumultuous time of the century? Bringing forth a heart in a palm in all this dictator war confusion.”
Spicy splendid
Before arriving in Venice, Ásta described the Biennale as something between summer camp and the hardest obstacle course of her life. Now that she has spent time there, she thinks that may have been an understatement.
“Oh, I don’t think I realised how spicy it actually is.” Fortunately, she seems comfortable with that. “Luckily I am quite adaptable so it’s all going splendid, spicy splendid.” Venice itself has been both exhausting and rewarding, she says.
“It’s chaotic in a way I didn’t expect, but it’s also surprising in a way I didn’t realise. All the guests that come that I interact with, it’s been such a rewarding experience.”
One of the more unusual works in the pavilion centres around an empty chair. “I have asked or ‘hired’ my angel to have a performance there during the course of the Biennale.”
Whether visitors encounter that performance depends largely on what they choose to believe. “Perhaps the angel is there doing a performance? It depends on what you believe, what you see in your mind’s eye.” I mention a phrase from an earlier interview, where Ásta described artistic creation as a form of listening, or even “eavesdropping on the future.”
She laughs. “Eavesdropping on the future! Wow, I don’t remember saying that but I agree with myself 100 percent.” For her, creativity often feels less like invention than reception, she explains. “I think making art is speaking with the oracle tongue, you are drinking invisible star water.”
Then she laughs again. “Maybe that is what I have been doing in Venice for so long, I am slow cooking the old rules.”
For all the emphasis on intuition, openness and uncertainty, Pocket Universe is also carefully constructed. I mention that one of the things that struck me while working on this article was the remarkable level of finish present throughout Ásta’s work. Ásta sees these as two different layers of the same work.
“The editing, the careful construction and attention to detail is a part of the presentation, like a plate under the dish or clothes on a person. But the real art is the food, the person, the thing that is alive and will change or disappear. The work that you see is just a ladder into that space.”
Then she laughs.
“Talking about forks, I actually have always wanted to make or design cutlery. Perhaps I should do that one day.”
Reykjavik
Over the course of writing this interview, our conversations have taken place in person, during a weekend stop in Reykjavík between Venice commitments, over the phone from the countryside while Ásta chatted with a young niece at the same time, even by text at midnight from a party. The settings changed, but she somehow always found time for another question, another clarification, another story. Sitting across from me in a Vesturbær café, with rosy pink hair, glitter dusted across her eyelids and hologram nails catching the light, she seems perfectly at home in a universe populated by angels and wandering creatures.
When the idea of a cover shoot was suggested, somewhat last-minute as things often tend to go in Iceland, Ásta treated it as she does many of her projects: as an opportunity to create something entirely her own. Created in collaboration with photographer Sandijs Ruluks, the image leaves it unclear whether Ásta is pulling a world from her mouth or placing it inside. “It’s funny how we can create a whole world from some symbols, from a string of letters, from a place of the tongue where language resides, you can build a whole world just with your mouth. There are foldable universes all around us and within, the vastness of potential is unbelievable.
“I have really specific visions of how things should look to present a work, what colors, what shapes,” she says. For Ásta, this is a moment of transformation, when fear gives way to possibility. “When you realise that in the earth mind you have limited options and solutions, but if you enter a state of spirit there are endless possibilities available to you. That’s where miracles happen.”
Despite spending much of the year in Venice, Ásta is deeply attached to Reykjavík and its artistic community. “It’s so small you have to make due with what is there. So everyone is kind of in it together, the same boat, at the same pool.” And Pocket Universe itself emerged through collaboration with this community. “I got so much help doing this work.” Designers, musicians, performers, carpenters, singers, filmmakers and friends, she explains.
“The generosity of people around me is tremendous.” But these months abroad have also changed the way she thinks about Iceland. “I love Iceland more, it can be pretty grim here but somehow when you leave, you tend to love it more.”
Outside the art world, she suspects that she as a person is not especially different. Although perhaps shyer than people expect. When she needs to disappear, she heads outdoors. “Oh, to the mountains! Or to the swimming pool, where you can’t take your phone with you.”
And what happens after Venice? After Pocket Universe? After two years of preparation?
Ásta Fanney’s answer is short. “Oh! It’s a surprise,” she replies, grinning.
