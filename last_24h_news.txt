The government of Mexico, together with the European Commission, celebrated the modernization of an agreement aimed at integrating their economies and strengthening a deep relationship on the global stage, marking a new era between the two sides.
The government of President Claudia Sheinbaum welcomed an EU delegation led by European Council President António Costa and European Commission President Ursula von der Leyen, 26 years after the first global agreement between the parties was signed.
Sheinbaum, alongside Foreign Minister Roberto Velasco Álvarez and Economy Secretary Marcelo Ebrard celebrated the ambitious agreement under which 99% of tariffs on Mexican goods will be eliminated, alongside multimillion-dollar European investments.
“It is a truly global agreement from the beginning because it also covers political dialogue, cooperation, investment and, naturally, trade. Today, we are elevating this broad platform to a new level,” von der Leyen said during a ceremony at the National Palace.
In addition, the European Union’s Global Gateway investment strategy will mobilize €5 billion (47 billion), more than 100 billion pesos ($5.7 million), in Mexico for new wind and solar power generation projects, as well as clean mobility initiatives.
“The strength of what we are signing today — the update and modernization of the Treaty and the Global Agreement — is rooted in a vision of shared prosperity, joint work, and shared development. And just as Mexico has a relationship with the United States, we also have a relationship with the European Union, and we want to strengthen both relationships,” said Sheinbaum.
Part of the agreement also includes strengthening ties in security strategies and the fight against transnational crime.
Prior to the ceremony and the signing of the agreements, Mexico’s Security Minister Omar García Harfuch met Kaja Kallas, the EU’s foreign policy chief.

At least 16 injured in fire, explosion at New York dry dock facility: Reports
13 firefighters among injured after blast at Staten Island shipyard, according to local media
Ahmet Salih Alacaci
23 May 2026•Update: 23 May 2026
WASHINGTON
At least 16 people were injured Friday after a fire and explosion at a dry dock facility in New York City, local media reported, citing police and fire officials.
The incident occurred at a shipyard on Richmond Terrace in the Mariners Harbor neighborhood of Staten Island.
The Fire Department of New York reportedly said authorities received reports in the afternoon of a fire in the basement of a metal structure behind shipping docks, along with reports of workers trapped.
A major explosion was reported later as firefighters worked to extinguish the blaze, NBC News and ABC News reported.
The Fire Department said at least 13 firefighters were among the injured. Two firefighters suffered serious injuries, while two others sustained moderate injuries. Several others, along with two emergency medical service personnel, suffered minor injuries.
One civilian, believed to be a worker, was seriously injured, according to the reports.
Two people were pulled from the area and transported to nearby hospitals, fire officials said.
ABC News, citing police sources, reported that one worker may still be unaccounted for.
Several firefighters issued mayday calls while battling the blaze, but all were later located and were receiving treatment, the report added.
The cause of the fire and explosion was not immediately clear.

Entrepreneur says the rich should stop complaining about tax changes

Tech sector onslaught against proposed changes to capital gains tax could see start-ups carved out
Sat 23 May 2026 at 12:04pm
The federal government didn't really see it coming.
It had expected a political fight in relation to its budget announcement to axe the 50 per cent capital gains tax discount in favour of a "cost-based indexation" model.
But it hadn't expected a meme-fuelled backlash would come so strongly from the little guys — savvy young founders of small businesses and start-ups who took their messages of discontent to their millions of followers on social media.
"What changing capital gains tax around small businesses has to do with making housing more affordable for young Australians was a confusing puzzle piece to try and put together," says Rachael Wilde, co-founder of York St Brands.
Ms Wilde is one of the entrepreneurs who took to social media to share her frustration when the budget changes were announced.
Loading...She started a skincare business six years ago, with the backing of investors who were willing to take a punt on her. She's now running a multi-million-dollar beauty brand.
"The start-ups of today become the employers of tomorrow," Ms Wilde says.
Ms Wilde told the ABC she feared changes to the capital gains tax could mean lower returns for investors, and with fewer rewards, there could be reduced funding for start-ups.
"It is very rare that businesses get started with giant funding rounds from venture capital and private equity," she says.
"A lot of the time, we're relying on personal investors. But if you materially reduce the upside, and you don't at all reduce the risk, then you are kind of left asking the question: 'Why would these investors now come in and back such high-risk assets?'
"It's also left the question: 'Why are people going to take this leap in the first place?' Again, if the upside's been materially reduced, but the risk remains the same, I do think it will make young founders hesitant."
There is speculation that now, in response to the onslaught from Ms Wilde and many other well-known entrepreneurs and venture capital investors, Treasurer Jim Chalmers may announce some kind of concessional treatment for start-ups.
Over the past week, many in the tech sector have continued lobbying the government for an exemption, arguing start-ups shouldn't be the ones who become unintended victims of an attempt by the federal government to solve Australia's housing crisis.
Will start-ups be carved out of the CGT changes?
Under the government's proposed budget changes, the 50 per cent capital gains tax discount will be replaced with cost-based indexation from July 1, 2027 for assets held for more than 12 months, with earlier gains remaining under the current rules.
Instead of effectively halving a taxable capital gain, the purchase price will be adjusted for inflation, and tax will apply to the remaining "real" gain.
The government says there will also be a 30 per cent minimum tax on real capital gains.
It is unclear how a carve-out for start-ups will look. The government says it is still consulting on the changes, and earlier this week was at pains to point out that most small businesses would not be impacted.
Tech Council chief executive Kate Cornick has been meeting with the federal government about the issue.
Dr Cornick told ABC's Insiders On Background podcast: "Successful founders will see their tax rate increase significantly, and that is a disincentive to operating and starting and growing a start-up in this country, so we absolutely need to make sure that we are rewarding that risk."
Industry Minister Tim Ayres was asked by Sky News on Thursday about whether start-ups would be carved out of the new rules. He did not explicitly rule out future changes or carve-outs, instead saying the treasurer was consulting with the start-up community.
A spokesman for the treasurer did not detail whether there would be a change to its proposed CGT legislation, but told ABC News, certain "existing small business CGT carve-outs and concessions are not changing".
"The most recent tax data shows nine in 10 small businesses are eligible for CGT concessions, meaning they can pay reduced or zero CGT when they sell their business," he says.
"Any business with annual turnover below $2 million, or below $6 million in assets, will remain eligible for CGT concessions."
But entrepreneur Peter Dunne, who in 2015 helped the then government write laws on start-ups, says while he is apolitical on the issue, the current exemptions the government refers to do not apply to all small businesses, and therefore a specific carve-out for start-ups is needed in the proposed legislation.
Mr Dunne is the head of corporate development at Safety Culture — one of the country's biggest private software companies — and also sits on the advisory board of the Tech Council.
He told ABC News he saw merit in reducing tax breaks to tackle generational inequity, but says there would be unintended consequences if there were no carve-out for small businesses and start-ups.
"You almost have to be crazy to back yourself — to think you can create a business — and you've got to fight against your family and friends and all the advice of: 'Don't do it'," Mr Dunne says.
"If you make it harder for people to start businesses, or you disincentivise them, or they think, 'Why would I do that?' there are flow-on effects.
"We should never accept the lie that we're not smart enough to lead the world. We've got some of the best minds on the planet in our universities. How do we create the pathways for them to grow and develop? I feel like one of them is being closed right now."
Mr Dunne's message to Jim Chalmers is: "Keep being brave, but be brave enough to say, 'Hey, the consequence here might be not what we intended.'"
His solution is to essentially keep the 50 per cent CGT discount for anyone starting a business or investing in one.
"Anyone who starts a business that goes on and employs people, if they sell their equity in that business at a future point in time, the tax rate is half their marginal tax rate. Forever," Mr Dunne says.
"And anyone that they give equity to in that business, the tax rate is half the marginal tax rate when they sell."
Mr Dunne says, without a change, the risk of people leaving Australia for lower-tax jurisdictions "is not insignificant because you can now start a business anywhere in the world".
Some want the government to scrap its CGT change entirely
Stockspot founder Chris Brycki also warns that scrapping CGT for an indexation model will lead to many people questioning whether starting a business is worth it.
In his view, the government should scrap that entire policy rather than looking at carving specific groups out, but says: "Any carve-outs are better than no carve-outs."
He says that while he does not intend to leave Australia because of the change, because he has young kids in school here, he's hearing that others looking at starting a business may do so.
"I wouldn't be starting a business in a tax regime where 47 per cent is what I'll be paying away at the end," he says.
"And that's sad because there's a lot of technological change going on, obviously, behind the scenes with AI and Australia wants to be a country that's ahead of these changes and creating great innovations."
But others, like high-profile technology entrepreneur and chief executive of Freelancer, Matt Barrie, suggest the CGT change the government is proposing be scrapped entirely in favour of even more lucrative tax settings that encourage investment in Australia.
"It [the government's proposed budget changes] is going to create distortions for a future government to deal with when the property market comes off in future, my position is [for the government to] revert everything," Mr Barrie told ABC News.
"Or to go the other way and [bring a new] flat [CGT] rate of 20 per cent, or even 15 per cent or 0 per cent, if bold, for everything."
"The property bubble isn't because of tax; it's because of too many people [competing for housing]."
He says if the government's proposed CGT changes get legislated, there will be a "brain drain".
"You won't have people coming back," he warns.
"You won't have capital coming to Australia. You'll have Australia written off, if it isn't already, from the investment mandates of fund managers overseas.
"Hong Kong fund managers are looking to decamp somewhere; Singapore has taken some of it [investment that would have otherwise gone to Australia]."
Is the call to go offshore just a bluff?
Not everyone is convinced that the policy change will see people move offshore.
Some, like Airtree Ventures co-founder Daniel Petre, have taken to Australia's media, including the ABC, to blast his rich peers for complaining about paying fair levels of tax.
Mr Petre made his early fortune when he sold shares in his former employer, Microsoft, in the late 1990s, and says he had accepted a high tax rate at the time as a fair price to pay.
He later went on to start venture capital firms, including Airtree.
He says more favourable tax settings in countries like Singapore and the United States are not the only factor a founder considers when deciding where to set up.
His message to some of his tech community peers is to "just calm the f**k down".
"I think you need to take a Valium, take a Bex and have a lie down," Mr Petre told ABC News.
"I mean, I think it is just appalling that you have this sort of hyperbole going on without people firstly thinking through what might be fair, what might be fair in this country, and then where would fair land?"
Like his peers, Mr Petre also thinks any changes to the CGT proposed laws should solely focus on carving out genuine start-ups, but he wants the scare campaigns to stop.
"I have spoken to a founder who says they're going to leave Australia," he says.
"And I go, 'Well, OK. Have you thought through the talent pool? Have you thought through where you're going to get funding? Is your husband or wife and family happy to be uprooted and taken [overseas]? And where are you going to go? Where is this tax haven that you're going to that will allow you to build your business?
"I think there is a reason to be concerned, and we should solve the problem, so that we don't have this sort of aberration in the building of a significant start-up industry as we have.
"But I think the idea that some change, a reasonable change, will produce some flood of people out of the country is just not true."
Mr Petre notes that early-stage start-ups with little cash flow often offer employees equity in the company, or stock options, in lieu of higher pay. And he says, as such, it encourages founders to take risks with the potential of a big payday when they sell their companies.
He says founders and employees risking below-average pay and conditions for the prospect of future success should still be carved out. But employees of companies that are already successful and paying good salaries when staff join should not get lucrative tax perks.
"What I'm suggesting is a good place to land would be to allow a tax regime that ensures investors are still interested in investing, and that founders are still interested in founding companies and employees," Mr Petre says.
"And I think that can be done without just saying everybody gets the current 50 per cent CGT discount. That whole discount is not required. There is somewhere between that and what people are screaming about is what they think's going to happen that will be the right place to land."
