JPMorgan eyes role in Japan’s jaw-dropping $550 billion U.S. investment plan: report
The financing is seen as critical because Japanese banks have been reluctant to fund long-term U.S. infrastructure projects, citing the high cost of raising U.S. dollars

JPMorgan and other major U.S. banks are in talks to help finance Japan's $550 billion investment pledge in the United States, a move that could help Tokyo deliver on commitments made under a trade deal with the Trump administration, according to two people familiar with the discussions.
The financing is seen as critical because Japanese banks have been reluctant to fund long-term U.S. infrastructure projects, citing the high cost of raising U.S. dollars.
Japan has so far unveiled more than $100 billion in projects under the investment framework agreed to in July 2025. The package helped secure a 15% U.S. tariff on Japanese exports instead of a threatened 25% rate. Japanese officials are now eager to show progress.
The Trump administration has also presented Tokyo with a list of additional projects that could fall under the agreement, according to three sources familiar with the talks.
It remains unclear how much U.S. banks would contribute, which projects they would finance or whether the U.S. government is directly involved. JPMorgan declined to comment.
Japan's Ministry of Economy, Trade and Industry said no decision has been made on U.S. bank participation and that discussions on future projects are continuing.
Only $2.2 billion has been committed to the first round of projects announced in February. About one-third is being provided by the state-backed Japan Bank for International Cooperation, with the remainder funded by Mitsubishi UFJ Financial Group, Sumitomo Mitsui Financial Group and Mizuho Financial Group.
Those lenders have warned the government that raising long-term U.S. dollar funding is costly, limiting their ability to finance additional projects, according to sources.

The first investments include an oil export terminal in Texas, an industrial diamond plant in Georgia and a natural gas-fired power plant in Ohio. A second round includes small modular nuclear reactor projects in Tennessee and Alabama, along with natural gas plants in Pennsylvania and Texas.
Sources cautioned that even if major U.S. banks join the effort, infrastructure investments remain risky because they can take decades to generate returns and repay debt.

Three injured after Ukraine-bound tanker struck near Romanian coast
- A Liberian-flagged vessel, the LPG Gas Lisbon, was struck off Romania's coast, an incident Romanian president Nicusor Dan attributed to Russia's war on Ukraine.
- The attack occurred approximately 20 nautical miles outside Romanian territorial waters, resulting in three injured crew members who were subsequently evacuated by Romanian rescuers.
- A tugboat was dispatched to secure the vessel and prevent it from drifting, with Romanian authorities investigating the "serious incident" as likely part of Russia's aggression.
- The incident follows repeated breaches of Romanian airspace and territorial waters by Russian and Ukrainian drones throughout the current year.
- Kremlin spokesman Dmitry Peskov stated Russia would continue striking vessels supplying Ukraine's military, following a previous attack on the Golden Leo ship leaving Odesa which caused Indian casualties.

Tekever unveils AR6 drone able to lift 200kg over 500km

Tekever has unveiled the AR6, a new family of autonomous heavy lift aircraft designed and built in the UK that the company says will carry 200kg over 500km, with military applications including casualty evacuation, forward logistics and teaming with British Army Apaches under Project Nyx, the company announced at the Farnborough International Airshow.
The AR6 expands the company’s portfolio from the intelligence, surveillance and reconnaissance drones for which it is best known into the heavy lift class, addressing what Tekever describes as a core market gap, since the segment is dominated by electric VTOL platforms typically able to lift up to 100kg over very short distances of one to five kilometres. By drawing on specialist advances in hydraulic and propulsion technology, the company says the AR6 aims to set a new standard, built for harsh environments and able to operate in very hot and cold conditions with minimum maintenance and sustainment costs. Designed and built in the UK with British SME partners, the family delivers a sovereign solution for heavy lift autonomy, with production due to start by the end of 2026 at the company’s Swindon manufacturing hub.
“AR6 represents the next evolution of our autonomous capability,” said Ricardo Mendes, Tekever’s Chief Executive, as quoted in the press release. “For years, we’ve helped customers move information. Today, we’re extending that capability to moving what matters most. Autonomous Lift opens a new category of missions for TEKEVER and, more importantly, for our customers.”
The family will serve both civil and military markets, with civil use cases including logistics, offshore operations, medical support, search and rescue and disaster relief, while military roles span casualty evacuation, forward logistics and combat wingman concepts, among them the UK Apache teaming concept under Project Nyx, the Army’s effort to pair uncrewed aircraft with its attack helicopter force. The military variant will be able to operate fully without any reliance on satellite navigation, according to the company, and will carry advanced Mission Autonomy features for multi-platform swarming, crewed teaming and survivability, drawing on the company’s proprietary autonomy stack and what it describes as lessons from Ukraine, where its aircraft have flown extensively on surveillance missions.
“The future of autonomy is about delivering operational outcomes,” said Karl Brew, Head of Tekever UK, as quoted in the release. “AR6 expands the envelope enabling us to leverage all of the experience we have built up in Ukraine and around the world into a whole new set of use cases.”
The launch continues a striking period of British growth for the Portuguese-founded firm, which has committed £400 million of UK investment under its five-year Overmatch programme, achieved unicorn valuation, and operates its surveillance aircraft for customers including the Home Office, with the Swindon production line placing the AR6 within the same industrial expansion. A heavy lift platform with the range claimed would also speak to requirements emerging across NATO for uncrewed resupply and casualty evacuation in contested environments, where the combination the company describes, meaningful payload, long range and no satellite navigation dependence, addresses the conditions the war in Ukraine has made standard, though no launch customer or order accompanies the announcement.
