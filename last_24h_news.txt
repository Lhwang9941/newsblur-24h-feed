$40 million gift to a school where love and architecture bloomed
Like many architects, Susan and Garry Rothwell found love in the Brutalist grey concrete of the Wilkinson building at the University of Sydney, with vivid memories of being immersed in ideas and talking late into the night.
Sixty years after meeting as students, including 57 years of marriage, Susan, a practising architect, said she was happy to say they still shared a passion for how architecture can shape lives, how cities works and what good design meant.
This passion has prompted the Rothwell Family Foundation to give the university $40 million to support a new $74 million building to provide the same opportunities for future students.
Garry said that when he walked through the campus he remembered where “they’d studied, debated, put on architecture reviews and imagined what sort of architects – and people – we might become”.
It felt “like a full circle moment” to think they were creating a new building for the next generation of architecture, design and planning students.
A former practising architect, and founder of the family’s property development company, the Winten Property Group, Garry said the new 2000-square-metre school aimed to “thoughtfully integrate” with the 67-year-old Wilkinson building.
But the old building is no longer big enough to meet increased demand.
The dean of the School of Architecture, Design and Planning, Professor Donald McNeill, said enrolments across the three disciplines had risen 145 per cent in the past 10 years. The university will fund the balance of the project.
As part of the changes, McNeill said the profile of the Wilkinson building’s gallery space, the much loved Tin Sheds on City Road, would be strengthened.
To create a larger Architecture, Design and Planning precinct, the new building will be constructed on the site of existing greenhouses on Maze Crescent behind the Wilkinson building. The space between the old and new will become a gathering space for students.
McNeill said the new building would be dedicated entirely to teaching and studio spaces, encouraging students to collaborate.
The school’s alumni include many of the country’s most celebrated architects, including Camilla Block, Elizabeth Carpenter, Louise Cox, Philip Cox, Richard Leplastrier, Penelope Seidler, Imants Tillers, Philip Thalis, Peter Tonkin and Hannah Tribe.
Susan said architecture at Sydney had been intellectually demanding, yet deeply human – and the degree had opened their eyes to the world. “It taught us how to observe, how to think visually and spatially, and how to question assumptions.”
That rings true with the cofounder of architecture and design studio Trias, Jennifer McMaster, 2024’s winner of the Australian Institute of Architects’ national emerging architect prize.
A graduate of the University of Sydney, and a practice educator in architecture in the faculty, she said the school had always had an idealistic program that was about leadership and problem-solving.
It brought like-minded people together. It was where she met her husband, Jonathon Donnelly, a director of Trias, and Casey Bryant, its third founder: “We fell in love with architecture there, and this love is the DNA of our practice.”

Surrogacy reforms should take the stress out of a legal arrangement
Today we celebrate Mother’s Day with gifts, cards and family gatherings but some among us face difficulties becoming mothers – and some of them decide to turn to surrogacy.
For some it is a positive experience – for others it is fraught with cost and difficulty.
Our Sunday Life story by Courtney Thompson about two friends from childhood who agreed to walk the path of surrogacy together is both heartwarming and an illustration of the difficulties to be faced.
Former business director Lucy Koops had endured more than 10 uterine operations, multiple IVF treatments, seven angel babies (including one stillborn), deep depression, financial hardship and, ultimately, the loss of her uterus before she and her husband accepted the offer from her life-long friend Ashleigh Raper to be a surrogate.
Baby Mia, conceived from Koops’ egg and her husband’s sperm, was carried by Raper, Network Ten’s political editor, with two children of her own, to full term.
In addition to spending $200,000 on trying to conceive, Koops faced immediate hurdles when Mia arrived, ranging from waiting for a parentage order being granted to remove the surrogate as the legal mother, to not being shown how to feed and wrap the newborn, to the logistical nightmare of adding her to her Medicare account. She now advocates for regulated surrogacy reform to protect both the intended parents and the surrogates from legal limbo.
Mia’s story is an example of altruistic surrogacy where women carry a child for others without profit and selflessly provide the opportunity to be parents. Commercial surrogacy is illegal in Australia; international surrogacy comes with significant risks.
The experience of two Canberra women, Melinda and Gail McCann, as reported last week shows what can go wrong. They paid Melbourne lawyer Paul Norris-Ongso’s company Global Surrogacy $100,000 for a baby born in Colombia in 2024. The women alleged the surrogate mother had health problems, was not paid adequately, and it took two months for their new baby to obtain Australian citizenship.
Last financial year, 369 children born through surrogacy arrangements became Australian citizens, up from 218 in 2021. It has been reported that in 2020, about 76 children were born through altruistic surrogacy.
The Australian Law Reform Commission is holding an inquiry reviewing surrogacy laws and among the 438 submissions there are stories joyful and horrific.
Lucy Koops’ experience with newborn Mia highlights a need for improvement: changing the altruistic surrogacy system to allow intended parents to apply for a parentage order during the pregnancy. This would allow them to be recognised as the legal parents before birth and obviate issues with Medicare, passports, hospital admissions, enrolling for childcare and remove some of the stress from what is already a legal arrangement. Further, NSW Health needs guidelines for families going through surrogacy.
Many still believe altruistic surrogacy is illegal. Dispelling this misconception would make it easier for those involved in a practice that has the imprimatur of all states and territories in Australia.
The ALRC report is due to be delivered to the Attorney-General Michelle Rowland by July 29, and it is clear reforms are needed but the rights of surrogates, intended parents and, especially, children must be protected.

Players across the country to protest gender-based violence and call for better treatment in sport
Jaydan Thomson used to dread heading home after work as a basketball player and coach as she was bound to run into her alleged stalker in the car park.
He was a fellow NBL1 player from the men's team of the same club, Ipswich Basketball Association, and she said he would sit in his car and wait for her to lock up.
Thomson alleges he stalked and harassed her, which led to her taking out a temporary protection order and domestic violence order against him in April of 2022, which the ABC has seen. He never received criminal charges.
Thomson raised her concerns multiple times to the club at the time and took it to Basketball Queensland — correspondence the ABC has seen — particularly concerned about his involvement in coaching a youth team and being invited to speak at a school.
She believes the situation was not adequately addressed by the club, which told her the matter was noted but allowed him to continue to play.
The ABC has contacted Ipswich Basketball Association and the former general manager of Ipswich multiple times for comment.
Basketball Queensland would not comment on the specific circumstances of Thomson's experience or concerns but told the ABC it "takes all matters relating to the wellbeing, safety and integrity of our members and wider basketball community seriously. Basketball Queensland's policies are in compliance with Sport Integrity Australia's standards with regard to issues and complaints".
Disappointed by the lack of action, Thomson, who now plays for the Gold Coast Rollers, says the handling of the incident highlights the lack of safeguarding for women and girls when issues arise in sporting systems.
"Even when that whole situation happened, I don't think anyone believed me, besides my own family. No one believes me," Thomson told ABC Sport.
"It makes you believe that it wasn't that bad … That maybe I was overreacting, maybe they were right and I kept overthinking."
When she shared her story on social media she was overwhelmed by the support from people with similar stories.
"I was amazed. There was an influx of people in my messages saying, 'this happened to me, I'm so sorry' or 'can you please tell me who it was so that we can stop them from playing or not coming to the club' or they were telling me their story," she said.
"Talking about it made me understand that we've got to actually speak up."
"I know it's really hard sometimes for people, but speaking up does a lot more (than not), especially when you have the right resources [and] with support."
Thomson will be part of a protest across the nation at the end of May calling for greater accountability from clubs when it comes to the safety of women and girls in their systems.
Wear Red Round
Last year, football teams across Victoria took to the field in red armbands to protest the axing of state government women-in-sport programs and honour women who have been killed in gender-based violence.
The protests came after the closure of the Office for Women in Sport and Recreation and the end of Preventing Violence Through Sport Grants in the state budget.
Teams that joined included Bass Coast Breakers, Mount Alexander Falcons, Darebin Falcons, North Bendigo women's team, Altona Football Club, West Brunswick, Williamstown and Gisborne Football Club, among others.
This year, the initiative, spearheaded by social media advocacy group Not All Clubs, which calls out inequality in sport online, will be across the nation and include various codes, including football, basketball, rugby, netball and surfing.
Players across the weekend of May 29–31 will wear two armbands: one to honour victims of gendered violence and the other to call for greater accountability in safeguarding in sport.
Official numbers of clubs and leagues set to take part are unknown as registrations are yet to be sent out.
Reprimands for speaking up
Ruby Campbell is a women's footy player and will be joining the protest at her club, Sandhurst in the Central Victorian Football League.
She believes women are often on the receiving end of negative consequences for speaking up about their situation or advocating for themselves in the context of grassroots sport.
Last year, she was reprimanded by her league for breaching social media policy after calling out an umpire for a misogynistic comment on Facebook.
The umpire for her league replied to a comment on a thread about coverage of women's football saying the female footballers' games were "hardly worth writing about".
Campbell replied to this by tagging the Bendigo Umpires Association and writing "it's great to see umpires who are appointed to female games believe in us and support us!!!! Do better [named redacted]. We are so glad our games pay your misogynistic wage".
Following this, Campbell says she was sent a "please explain" from the league, which the ABC has seen, and subsequent reprimand.
"Why am I in trouble?" Campbell said.
"It feels like you can't ask for too much, like, 'don't bite the hand that feeds you' type thing."
Sandhurst and the Central Victorian Football League have been contacted for comment.
It was important to Campbell to continue to be an advocate, despite feeling like "the angry woman" or "loud woman" in the room.
"It feels like you're consistently trying to correct behaviour and that makes you feel like the bad person … [it] makes you feel like the angry person, but it's not angry for asking for the bare minimum," she said.
Campbell said the issues women faced went well beyond the sporting field.
"We know that this issue of the violence against women, it's not getting any better, it's not going anywhere … And we want to be a good example to show for our under-18 side that we value these things. We think it's important. We want to use our voice and show that this isn't OK," Campbell said.
Campbell plays in the league the Wedge-Tailed Eagles Football Club landed in after they defected from Kyneton Football Netball Club in late 2024 after claiming "a horrible, volatile environment", and has watched women's teams across her region fold due to lack of support.
That reporting by the ABC on Kyneton revealed a widespread issue around Victoria where players from various clubs claimed poor treatment, inequitable access to resources and a lack of support from their football clubs.
Wedge-Tailed Eagles club president Natalie Korinfsky told the ABC when they went public with allegations of mistreatment, they had a number of players from clubs across the country share similar stories with them.
Not just grateful to get to play
Courtney Plowman, 30, who comes from an Aussie Rules background and now plays and coaches girls state rugby in Adelaide, has witnessed a "boys club mentality" throughout her years in sport.
She will take part in the red armbands protest with her rugby club, Old Collegians, who she said she's lucky to be at as the men are "champions for us" and help to break down barriers, including equal sharing of resources. But she is aware that is not the case for others.
"Most old footy teams that I've been a part of and previous clubs, it's very 'boys' club'," Plowman told ABC Sport.
"It starts from the top down, basically, and it not being a 'boys will be boys' club. That can be very toxic and unsafe space for women to come into and young girls to come into."
Growing up, Plowman said there was a notion amongst her generation of having to be grateful to just get the chance to play at all.
However, with the next generation coming through, she's seeing a demand for better and more equal conditions.
"And I just want to touch on that it's great that we are raising awareness [through the protests], especially in male-dominated codes," she said.
"[But] I want it to be the start of the conversation, especially with violence against women, it's the start of it. We''ve got a long way to go."
