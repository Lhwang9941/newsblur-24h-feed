The October 7 massacre fundamentally changed Israel’s perception of security. What had long been viewed as an extreme scenario became a brutal and painful reality. Thousands of Hamas terrorists infiltrated communities in southern Israel, murdering residents, taking hostages, and destroying entire communities.
Since then, it has become clear that Israel cannot rely on outdated assumptions or security concepts that have not been reexamined. Against this backdrop, a study by retired lieutenant-colonel and attorney Maurice Hirsch deserves particular attention. It warns of the potential threat posed by the Palestinian Authority’s security forces.
The study raises serious questions. How did a limited police force, originally intended to maintain public order under the Oslo Accords, become a much larger security apparatus?
Findings of the study
According to the study, the force now includes more than 70,000 security personnel equipped with tens of thousands of weapons. They receive training in areas including urban warfare, parachuting, diving, and other military activities.
The study asks whether such a force continues to serve its original purpose or whether its growth requires Israel to reassess the threat it may pose.
One of the study’s most striking comparisons is with the Hamas attack on October 7. Thousands of terrorists infiltrated Israel that day. The force that penetrated the farthest reached the Ofakim area, about 22 kilometers from the Gaza Strip.
By comparison, the distance from Kalkilya to Israel’s coastal plain is only about 14 km. That area includes Kfar Saba, Ra’anana, Hod Hasharon, Herzliya, industrial zones, and critical national infrastructure.
The significance is clear. Central Israel has almost no strategic depth, and any unusual incident could escalate with extraordinary speed.
This does not mean that the Palestinian Authority has decided to launch an attack against Israel. However, every responsible security establishment examines not only intentions but also capabilities.
Intentions can change overnight. Military capabilities built over many years remain in place. This is one of the central lessons Israel has drawn from the events of October 7.
Israel’s political and security leadership has also significantly changed its approach to the threat over the past year. Defense Minister Israel Katz regularly conducts security assessments and meetings concerning emerging threats in the West Bank.
Lessons of October 7
Learning the lessons of October 7 requires a fundamental change in thinking. For years, many believed that economic interests and security coordination would be enough to prevent escalation.
Similar arguments were made about Hamas. It was said that the organization was focused on governing the Gaza Strip and was not interested in a broad confrontation. Reality proved otherwise.
Therefore, even when security coordination with the Palestinian Authority continues, Israel must constantly evaluate the potential risks.
A scenario in which hundreds or thousands of armed men infiltrate central Israel from the West Bank cannot be dismissed simply because it appears extreme. The role of the security establishment is to prepare even for scenarios that may be unlikely but could have devastating consequences.
Should such an event occur, the response time would be extremely limited. The potential damage to civilians, transportation routes, and national infrastructure could be severe.
At the same time, it is important to distinguish between a security warning and a definitive conclusion. The existence of a large security force does not, by itself, prove an intention to launch an attack.
That is precisely why close intelligence oversight, continuous monitoring, and a professional assessment of force structures, weapons, and training are essential. A responsible country does not wait until a threat materializes.
Israel must also continue strengthening the defense of communities along the seam line. It must expand early warning capabilities, improve physical barriers, reinforce local emergency response teams, and ensure that the IDF, police, and other security agencies can respond quickly to any scenario.
There is no room for complacency in the new security reality.
Hirsch’s study is not necessarily a prediction, but it raises questions that a responsible country cannot ignore. Following the national trauma of October 7, Israel has an obligation to reexamine every basic assumption, every military buildup, and every potential threat.
It is better to confront a warning in time than to explain afterward why it was ignored.
The most important lesson of October 7 is simple. A country cannot base its security on the hope that the other side will always choose restraint.
Security is built on intelligence, preparedness, deterrence, and clear-eyed thinking. If Hirsch’s study helps ensure that decision-makers continue examining reality without outdated assumptions or illusions, it will already have played an important role in protecting the citizens of Israel.
The writer is CEO of Radios 100FM, an honorary consul and deputy dean of the Consular Diplomatic Corps, president of the Israeli Radio Communications Association, and a former news monitor for Israel Army Radio and television correspondent for NBC.

From soldiering to start-ups: Israel's Druze community is building its own start-up ecosystem

The Druze nation in Israel is known for its firm ties to the state, its resilience, and its ability to adapt. After years of preparation and effort, the Druze are widening their focus on the future and moving from inspiration to creating an infrastructure to benefit their community and Israel.
“Our young people are our future,” says Koftan Halabi, Druze hi-tech entrepreneur and founder of the Druze tech center in Isfiya.
“We proudly say our national identity is Israeli. Most of our young people enlist in the IDF or do National Service. After completion of their service, many choose the Israel Defense Forces as their career or enter other types of security work.
“We came to the realization that there needs to be more choices, and it is our goal to provide viable options for IDF veterans through higher education, which will enable entrance into technological fields and the hi-tech job market.
“In addition, we have now created a fund to advance their creative, technological ideas. Therefore, the discharged soldiers will have the monetary support to enter the hi-tech start-up ecosystem and become entrepreneurs.”
This process began in 2009, when Halabi established the Druze Veteran Association and initiated a program for Druze veterans to study at Haifa’s Technion-Israel Institute of Technology. 
D-TEC hosts hi-tech festival launching BEYOND-D innovations program
In 2025, Halabi led in building and opening the state-of-the-art Druze Technological and Empowerment Center (D-TEC) in Isfiya. In June of this year, the center initiated a hi-tech festival whose focus was to launch a new innovations program called BEYOND-D (Druze) and inaugurate a new investment fund connected to inspire and support new hi-tech start-ups.
“It is a historic milestone,” he proudly says, “which is the fruit of years of trust, strategic proposals, and a strong desire to pave new paths for the younger generation,” states Halabi.
A tech fair displaying new ideas is important, says lawyer Anan Kheir, one of the founders of Isfiya’s Druze tech initiative. “We take pride in opening a fund which will benefit former IDF Druze, Circassian, and Jewish soldiers in their entrepreneurial objectives. Overall, our goal is to create a practical mechanism that connects entrepreneurs with professional mentoring, strategic partners, industry leaders, and investment capital.”
Kheir explains to the Magazine that the fund is not a personal grant fund for individual soldiers, but rather an investment fund for start-ups and technology ventures. He envisions eligible applicants as the founders or team members who are working on technology ventures with a serious technological idea.
The fund will evaluate each according to the capabilities of its founding team, its product and ideas, its growth potential, as well as its connection to the goals of the fund.
“The fund,” explains Kheir, “is a vehicle to invest in ventures led or co-led by Israeli combat-discharged veterans or active-duty Israeli combat soldiers (Druze, Circassian, and Jewish) with innovative ideas and potential.”
It is intended to support Israeli hi-tech ventures at their early stages: pre-seed, seed, and Round A. The broader purpose is to open a real path for young entrepreneurs with technological potential and enable them to enter the Israeli hi-tech ecosystem, build companies, and create long-term economic leadership.
“The fund is seeking capital commitments of up to $40 million, with an initial closing possible once commitments of at least $15 mil. are secured,” Kheir continues. “The amounts invested in each start-up will depend on the stage of the company, the investment decision, the due diligence process, and the fund’s internal investment policy.”
The money, he explains, is expected to come from private investors, limited partners, and strategic investors who commit capital to the fund. “This is different from event sponsorship,” he explains. “The fund is structured as an investment vehicle, not as a donation campaign.”
Kheir points out that they will consider early-stage technology ventures in areas such as software, artificial intelligence, cyber, digital health, industrial technologies, deep-tech, security-related technologies, and other Israeli or Israeli-related innovative ideas.
Halabi says their vision is to see new hi-tech start-ups and companies in the vicinity of Druze communities across the North. “Currently, our graduates are working throughout Israel. Our investment fund will enable those who wish to develop innovative ideas and build entrepreneurship close to home.”
Hi-power hi-tech guest list
The recent D-TEC Druze-Tech Festival evening event was distinguished by attendance and participation of senior figures in the fields of hi-tech, investment, innovation, government, and public service: venture capital and hi-tech entrepreneur Nechemia “Chemi” Peres, co-founder and managing partner of Pitango Venture Capital and one of the founders of the Peres Center for Peace and Innovation; Maj.-Gen. (res.) Amiram Levin, co-founder of NextVision, as well as a prominent figure in Israel’s security investment and technology sectors; Scott Tobin, founding partner of Battery Ventures and a leading international investor in technology and innovation; Raanan Raz, cyber entrepreneur, former VP of R&D who led the engineering team at Datorama, and co-founder and CEO of Avalor Security, both involved in major exits; and Ran Ber, entrepreneur and leader in the field of innovation processes, who is also CEO of Beyond – Druze Innovations and Beyond Ventures, and the former director-general of the Innovation, Science and Technology Ministry.
Other guests included Hagai Reznik, chairman of the Fund and Unit for Discharged Soldiers at the Defense Ministry, who has been instrumental in promoting opportunities for discharged soldiers in education, employment and social mobility; Dr. Ramzi Halabi, chairman of the Beit Yad Labanim in the Druze community, researcher and manager promoting initiatives for economic, social, and regional development; Kheir, who, with Koftan Halabi and Ber, is one of the three founders of the Druze Investment initiative, as well as board member and legal adviser of D-TEC, and active in legal, public, and international work with a focus on innovation, economic empowerment and the advancement of the Druze community; and Shlomo Gradman, chairman of the Israeli High-Tech Association CEOs Forum and one of the leading figures in Israel’s semiconductor and innovation industries.
The participation of the senior supporters, speakers, and advisers at the Druze Tech-Fest gave not only prestige to the event but also meaning to the evening, as they shared practical knowledge and useful tips with the new entrepreneurs through panels, roundtable discussions, and informal meetings.
Hanan Markovich, CEO of the Haifa Economic Corporation (HEC), was very pleased to take part in the investors’ panel and is supportive of the Druze tech initiative.
As the CEO of a large company that is one of the driving forces in Haifa and the North, Markovich understands the importance of economic growth in undeveloped areas. HEC is a leader in large-scale, strategic projects in real estate, energy, tourism, defense technology, and venture initiatives.
Markovich says he is committed to encouraging entrepreneurship in Haifa and the North through HEC’s subsidiary and investment fund, HiCenter, which is inviting entrepreneurs to apply to its investment program.
“Haifa is a unique city where five religions [Judaism, Christianity, Islam, Druzism, and Baha’i] live side by side in a spirit of coexistence,” says Markovich.
“I share the vision of Haifa’s mayor, Yona Yahav, who has long supported strong relations with the Druze community and meaningful cooperation among the city’s diverse communities.
“With my 20 years of experience in hi-tech, I am committed to assisting in turning this vision of growth into development by supporting and developing initiatives which promote economic growth, attract hi-tech companies, and empower entrepreneurs in Haifa and the northern region.
“Tel Aviv is currently home to a large share of Israel’s start-ups,” Markovich continues. “That is a major strength. However, the right path for Israel, both [from a Zionist and economic perspective], is to develop additional hi-tech centers which will include Haifa and the North as well as in the South. Investing in start-ups beyond Tel Aviv will strengthen the country’s resilience, create new opportunities, and turn innovation into a broader national asset.”
Markovich explains that he and his fellow investment panel members discussed guiding principles and practical advice for early-stage entrepreneurs. One of the key points was the ability to tell a clear and compelling story, and to present it effectively in English as well as in Hebrew, since many of the major markets and investors are overseas.
He also emphasizes the importance of the founding team. “Many start-ups are required to pivot during the life of the company,” says Markovich. “When that happens, the strength, flexibility, and complementary capabilities of the team often become a decisive factor.”
He explains that the panel also discussed the need to focus on solving an acute problem in a large market, which significantly improves the start-up’s chances of success. In addition, Markovich highlights the importance of strong intellectual property and its proper protection to make it difficult for competitors to copy.
“Turning an innovative idea into a successful company is never simple,” Markovich states, “but it is one of the most exciting journeys an entrepreneur can take.”
Halabi is optimistic the tech initiative will succeed.

Bahrain and Kuwait secretly sent fighter jets to attack targets inside Iran, marking their first direct retaliatory action against Tehran, the Wall Street Journal reported on Friday.
According to sources familiar with the details, the airstrikes, carried out earlier this month, targeted drone and missile storage facilities, among other military sites.
Sources familiar with the matter told WSJ the two Gulf states have relatively small air forces equipped with US and European aircraft, but were unwilling to allow Tehran to continue attacking them without a response.
Countries such as Oman and Qatar have for years pursued a balanced policy between Iran and the West and have shown little willingness to confront Tehran directly, people familiar with the details shared with WSJ.
According to the report, Saudi Arabia joined the United Arab Emirates in strikes against Iran at an earlier stage of the war, but is now reassessing its position.
UAE provided intelligence on the targets and defensive air cover for the strikes
The report also claimed that the United Arab Emirates, which attacked Iran several times at the beginning of the war, provided intelligence on the targets and defensive air cover for the strikes, a sign of emerging Arab cooperation against Iran, according to the sources.
