Lius Diaz dazzles as Colombia beat World Cup 2026 debutants Uzbekistan 3-1
Winger Luis Diaz scored a goal and set up another, as Colombia made a winning return to the FIFA World Cup after missing the last edition.
Colombia opened their World Cup Group K campaign with a 3-1 victory over Uzbekistan at the Estadio Azteca on Wednesday, as Daniel Munoz, Luis Diaz and Jaminton Campaz struck to overcome a spirited second-half response from the tournament debutants.
Uzbekistan were disciplined for long periods under their Italian coach Fabio Cannavaro, but Colombia’s greater quality stood out in front of a crowd of over 80,000 on a cool, rain-tinged evening in Mexico City.

Colombia, the Copa America runners-up, had early sights of goal through Jhon Arias and James Rodriguez, but Uzbekistan sat deep, scrapped gamely and waited for mistakes. Bekhruz Karimov almost profited when he burst forward, only for Jhon Lucumi to intervene before he could shoot.
Diaz had the clearest chance of the opening half when he struck the post, before Abdukodir Khusanov slid in after the winger had knocked the ball past him, taking out both the Colombian player and a pitchside cameraman who required medical treatment.
Uzbekistan’s resistance finally cracked in the 40th minute. Diaz gathered the ball after an attack had broken down and clipped a fine pass into the path of Munoz, who guided home a neat finish for his third international goal.
The large Colombian contingent erupted, their yellow shirts making the Azteca look and sound almost like home. Chants of “Vamos Colombia”, adapted from a Club America-style chorus, rolled around the ground, while Uzbekistan’s small band of supporters answered with drums of their own.
Fayzullaev scores Uzbekistan’s maiden World Cup goal
Uzbekistan improved after the break and equalised on the hour with the country’s first World Cup goal.
Dostonbek Khamdamov fed Eldor Shomurodov, whose shot from the right side of the box was saved low by Camilo Vargas. The goalkeeper could not hold it, however, and Abbosbek Fayzullaev nodded in the rebound from close range.
However, Uzbekistan’s joy lasted only five minutes.
Gustavo Puerta released Diaz in the 65th minute, and the forward side-footed across goal to restore Colombia’s lead. The crowd responded with chants of “Lucho, Lucho”.
Uzbekistan kept pushing. Akmal Mozgovoy shot narrowly off target in stoppage time, Karimov hit the bar with an effort from distance, and Azizbek Amonov had a shot blocked after Otabek Shukurov’s pass.
But Colombia had the final word, Campaz scoring in the ninth minute of stoppage time to settle a contest in which Nestor Lorenzo’s side had 15 attempts to Uzbekistan’s nine, and extended their strong recent group-stage record to seven wins in eight World Cup matches.
Colombia face DR Congo on Tuesday in Guadalajara, after Uzbekistan play Portugal on the same day in Houston.

From the capital
Brussels will welcome a reshuffled cast of leaders today for a two-day summit, but the real question is whether the new faces will merely change the atmosphere, or the politics.
The most consequential newcomer is Hungary’s prime minister, Péter Magyar. His arrival has already had an immediate impact. In the past month, with Viktor Orbán gone, EU capitals have been able to open Ukraine’s first accession negotiating cluster and approve the stalled 20th sanctions package against Russia.
For the first time in years, leaders are also expected to agree summit conclusions unanimously at 27 – a reminder of how much a single departure can alter the dynamics around the table.
Not everyone is new. Familiar figures are coming back, including the populist leaders Janez Janša of Slovenia and Rumen Radev of Bulgaria. They may not represent the bloc’s largest countries, but smaller players can still become effective spoilers.
Magyar’s impact is already visible but whether he will help reshape “the long-term dynamics of EUCO is yet to be seen,” one EU diplomat said.
Leaders face two thorny debates today: Ukraine (meaning enlargement), and “macroeconomic imbalances” (the diplomatic euphemism for talks about China). They will meet again on Friday to tackle the next long-term budget, where fresh numbers are now on the table, and the Middle East, where capitals remain divided over further sanctions on Israel.
For Kyiv, the immediate question is whether the five remaining negotiating clusters can be opened this summer. Momentum is stronger than it was a few months ago, but several countries still harbour political reservations, diplomats said. Hovering over budget talks is another source of uncertainty: a packed electoral calendar next year. France, Poland, Italy and Spain are all expected to head to the polls.
That prospect is already shaping discussions on the next long-term budget. While EU institutions are pushing for a deal by the end of the year – partly out of concern that a stronger far right, particularly in France, could complicate negotiations after 2027 – many capitals see little urgency.
“It’s in the back of everybody’s mind,” one diplomat said of the French elections. “But no one’s going to sacrifice their own interests just to get a deal done faster.”

EU ministers skip Arab League gathering
The bloc’s foreign ministers largely declined to attend a meeting with the Arab League in Jordan next week, prompting Kaja Kallas to step in and represent the EU, diplomats told Rapporteur.
Just four European foreign ministers had confirmed attendance at Tuesday’s ministerial gathering in Amman, compared with close to 20 counterparts from Arab states. “The lack of engagement from the European side was perceived by the Jordanians as an insult,” one European diplomat briefed on the discussions said.
Kallas’ office told Rapporteur that “the hard part of the US-Iran talks still lies ahead, making this a particularly important moment for the EU to engage closely with the region.”
Von der Leyen’s fashion diplomacy
It is hard to name many people in top EU positions who are known for their eye-catching style. Ursula von der Leyen is no exception. But, perhaps, that is the point.
The Commission president dresses with studied restraint, seemingly intent on keeping clothes out of the story while recognising they are always a part of it.

Taliban gets ready for Brussels visit
A Taliban delegation is gearing up for a visit to Brussels after Belgian Foreign Minister Maxime Prévot told the national parliament on Wednesday the country has formally received visa applications from all five members expected to attend the talks.
Last week, Rapporteur reported that the delegation had yet to apply for visas, pushing back a visit that was initially expected in June. With processing times uncertain, July is seen as a more realistic target.
Preparations in Brussels are already underway. On Wednesday, the Commission’s migration department circulated a note asking EU capitals to designate a contact person should they wish to engage with the delegation during its visit, EU officials told Rapporteur.
A Commission spokesperson declined to comment further.
Montenegro wants a different deal
Montenegro should not face the same post-accession safeguards as other membership hopefuls, the country’s EU minister, Maida Gorčević, told my colleague Magnus Lund Nielsen in an interview.
On Monday, Enlargement Commissioner Marta Kos said new members could face probationary measures and restrictions for more than a decade after joining the bloc.
Gorčević, a pro-EU minister from Montenegro’s centrist Europe Now Movement! who has overseen EU affairs since 2023, argued Podgorica is uniquely prepared for membership and should therefore face a shorter transitional period than candidates such as Moldova, Ukraine and Albania. Meloni delays EU defence money
Divisions within Giorgia Meloni’s government are delaying Italy’s decision on whether to pursue its €14.9 billion share of the EU’s SAFE defence loans, exposing tensions over military spending at the heart of the coalition.
Defence Minister Guido Crosetto is pushing for greater investment, while Economy Minister Giancarlo Giorgetti is urging caution amid competing fiscal pressures.
The rift widened in May when, under pressure from far-right Matteo Salvini’s League, the government signalled it could scale back its SAFE request and instead seek greater fiscal flexibility from Brussels to help absorb energy costs.
Rome has yet to decide, officials told my colleague Pietro Guastamacchia, leaving Italy’s participation effectively on hold.

Tech workers who don’t embrace AI face triple the layoff risk, Gallup finds

SAN FRANCISCO – For tech workers, artificial intelligence may be delivering a benefit that goes beyond efficiency: a lower risk of being laid off.
New research from Gallup finds that tech workers who aren’t regular AI users are three times more likely to lose their jobs than their peers.
Among US tech workers who use AI at least monthly, the predicted probability of being laid off is about 6 per cent, compared with 18 per cent for workers who use the technology less often.
The estimates are based on a survey data in February involving more than 23,000 US workers, including 660 respondents who reported being unemployed after their jobs were eliminated.
Gallup collected data on how often employed and displaced workers used AI – from daily to not at all – and then used a statistical model to estimate how factors such as AI-use frequency and industry were associated with the likelihood of job loss.
Outside the tech industry, infrequent AI users also face a higher layoff risk than their peers, Gallup said, though the gap is smaller.
The link between AI use and job security held even after accounting for factors such as age, education and the sector in which one works, according to the researchers, suggesting that employees who don’t use AI are “more vulnerable in the job market.”
The findings also indicate that AI adoption is becoming a fault line inside companies, one that’s increasingly affecting individual careers.
Employers are already screening candidates for a certain level of AI fluency, and the Gallup report suggests the technology may also be influencing which workers companies choose to retain when they downsize.
Meanwhile, executives continue to press employees to use AI, even as public perceptions of the technology have soured and concerns about job losses have intensified.
Even so, only about 1 per cent of laid-off workers attributed their job loss directly to AI.
The most commonly cited reasons were more general, including organisational restructuring, cost-cutting and economic conditions.
The data point may “understate AI’s indirect influence” in companies’ layoff decisions, the researchers said.
“That surprised me the most,” said Jim Harter, chief scientist for Gallup’s workplace management and wellbeing practices. “They didn’t just blame AI.”
That leaves a disconnect between how workers and executives explain layoffs.
AI was the top reason companies cited for job cuts in May, accounting for about 40 per cent of such announcements, according to outplacement firm Challenger, Gray & Christmas Inc.
How AI factors into job cut deliberations remains an open question, Harter said.
It’s possible that workers who use the technology more frequently are simply able to get more done than their peers.
Or employers may be paying attention to how often workers use AI – for example, how many times they prompt a chatbot in a given week.
“I don’t think that’s the right direction,” Harter said.
Tying performance evaluations to AI usage could encourage employees to overuse the tools in an effort to game the system.
“The real bottom line is: Are they more productive?” BLOOMBERG
