EU ambassadors will meet on Wednesday in the hopes of reaching a compromise with Greece and passing a 21st package of sanctions against Russia over its invasion of Ukraine, EU diplomats said.
The new package targets the country's banking sector in an effort to squeeze Moscow's financial system at a vulnerable moment.
However, Greece, which wants the EU to soften planned restrictions on Russian LNG supplies, has become the main obstacle to its adoption.
Athens argued last week a forthcoming transfer ban on Russian liquefied natural gas (LNG) would simply shift market share outside Europe and would not impact Russian revenues.
Greece dominates Europe's LNG carrier market and is among the biggest players globally, competing with Japan, China and the United States.
EU diplomats had hoped the departure of Hungary's Viktor Orban, who consistently delayed Ukraine aid and Russia sanctions, would translate to smooth sailing on future packages.
However, new cracks in the bloc's unity have come to the surface as the European Commission tries to close remaining loopholes for Russian business and put further pressure on Russia's vital energy revenues.
Banking, crypto measures
Russia's major banks were disconnected in 2022 from SWIFT, a secure global financial payment instructions system, under Western sanctions.
However, Russian companies have succeeded in maintaining trade and financial flows via small or regional lenders as well as cryptocurrency networks.
The package lists about 215 individuals and entities including 94 financial institutions.
Close to 90 of these are banks, taking the total number of sanctioned banks to over 100, or more than half of Russia's 213 internationally connected lenders.
A European state intelligence service report, seen by Reuters, warned in June that Russia risked an "explosive" banking crisis and a new sanctions package targeting banks could trigger an economic shock.
Russian authorities have dismissed warnings of a looming crisis.
EU diplomatic sources said the aim of the listings was to discourage third countries from dealing with these Russian lenders, given any EU ties are now minimal or non-existent.
Alongside the listings, the European Commission proposed to freeze the oil price cap at its current level of $44.10 a barrel for six months.
The price cap was redesigned last year to track oil price movements and lowered from $60 a barrel.
EU envoys agreed last week to temporarily freeze the cap until July 23 in the hopes of finding a broad agreement.
A scheduled review would have increased the price cap on the back of the Iran war and thus provided substantially higher earnings to Moscow.

Trek from Poland to Italy: New 3,500km hiking trail crosses six European countries

From Poland’s Baltic coast to Italy’s Adriatic shores, the new Wolf Trail takes hikers on a mammoth journey through six European countries, crossing mountains, forests and historic cities along the way.
Europe just got a new long-distance hiking challenge – and it’s a seriously ambitious one.
The Wolf Trail covers nearly 3,500 kilometres across six countries, taking hikers from Poland’s Baltic coast to Italy’s Trieste waterfront, through Germany, Austria, Switzerland and Slovenia, with 78,400 metres of elevation gain thrown in for good measure.
In other words, it’s not for the faint-hearted, but for those willing to spend four to six months on the trail, it could very well be one of the ultimate ways to see Europe, one step at a time.
What is the Wolf Trail and what will you see?
This is very much a trail for the most committed hikers, as it spans a mind-blowing 3,494km, split across seven continuous stages and crosses no less than six European nations.
The sheer distance isn’t the only consideration either. The reason it’s likely to take up to six months to complete is that the trail features more than 78,400 metres of elevation gain – that’s the equivalent of summiting Mount Everest about nine times over.
As it takes so long, hikers need to pick their timeframe for the trek carefully as, between October and March, Alpine blizzards could be a serious stumbling block.
The epic trail begins with a 428 kilometre journey along Poland’s flat Baltic coast from Hel to Świnoujście.
Next up, as Poland becomes Germany, and then Switzerland, expect a 1,930km walk, taking in historical cities including Rostock, and continuing along craggy paths towards the Austrian border.
You’ll then hike a 392km stretch through the Allgäu Alps and into Innsbruck, Austria’s fifth-largest city.
Finally, hikers will walk along the iconic Via Alpina, through the Italian Dolomites and Triglav National Park in Slovenia. You’ll finish the journey in Trieste, where you can enjoy a refreshing – and celebratory – drink with views of the Adriatic Sea.
How practical is hiking the Wolf Trail?
While such an epic journey might seem near impossible, it’s actually more straightforward than you might think.
In terms of safety, the trail has been seriously vetted by Canadian long-distance backpacking pro Lauren Roerick, who confirmed the trail is safe and that hikers won’t get lost along the way.
The brains behind the trail are actually outdoor gear experts Jack Wolfskin, who have been encouraging walking fans to get involved with ‘thru-hiking’, which effectively means you walk a very long, continuous route and pick up necessities in each town you arrive in.
Europe has notoriously strict camping laws – and pitching tents in Germany in particular can see hikers hit with a steep fine.
To bypass this, and allow people to camp in the wild without fear of punishment, Jack Wolfskin actually connected the near-3,500km route, made up of historic pathways, into one exceptionally long trail.
As a result, the whole journey takes hikers through places to sleep including pre-approved trekking camps, mountain huts to lay heads, and local villages, with slightly more luxurious accommodation.
If you’re looking for a holiday that involves more than just a few hours by the pool, the Wolf Trail could very well be the ultimate European adventure.
