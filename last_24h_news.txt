Hezbollah supporters take to streets of Beirut to protest Israel deal

BEIRUT - Hezbollah supporters took to the streets of Beirut late on June 26 to protest an agreement between Israel and Lebanon, as a lawmaker from the Iran-backed group warned enforcing the deal risked sparking civil war.
The state-run National News Agency (NNA) reported that “Hezbollah supporters rode motorbikes through the streets of Beirut” including central areas and along a road leading to the airport “in protest at the framework agreement announced between Lebanon and Israel”.
An AFP correspondent saw people riding motorbikes and chanting slogans along one road, as well as Lebanese army temporary checkpoints set up along several streets of the capital.
Lebanese “authorities will be unable to impose the implementation of the agreement signed in Washington unless they go, with American support, to civil war”, said Hezbollah lawmaker Hassan Fadlallah, whose party has long rejected the direct Israel-Lebanon talks.
He added that “what happened in Washington is an attempt to disrupt the Islamabad path, and without the resistance (Hezbollah) nothing will pass”, referring to the initial agreement between the US and Iran on halting the Middle East war, which includes Lebanon.
Footage circulating on local outlets and social media showed hundreds of Hezbollah supporters on motorbikes and mopeds roaming Beirut’s southern suburbs, the group’s stronghold, before they headed to the heart of the capital.
Some had gathered near the seat of the government and blocked a main road nearby, while other footage showed soldiers chasing away Hezbollah supporters who blocked the main road leading to the airport with burning tyres, before the army reopened it.
The NNA had said protesters blocked at least one major road with burning tyres. AFP

Bible passages to be required reading in Texas public schools
Critics say such decisions are at odds with the Constitution’s ‘establishment clause’, long understood as separating church and state
The Texas Board of Education on Friday approved mandated reading lists for public school children that include passages from the Bible – the latest effort by leaders there to infuse the education system with conservative and religious ideals.
The Republican-dominated board, in a 9-5 vote with one member absent and not voting, approved the reading lists for over 5 million public school students beginning in 2030.
Texas had already mandated that the Bible’s Ten Commandments be displayed in all public schools, a decision that was upheld by a federal appeal court earlier this year, following on the heels of other Republican-led states seeking to infuse public education with Christian teachings.
Critics say these decisions are at odds with the Constitution’s “establishment clause”, long understood by courts as separating church and state.
Supporters say the measures restore basic Judeo-Christian teachings to school systems, which many have said are historically significant.

Trump claims high approval rating despite polls showing otherwise
- President Donald Trump claimed on Truth Social that his approval rating is as high as 65 percent, stating that "Honest Pollsters are very rare and hard to find."
- He shared a Newsmax article highlighting a McLaughlin & Associates survey that placed his approval at 50 percent among likely midterm voters.
- In contrast, recent credible polls from YouGov and Quinnipiac show Trump's approval rating significantly lower, hovering around 38-39 percent.
- These surveys also indicate high disapproval rates for Trump's overall job performance and his handling of key issues such as immigration, the economy, and the Iran war.
- Trump has a history of dismissing polls he deems unfavorable, previously labeling them "fake" and even filing a lawsuit against a pollster in 2024, which was later dropped.
