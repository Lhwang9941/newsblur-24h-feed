When is the 2026–27 federal budget? Here's what you need to know before tomorrow
Federal Treasurer Jim Chalmers will hand down the Albanese government's fifth budget tomorrow night.
And while this will inevitably involve a lot of political talk it will also, either directly or indirectly, affect your finances.
Here's what to expect and what it could mean for you.
What is the federal budget?
The federal budget is the government's plan for raising and spending money. It also provides a snapshot of the national economy.
While the comparison is often made to a household budget, it's a poor one: the purpose of a government budget is to help the entire economy, whereas a household budget is mainly concerned with its own financial situation.
Each year, the federal government looks at how much money it has coming in (revenue) and how much money it has going out (expenditure).
Sources of revenue include personal and business taxes, excises, levies, selling government assets and money generated from government-owned entities.
How revenue is raised and where it is spent can impact your financial situation in one way or another, so it's quite important.
Why does the budget matter?
The federal budget lays out where the federal governments plans to allocate spending over the next few years.
It can either be in surplus, where spending is less than revenue, or deficit, where spending is more than revenue.
This includes things such as our hospitals, schools, roads and the NDIS.
It shapes how our economy functions so its ripple effects have an impact on all of us, whether we're taxpayers or not.
A good way to get a quick glimpse of these impacts (spending priorities) is by reading what we call our winners and losers list, which sums up which groups will benefit the most from the new plan.
We'll publish this shortly after the budget is released.
What time is the federal budget speech?
Tomorrow night.
Mr Chalmers's speech will be broadcast live from Parliament House in Canberra at:
- 7:30pm AEST: ACT, New South Wales, Queensland, Victoria and Tasmania
- 7pm ACDT: Northern Territory and South Australia
- 4:30pm AWST: Western Australia
It typically lasts about half an hour.
Generally speaking, this speech talks up the positive points the government of the day wants to promote and glosses over the negatives.
That's why we have a team of journalists who will be combing through the nitty-gritty details of the budget papers to suss out the real-world impacts of these plans.
Where can I watch the speech?
It'll be broadcast on free-to-air television on ABC TV as well as on the ABC News Channel.
You can also stream it on ABC iview.
And you can watch the ABC News live stream via the ABC News website by clicking "watch live" at the top of the page.
We'll also be blogging the whole thing live on the ABC News website.
Where can I read the budget papers?
On the federal government's 2026–27 budget website.
There's only a countdown clock on there at the moment, but it should be updated as soon as Mr Chalmers tables the papers.
What's in this year's budget?
This year's budget is shaping up to be one of the biggest in decades.
Significant tax changes are coming for property and share investors, electric car drivers and the beneficiaries of more than 818,000 discretionary trusts.
Prime Minister Anthony Albanese has said the changes are needed to make Australia fairer.
"For many young people, they feel like they haven't got a fair crack compared with my generation and the generations beforehand," Mr Albanese said.
At its core, the budget will contain three central packages: tax reform, savings and productivity and investment.
Here are some items that have been made public prior to budget night.
Tax reform trio
Read moreThe budget will feature changes to the capital gains tax (CGT) discount, negative gearing and the taxation of trust funds.
The CGT discount is expected to be equal to the amount of inflation, but the government may also consider a flat discount such as 25, 30 or 35 per cent.
For negative gearing, Labor could opt to abolish it entirely or to cap the number of properties that can be negatively geared. Several sources told the ABC there would be changes to the way discretionary trusts were taxed, but the specific details remain uncertain.
EVs
Read moreThe government will begin winding back the tax discount for EVs bought under a novated lease, saying electric cars worth more than $75,000 will have to pay some fringe benefits tax from April next year, and all EVs will have to pay from 2029, though at a discounted rate.
Fuel security package
Read moreMore than $10 billion will be spent bolstering Australia's fuel security as the federal government attempts to shield the nation from the long-term consequences of the war in Iran.
Mr Albanese said $7.5 billion would go towards providing financial support for the supply of fuel and fertiliser, including loans, equity, guarantees and insurance, while $3.2 billion would fund an onshore fuel security reserve.
Tax deduction for workers
Read moreA one-off tax cut of up to $300 this financial year for workers has not been ruled out by Labor. Mr Albanese has refused to confirm whether the offset would be included in the budget.
NDIS cuts
Read moreHealth Minister Mark Butler announced $15 billion would be cut from the NDIS over the next four years to clamp down on ballooning costs and prevent it from becoming "an ATM for shonks, grifters, fraudsters and crooks".
Initial modelling shows the changes will reduce the number of people on the NDIS from 760,000 to 600,000 by the end of the decade.
Defence
Read moreAn extra $53 billion will be spent on defence over the next decade in order to lift military spending to 3 per cent of Australia's GDP.
The spending will be focused on infrastructure such as drones and long-range missiles.
Receipt-free tax deduction
Read moreAround 6.2 million Australians will be able to claim an instant $1,000 tax deduction without receipts.
It will come into effect for the 2027 tax season next year.
The minimum tax saving under the legislation will be $470, with the average saving being $205. The legislation will mean workers won't need to provide receipts for up to $1,000 in work-related expenses.
Private health insurance rebate
Read moreMore than 3 million Australians aged over 65 will have to pay, on average, between $226 and $255 more a year for their private health insurance. Mr Butler said the current policy was "not fair between generations" and not the best use of taxpayer money.
Countering terrorism
Read moreThe federal government will spend $74 million over the next two years to set up a national Counter Terrorism Online Centre in an effort to curb youth radicalisation.

An east London man was charged on Sunday for an assault against three Jewish residents in Enfield on Saturday, the Metropolitan Police said.
Hornchurch resident Dylan Ossei was charged with assault, religiously aggravated assault, threatening behavior to cause fear, religiously aggravated harassment, and threatening behavior to cause harassment after he had allegedly harassed, threatened, and assaulted the Jewish community members.
The thirty-four-year-old man was arrested at the scene, and the victims, a fifty-three-year-old woman, a 58-year-old man, and a 26-year-old man, sustained injuries that did not require further medical treatment.
Enfield and Haringey policing head Detective Superintendent Marco Bardetti said in a press statement that the Met would work closely with the Jewish community to ensure that they felt safe going about their lives.
“The Met has made it abundantly clear that we treat reports of alleged antisemitic hate crime with the utmost seriousness and diligence, as demonstrated by the swift arrest and charge following this incident," said Bardetti.
Met dedicates 100 extra officers to protect London Jews
On Tuesday, the Met announced it was setting up a Community Protection Team of 100 officers to ensure the protection of the capital's Jewish residents amid a rash of antisemitic incidents.
Over the weekend, the Met made a series of arrests, and the Crown Prosecution Service leveled a series of charges against alleged antisemitic criminals.

Federal government has missed one idea to get housing back on track: build more homes
If it's serious about improving intergenerational fairness and housing affordability in this week's budget, perhaps the government should just build some houses.
That's about as likely as my last bright idea of nationalising child care, but back when housing was affordable, public housing was an important part of the mix.
Whitlam tried to restart it after Menzies slashed it, but the states resisted and since then federal governments have exited public housing.
The Housing Australia Future Fund support for community housing is good policy, but too limited.
Over the weekend the government pre-announced a new housing supply measure to be included in the budget: $2 billion paid to states and local councils to unlock infrastructure for another 65,000 homes … over 10 years, so 6,500 a year.
It's the latest in a mostly worthwhile chipping away at restrictions to housing supply over the past four years that the government says add up to $47 billion, except that includes buyer assistance schemes that increase demand instead of supply.
But it might be time to do more than dish out cash and targets to state governments and local councils, as part of the National Housing Accord.
Housing starts behind
It's true, as the weekend press release said, that housing commencements in the December quarter were 26.1 per cent above the year before, at 53,567, but that just gets quarterly starts back to where they were before the housing accord was signed, when everyone agreed that housing starts were not enough.
The accord target was 1.2 million homes over five years from July 1, 2024; to be on pace to meet that target, 420,000 should have been built by the end of March.
Completions up to December 31, 2025 (the latest for which there's data), totalled 262,592 and approvals for the next three months were 51,406, so the total after 21 months is 313,593.
To hit the five-year target, the quarterly run-rate now has to rise almost 50 per cent, and the Iran war just made it harder by increasing the price of building materials and diesel.
Commonwealth Bank's economists estimate that building input costs have increased about 4 per cent on average as a result of the closure of the Strait of Hormuz, making it more difficult for developers to get projects to stack up.
As a result, they predict 15,000 fewer dwellings will be built by mid-2029.
So, if the budget is to have credibility as the solution to intergenerational unfairness, Jim Chalmers might have to have more up his housing supply sleeve than $2 billion for infrastructure.
It will certainly need to involve more than tweaking the capital gains tax discount and negative gearing.
There either needs to be a dramatic increase in housing supply, or an equally dramatic cut to immigration, which would cause more problems than it would solve.
Can slowing immigration help?
The Canadian government claims to have cut migration to that country by 90 per cent from the historic peak in 2023, although it seems to have been almost entirely temporary, not permanent.
Canadian house prices did fall about 18 per cent, but many things contributed to that, including the official interest rate going up from 0.25 to 5 per cent, and now there are labour shortages across the economy.
In Australia, net overseas migration is already falling. In 2024-25 it was 306,000, 129,000 less than the previous year but Treasury's forecast in the last budget of 260,000 for this year and 225,000 next year look out of reach.
In the September quarter, the latest data we've got, it was 87,800, 7.9 per cent higher than the previous year, and an annual rate of 348,000, so this year is off to a bad start.
Anyway, the Labor government wants to focus on supply and deal with demand only via tax changes, leaving the Coalition and One Nation to compete for the title of Most Against Migration, which is probably a reasonable move politically because, well, there are a lot of migrant voters these days.
Beware Schrödinger's numbers
How much money might be available if the government decided to build houses?
Savings from the NDIS are projected to cut $15 billion a year from spending by reducing the number of participants by 160,000.
There will probably be additional savings measures announced on Tuesday night, but two days before Health Minister Mark Butler unveiled the NDIS reforms on April 22, Treasurer Jim Chalmers told a press conference: "It is easily the most important part of the savings package that we will present on budget night."
Will Butler's team be able to get 160,000 people off the NDIS so those savings actually happen?
It doesn't matter. They'll be in the forward estimates and these are Schrödinger's numbers: alive and dead at the same time.
They are printed in the budget so they must be true, but at the same time they are never true because they are "spreadsheet guesses", which is to say they come out of a model in which guesses and errors procreate like rabbits.
For example, the forward estimate modelling in the 2021-22 budget produced a 2023-24 balance, two years hence, of a deficit of $79.5 billion; it turned out to be a surplus of $15.8 billion. No-one remembered the 2021-22 budget or cared.
LoadingTradie shortage another factor
How many houses could the government build with those savings?
Well, houses and apartments cost between $350,000 and $400,000 each to build.
So let's go with $375,000. The $15 billion from the NDIS would be enough for about an extra 35,000 houses a year plus sewerage and roads, which would not cover the shortfall versus the accord target, but if Treasury is right that net overseas migration will fall to 260,000 next year and 235,000 the year after, it would be better than 6,500 per year and might be enough to start bringing house prices down, or at least stop them rising.
There's more to it than that: there aren't enough tradies, productivity is low and zoning and NIMBYism are a challenge.
Those things have to be dealt with anyway, and at least if the government was doing the building the question of whether a developer is going to make a profit is removed, along with the complications of developer contributions for infrastructure, and maybe even local council zoning problems.
Like the NDIS (and child care) housing affordability is unsustainable and can't be left to be a by-product of monetary policy, the productivity and profitability of the building industry and the zoning vagaries of local councils.
And as with the NDIS (and child care), intergenerational fairness requires a radical solution: the government could do worse than going back to building some houses.
Alan Kohler is finance presenter and columnist on ABC News and he also writes for Intelligent Investor.
